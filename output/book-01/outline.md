## Solution: the-central-mystery
- culprit: Mary Burrell (Tom's paternal aunt; his father's sister, daughter of the dying man)
- victim: Dr Neil Hartigan, the town GP
- central deception / motive: The aunt has believed for a decade that her father was murdered; a recent discovery finally named Neil as the hand behind his death. She kills him believing she is avenging a crime — but Neil eased her father's passing at the old man's own repeated, desperate plea. She is right that his death was no accident, and catastrophically wrong about what it meant. Neil's compassion is the thing that kills him; his honesty in letting the truth surface is the trigger.
- suspects: Mary Burrell, Saffron, Beryl Foss, Faye, Vincent Calloway, Cobber (witness)
- key locations: Neil's cottage kitchen (the erasure scene), The Wheelhouse, the community hall, the beach at dawn, the old cemetery

## Threads
- A-murder — a beloved GP is killed for a mercy mistaken as murder; the truth surfaces through Meg's gift and detonates the family she is falling for
- B-romance — a slow-burn between Meg and Tom, carpenter, complicated when the killer is revealed to be his aunt; wounded but bonded by the end
- C-internal — a woman who stopped seeing her own life honestly learns to see again; recovering from a stale marriage, reclaiming her art, finding a place
- gift — the Migraine Sight moves from binge-and-hangover party trick, through recording-without-understanding, to the click that solves the case; the origin mystery is teased but not resolved
- victim-reveal — Neil is mourned as a saint, exposed as a sinner when his old mercy surfaces, then understood as a person; the arc completes in Act III
- comedy — the op-shop sisters' contradictory testimony, Faye's jam feud, Glaze the studio cat, and Meg's mortifying faux-pas blurts keep the tone warm throughout
- series-seeds — Artie Selwood hints he has seen Meg's kind of sight before; Calloway's buried bench skeleton is raised but left unresolved; Saffron's humiliation opens a possible future friendship

## Chapter 01
Meg Quill pulls into Pelican's Crook on an overcast autumn afternoon, her hatchback loaded with clay tools, a box of half-glazed seconds, and very little else she wants to keep from her old life. She opens The Wheelhouse for the first time — bare shelves, salt smell, light off the water — and feels the specific relief of a woman who has stopped pretending. Tom the carpenter arrives to fit the shelving, says about four words, measures everything twice, and leaves sawdust on the floor. Glaze, a ginger tom of uncertain allegiance, walks in through the open door, inspects the kiln, and sits on Meg's good apron. She does not throw him out.

## Chapter 02
Faye from the bakery appears at nine the next morning with a loaf of sourdough and the complete social history of Pelican's Crook delivered in the time it takes to eat it. Meg learns who owns what, who resents whom, and that the op-shop sisters Dot and Glad have not agreed on a single fact since 1987. She walks the main street, nods at Cobber on the beach at dawn — he is still there at noon — and is assessed from behind a display of donated crockery by two pairs of identical, unblinking eyes. Beryl Foss, chair of the CWA and unofficial mayor of everything, stops Meg outside the post office, welcomes her to town in a tone that is also a warning, and moves on.

## Chapter 03
Meg has a migraine — the real kind, the kind she has had since she was nineteen. She lies in the back room of the studio with a tea towel over her eyes and the world pouring in whether she wants it or not: the exact angle of light under the door, the sound of Glaze's claws on the concrete in a rhythm she can already read, the three distinct layers of paint on the skirting board. Dr Neil Hartigan comes in response to a call she barely remembers making. He sits on the edge of the bed, asks questions nobody has ever asked her before, and does not once use the word "stress." He says he would like to look into it further. She leaves the appointment feeling, for the first time, that the migraines might be something other than a sentence.

## Chapter 04
A commission arrives — a set of commemorative bowls for the surf club's centenary — and Meg has a reason to be in the world. She fires a test batch, ruins two, gets the third right, and understands that this is what she came here for. Tom returns to hang a shelf she forgot to ask for; they have a disagreement about its height that is also, possibly, something else. Faye's jam feud flares in the bakery — the agricultural show judge has been sighted — and Saffron from the wellness retreat sweeps through to leave a flyer that nobody takes. Neil's name comes up twice in conversation: once as the person who fixed Cobber's shoulder, once as the reason old Mrs Papadimitriou is still walking. He is the kind of man a town builds itself around without noticing.

## Chapter 05
Beryl's autumn community market fills the hall and the foreshore. Every future suspect is on the page at once, behaving normally: Saffron selling turmeric lattes from a stand the locals ignore, Dot and Glad running the op-shop table with their customary surveillance efficiency, Calloway the retired magistrate accepting a scone from someone who clearly owes him something, Cobber explaining the nature of time to a tourist. Meg, lonely and wanting to belong, lets the perception run — she notices things aloud, catches connections, says the sharp perceptive thing, and watches people lean toward her. She shines. It feels wonderful. She notices, without understanding why she notices, that Tom's aunt refolds a tea towel at the kitchen servery with the brisk, proprietary confidence of a woman who knows exactly where things live.

## Chapter 06
Meg spends the next day in a darkened room, the price of the night before. The migraine is longer and worse than usual, the pleasure curdled into shame and a head that feels like it has been packed with wet sand. She misses a call from Neil. She does not find out until the following morning, when Faye arrives white-faced instead of sourdough-bearing, that Neil Hartigan was found dead in his cottage the previous evening, and that the town is standing in the street not knowing what to do with its hands.

## Chapter 07
The community gathers and comes apart at the same time. Sergeant Dave Pruitt arrives from the next town over, sets up in the surf club, and asks everyone to stay calm in the tone of a man who expects the opposite. Meg, drawn to Neil's cottage with no clear reason she can name, is struck by a migraine on the path outside. In the flood she perceives, through the open kitchen window, the wrongness of a room that has been smoothed: one chair squared a fraction too neatly, a faint ring on the wood where a second cup stood, a tea towel damp and refolded, the biscuit tin lid fractionally misaligned. None of it means anything. She says aloud, to no one in particular, that someone cleaned up — and Pruitt, hearing it, glances at Tom's aunt who is standing nearby in obvious distress about her father's doctor and her father's death and her whole complicated grief, and the town collectively decides she is harmless. Meg, moved by Tom's quiet steadiness at the edge of the crowd, gently agrees.

## Chapter 08
The studio becomes an accidental incident room. People come in to buy things they don't need and stay to talk. Meg discovers she is the person the town is talking to — perhaps because she is new enough to be neutral, perhaps because she has Neil's ear and they want some of it. She cannot let it go. Neil knew everyone's business the way she once knew everyone's business in twenty years of HR: the quiet firings, the crises behind the leave requests, the things people would do anything to keep off the record. She understands, in her bones, what it means to be the person who holds that and to become a liability.

## Chapter 09
Saffron rises as the town's preferred suspect: she had loud motive (Neil had been steering patients away from the retreat), no alibi she will share, and the kind of surface that invites projection. Sergeant Pruitt finds her professionally interesting. Meg finds her professionally familiar — a woman performing a version of herself for survival — and is less convinced, though she cannot say why. Saffron denies everything with the energy of someone telling the truth about one thing and a lie about another.

## Chapter 10
Beryl becomes briefly plausible. It emerges she was overheard arguing with Neil the week before his death, in the car park behind the bakery, in a voice that carried. When Meg carefully doesn't ask Faye about it, Faye tells her anyway. The argument was heated. Beryl, when Pruitt raises it, goes very still in the way of someone deciding how much the truth will cost her. She does not yet say what the argument was about.

## Chapter 11
Dot and Glad are called in, separately, to give their account of who was where on the evening in question. Their testimonies agree on the large facts and diverge on every detail: the car was dark blue (Dot) or dark green (Glad), it arrived at half past seven (Dot) or twenty to eight (Glad), the driver was alone (both, firmly). The single real buried fact that emerges — a car, unusual for that street at that hour, parked briefly outside Neil's — is noted. Neither sister knows whose it was. Meg files it without knowing what to do with it.

## Chapter 12
Tom comes to hang a second shelf Meg actually asked for this time. They work around each other in the studio for an afternoon without quite arguing. He mentions his aunt in passing — she has been strange lately, stranger than her usual grief, and he is worried about her in the way of a person who has learned to worry quietly. Meg notices that when Tom is concerned he goes very still and measures things twice; it is the same thing he does with shelves. She likes him more than she intended to. The case is drifting closer to his family without either of them knowing it yet.

## Chapter 13
Faye becomes, briefly and horribly, a suspect. Neil knew something about Faye's past — the jam feud hides an older wound, a history between Faye and the competition judge that predates the jam by twenty years. Meg learns this accidentally, follows the thread because she cannot help herself, and has a very uncomfortable twenty minutes in the bakery in which she is sitting opposite her only friend in this town and asking questions whose subtext is obvious. Faye answers them. Her secret is sad and old and not a murder. They drink tea in silence for a while afterward and neither of them mentions it again.

## Chapter 14
Beryl is cleared, at volume. Her secret: Neil had given her a diagnosis she had told nobody, and the argument in the car park was her demanding he keep it out of her file and his refusing, gently, because he could not in good conscience. The iron matriarch is mortal and does not want the town to know it. She becomes, overnight, something closer to a person and something closer to an ally. Faye is cleared quietly and the friendship survives. Two down; the field narrows and Saffron is looking more and more like the answer.

## Chapter 15
Meg finds, through Neil's former receptionist and a stack of old appointment cards, that Neil had been reviewing old records in the months before his death — specifically a cluster of records from a decade ago. A prescription, a certified cause of death, something he had signed and kept. The town's grief curdles: there are whispers that perhaps Neil was not entirely what they thought, that there might be things in those records that certain people would have reason to want buried. Meg, who had trusted him most of anyone here, sits with the discomfort of not yet knowing whether she was right to.

## Chapter 16
A second migraine takes her out of commission for a day and a half. Before it does, Artie Selwood corners her at the lighthouse — he has been watching her in the way of a man who has been watching for something specific — and says, very carefully, that he once knew a woman who saw the way Meg sees. He does not say more. He runs out of steam, or nerve, and changes the subject to the 1878 wreck of the Loch Ard, which he narrates in exhausting detail. But the thing he said first stays with her through the migraine: someone else, before her, saw this way. The gift has a history.

## Chapter 17
Pruitt builds a case against Saffron. The circumstantial fits: motive, no solid alibi, a history of small deceptions about her credentials and her past that make the big deception feel possible. Meg, reviewing what she has, finds she can make the same architecture point at Saffron and it holds up. She tells herself this is because the evidence points there. She does not examine too closely why it is a relief to believe it.

## Chapter 18
The case against Saffron collapses in the surf club on a Tuesday afternoon, in front of Pruitt, Meg, Dot, Glad, and three members of the CWA who were there for an unrelated meeting. Saffron's alibi, which she has been protecting with the energy of someone protecting a secret worse than murder, turns out to be a meeting with a debt recovery agent in Colac at the time in question. She was not in Pelican's Crook. She was not near Neil's cottage. She was broke and ashamed of it and that is all. The case is gone. Meg is left in the surf club car park with nothing, and the sense that she helped put the wrong person in the frame. Glaze is waiting on the bonnet of her car. She sits on the kerb for a while.

## Chapter 19
Three days later, at a CWA hall clean-up Beryl has conscripted half the town into, Meg is drying cups after morning tea and watching Tom's aunt put things away. The aunt moves through the kitchen with the brisk, proprietary confidence of a woman who knows exactly where everything lives — reaching for the shelf without looking, returning a cup to a specific spot with an unthinking, physical certainty that belongs to a person who has done it a thousand times. The room tilts. Meg puts down her own cup. She understands, all at once, what she saw through the window at Neil's cottage. Not two cups left out. The absence of the second cup. The signature of someone who knew that kitchen as well as their own, erasing herself.

## Chapter 20
Meg sits with it for a long time before she does anything. She goes over everything she knows about Tom's aunt: the decade-long certainty that her father was murdered, always dismissed by the town as a fixed grief; the recent change in her manner that Tom noticed and worried about quietly; the car outside Neil's — dark, at the wrong hour — that Dot and Glad each described in their contradictory ways and that fits, when she adds Cobber's dawn sighting from the beach, into a timeline that places someone at the cottage that evening. The aunt was there. She welcomed herself in the way of someone who belonged in that kitchen, and then she erased the visit, and then Neil was dead.

## Chapter 21
Meg finds the record Neil had been reviewing. It is a death certificate, a decade old, for Tom's grandfather — the aunt's father. The cause of death is documented as natural. But Neil's private notes, which his receptionist had been preserving without quite knowing why, describe the weeks before: the old man's pain, his repeated requests, his family's exhaustion and grief. Neil's last entry is four words and then nothing. Meg reads it twice. The mercy could not be plainer. The aunt found the notes, or found the discrepancy, or Neil told her himself — trying, in his late-life honesty, to set the record straight — and she heard only that her suspicion had been right all along, that it was no natural death, and she filled the rest in wrong.

## Chapter 22
Meg needs it to be provable. Pruitt's scepticism has been the right friction all book; she cannot walk in and say she knows because of a cup. She maps what she has: Cobber's sighting (a car, a figure, an approximate time), Dot and Glad's car (combined into one vehicle, colour confirmed when she asks them separately about the aunt's car and both say the same thing without prompting), the death certificate and the private notes, the kitchen that was known too well and cleaned too carefully. She goes to Pruitt. He listens with the expression of a man who wants very badly to say no and is not quite able to.

## Chapter 23
She tells Tom before Pruitt moves. She owes him that. It is the worst conversation in the book: she tells him what she found and what it means, and watches him go through the phases — disbelief, then the sick recognition of a person who remembers small things that now mean something different, then a stillness she recognises as the way he handles things that are too big. He does not ask her not to do it. He thanks her in two words and walks back down to his workshop. She stands on the street outside and has the prolonged unpleasant experience of being right.

## Chapter 24
Meg goes to the aunt's house with Pruitt. The aunt sees them coming from the window and opens the door before they knock, which tells Meg that she has been waiting for this — that somewhere beneath the righteous certainty there was always a knowledge of what she had done. The confrontation is not dramatic. The aunt is not monstrous. She is a woman in her sixties who loved her father, spent ten years believing he was taken from her, and finally had a name to hold responsible. She was wrong about what the name meant. Pruitt makes the arrest with a quietness that does him credit. On the way out he says nothing to Meg, but he holds the gate.

## Chapter 25
The town absorbs it in waves. The first wave is disbelief; the second is the particular grief of realising the killer was the person they'd already written off as harmless. Pruitt files his report. The aunt is taken to Geelong; Tom drives down that evening and does not come back for two days. Saffron, vindicated and still broke, accepts a cup of tea from Meg in the studio without either of them making it significant, which is the beginning of something.

## Chapter 26
Neil is mourned again, differently. The town holds an informal gathering at the pub — Beryl organises it, which is how you know it is official — and people say things about him that are more complicated than the things they said the first time. A saint is too simple; a man who did something unambiguously compassionate and was killed for it by someone who loved him wrong is closer to the truth. Faye cries. Cobber says something that sounds like nonsense and turns out not to be. Artie Selwood sits in the corner and watches Meg across the room with the expression of a man with more to say when the time is right.

## Chapter 27
Tom comes back. He and Meg talk for a long time at the table in the studio, at the end of the day, with Glaze occupying the neutral territory between them. The wound is real — she followed her gift into his family and it cost him something permanent. But he is the kind of man who looks at the thing clearly rather than looking away from it, and they are, by the end of the evening, still themselves. It is not resolved; it is not meant to be. It is a slow burn and they have just decided to stay warm.

## Chapter 28
Late November, and the tourists are starting to arrive. Meg opens the kiln on a new batch — the commemorative bowls, finally finished — and they are good; she knows they are good without anyone telling her. Glaze rearranges himself on the good apron. Artie comes in on the pretence of commissioning a mug and stays for forty minutes, circling something he is not quite saying about the woman he knew who saw the way Meg sees, and about the lighthouse, and about a record he has kept for thirty years. He leaves without saying it. The Great Ocean Road hums with summer traffic outside. Meg sits at the wheel and starts a new piece, and for the first time since she arrived she is not waiting to see if she belongs here. She already knows.
