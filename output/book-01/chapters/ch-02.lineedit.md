# Chapter Two

The knock came at nine, three soft raps and then a fourth, as though the fourth were a correction.

Maggie had been up since six and dressed since seven and had spent the intervening time arranging her tools on the shelves Cal had hung the day before, then rearranging them, then putting them back the way they had been, which was the way she did most things when she was nervous and did not want to admit she was waiting for someone to look at her life and judge it. She had decided overnight that the goal for today was modest. She would meet the town without seeming to need the town. She would be friendly and unhurried and slightly hard to read, the way she had been good at being for twenty years, and nobody would guess that she had lain awake at two listening to a possum cross the roof and wondering what she had done.

She opened the door to a woman holding a loaf of bread the way other people held babies.

"Faye Denton," the woman said. "I run the bakery. This is sourdough and you'll want it today, not tomorrow — it's a good loaf but it's not a patient one." She looked Maggie over with frank, unembarrassed interest, the loaf still cradled against the front of an apron dusted to the elbow with flour. "You're the potter."

"Maggie Quill."

"I know." Faye said it kindly, the way a person tells you the weather. "Are you going to let me in, or are we doing this on the step? Because I've left Tania minding the ovens and Tania panics."

Maggie let her in.

By the time the kettle had boiled, Faye had established herself at the workbench as though she had always sat there, broken the loaf with her hands rather than waiting for a knife, and begun, without apparent effort, to hand Maggie the town.

It came in a particular order, and Maggie understood after a while that the order was not random. Faye was deciding what Maggie needed first. The op-shop on the main street was run by two sisters, Dot and Glad, who between them saw everything that moved on the street and agreed on none of it. "If you ever need to know what happened, ask them both and split the difference," Faye said. "If you want a quiet life, don't ask them anything." There was Cobber, who lived more or less on the beach and had been about to leave town since before Faye could remember. There was Beryl Foss, who ran the Country Women's Association and, by extension, the moral weather. "She'll find you. Don't go looking — let her do it her way. And be straight with her. She can smell a person managing her from across a hall."

Maggie, who had spent two decades managing people across halls, said nothing and ate her bread.

There was Saffron, who had the wellness retreat up past the headland and sold something Faye declined to name with a small movement of her mouth that did most of the naming. "She's not from here. Neither are you, so I won't say more than that, except that the town decided about her before she'd unpacked, and that's not always fair and isn't always wrong." There was Mara, who did the dune work along the foreshore — "Country first, everything else second, and she's right, so don't argue with her about it." There was Mary Burrell, who Maggie would like, Faye said, in a softer register, a woman who had brought up half the town's strays, human and otherwise, and who would probably turn up before the week was out with something from her garden.

"Burrell," Maggie said. "Like Cal."

"His aunt." Faye's mouth did something amused. "You've met Cal, then."

"He hung my shelves."

"Of course he did." Whatever the tone meant, it did not explain itself, and Maggie had learned long ago that the fastest way to lose information was to chase it. She let it sit. Faye refilled both mugs and moved on.

And there was Iris Poole, who did the jam and the preserves at the markets, and who Maggie should — here Faye paused.

It was a small pause. If Maggie had not spent her working life reading the half-second between a question and its answer, she would have missed it. Faye's face did not fall, exactly. It closed. It went smooth and pleasant and shut, the warmth still sitting on the surface like oil on water and nothing underneath it, and her voice when it came again was perfectly even.

"Iris does very good jam," Faye said. "Everyone says so."

Then she reached for another piece of bread and the subject was gone, sealed over, as though it had never been opened. Maggie watched the seam close and decided not to put a finger in it. She was a guest in someone's town and someone's kitchen and someone's morning, and she had been given a loaf of bread, and there are things you do not ask the person who has just handed you bread. She filed Iris Poole away with the possum and the two-o'clock dark — a thing she had noticed and would keep.

"I'll tell you what," Faye said, when the loaf was a wreck of crust between them and the second pot had gone cool. "People will ask me about you. They already are. And I'll send them your way, the ones who'd actually buy a nice bowl instead of telling you it's dear." She tipped her head at the shelves, at the careful, defensive little display of seconds Maggie had put out the day before. "But not yet. Learn the place first. Don't come at it with a story about yourself ready-made, the sea-change and the new beginning and all of that. They've seen the story. Let them find out who you are slowly, and they'll mind the shop for you when you're crook. Come at them with the brochure and they'll be polite and buy nothing." She stood, brushing flour from the apron onto Maggie's clean floor without noticing, and was somehow already at the door. "Six o'clock the ovens go on. Bread's gone by half eight. If you're serious about it, you're there by eight."

Then she was gone, down the stairs and out, and the flat was quiet, and Maggie stood holding a mug she did not want and feeling, against all her intentions, enormously and embarrassingly grateful.

---

She went out at eleven, because the alternative was to stand in the studio being grateful, and walked the long way, down to where the main street gave up and the foreshore began.

The wind came at her sideways the moment she cleared the last building, a cold clean shove off the Southern Ocean that smelled of salt and kelp and, faintly, of something green and crushed underfoot. The tide was out. The bay lay flat and pewter under low cloud, and the headland curled around the far side of it the way the town's name promised, a long arm of tea-tree and exposed rock holding the water still. A magpie carolled from a banksia; another answered from somewhere she couldn't see.

Cobber was where the beach said he would be, sitting on the same grey log as if he had grown out of it, looking at the water. She lifted a hand. He lifted one back, unhurried, and went on looking at the water, and somehow the whole exchange was complete; there was nothing else it needed.

Further along, where the dunes had been fenced off with low posts and orange mesh, a woman in waders and a high-vis vest crouched over a tray of seedlings, talking to a man in a council-grey polo shirt who was not crouched over anything. The woman — Mara, it had to be Mara — was speaking quietly and steadily, the way a person speaks when she has had the same conversation many times and intends to keep having it for as long as it takes. The wind brought Maggie pieces of it.

"— not a vacant block, it's a dune system, and it's holding the road up —"

The man said something Maggie didn't catch, and then a fragment she did: a name she didn't know, and the words *expressed interest in the headland*, said in the smooth way that meant money was somewhere behind them.

"They can express what they like," Mara said, without heat, pressing a seedling into the sand. "It's been here longer than the road and it'll be here after."

Maggie kept walking. It was not her conversation; it was the kind of thing a town argued about endlessly and settled never, the local weather of land and money, and she had enough sense to know that a woman three days in the place had no part in it. She registered it the way she registered the cloud and the magpie, a fact of the landscape, and walked on toward the point with the wind pushing her along.

She was still cold when she got back.

---

Mary Burrell came at four, as Faye had said she would, and brought the garden with her.

The knock was gentler than Faye's, and Maggie opened the studio door to a woman in her sixties holding a terracotta pot in both hands. She was neat in the way of women who have always been neat, grey hair pinned back, a cardigan the colour of wet sand, and in the pot was a young lemon tree perhaps two feet high, its leaves dark and glossy, a slip of something older grafted at the base.

"Mary Burrell," she said. "I won't keep you. I've brought you a tree."

"Maggie. Come in — please."

Mary came in carefully, the way a person enters a room they intend to remember, and set the pot on the workbench with both hands, turning it a quarter so the best of it faced the light. From a basket over her arm she produced a paper bag of lemons, six of them, the deep uneven yellow of fruit that has actually grown on a tree and not in a box, and tipped them into the chipped blue bowl Maggie had left out for keys.

"They're from the same tree," Mary said. "The cutting, I mean. It was my father's. He planted the parent the year I was born and it's still going, and I take cuttings off it for people who come to stay." She touched one dark leaf with a fingertip, briefly, and took the finger back. "It likes the wind off the water, which most things don't. It'll tell you when you've got it right."

Maggie looked at the small tree and felt something move in her chest that she had not planned on and could not have managed if she'd seen it coming. "That's an extraordinary thing to give someone you've just met."

"I give it to people I think might stay." Mary said it lightly. Her eyes, though, were not light; they were doing something careful and steady, the way Cal's had done over the level, and Maggie recognised the family resemblance before she had decided whether to.

"I'm told your nephew hung my shelves."

The carefulness sharpened into attention. It was not unfriendly. It was the opposite of unfriendly; it was the look of a woman who loved someone and had just heard his name in a new mouth and was working out what the mouth intended by it.

"Cal does good work," Mary said. "He measures everything twice. His father was the same." A small pause, filled with the magpie outside and the sea under it. "He's had a lot of people pass through. People come down here to fix their lives and then they go back up to the city to live them, and Cal's the one who builds the shelves they leave behind." She smoothed the front of her cardigan, a neat reflexive gesture. "I'm not saying that's you. I'm only saying he's a steady sort of person, and steady people remember."

Maggie understood that she had been told something, and that it had been told gently, and that the gentleness was the point. "I'll take good care of the tree," she said, which was the only true thing she had.

"I know you will." And Mary smiled, and it reached all the way up, warm as the bread had been warm, and for a moment there was nothing in the room but kindness. She gathered her empty basket and moved toward the door, and then, with her hand already on the frame, she turned back the way Beryl Foss was apparently also in the habit of turning back, the way people in this town said the thing they had come to say only on their way out.

"How long are you thinking you'll stay?" Mary asked pleasantly, looking at the little tree and not at Maggie. "With the studio, I mean."

The lemons sat in their chipped blue bowl, six of them, golden in the failing light, and Maggie had no answer ready.
