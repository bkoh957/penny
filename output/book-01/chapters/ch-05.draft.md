# Chapter 05 — The Autumn Market

The hall had been a different building at seven that morning, when Maggie carried her crate of pottery in through the side door and found Beryl Foss standing in the middle of the floor with a clipboard, directing trestle tables into place by pointing at them. By nine it was a market. The transformation was total and somehow invisible, the way a tide comes in: you looked away to set out your bowls and looked back and the room was full of people and smell and noise, bunting strung crooked across the rafters, a tea urn breathing steam in the corner, and the doors at the far end thrown open to the foreshore where more stalls ran down toward the grey shine of the water.

She had a table near the middle, which Beryl had assigned her with the brisk neutrality of a woman placing a stranger neither in the sun nor in disgrace. Maggie had spent twenty minutes arranging her work and then twenty more rearranging it, moving the safe pieces forward — the speckled mugs, the small dishes in the blue she knew people liked — and pushing the one ambitious bowl, the wide one with the running glaze she was proud of, toward the back where it would not have to be explained. She had not eaten breakfast. She had meant to, and then there had been the crate, and the side door, and Beryl, and now there was coffee instead, her third cup, in a paper thing that scalded her fingers, and the morning had a bright over-tuned quality she recognised and chose not to think about.

What she wanted was simple and she was almost ashamed of how badly she wanted it: for the town to buy something. Not the work, particularly. Her. For one of these people to stop and pick up a mug and decide that the woman at The Wheelhouse was someone you bought a mug from. A small, ordinary belonging. She would have traded the whole table for it.

So she watched. It was the thing she had always been good at, the thing twenty years of sitting across desks from people who were lying to her had trained into the grain, and a market was the easiest room she had ever read. The geography did the work for her.

Faye's bakery stall was the gravitational centre of the hall, a long table of sourdough and lamingtons and a queue that never quite dissolved, and the queue told you who was sociable and who was only here for the bread. Beryl held the door because Beryl held everything. Dot and Glad had the table by the urn, where the raffle book was, and they were already at war about it.

"You can't put the hamper as first prize, Dorothy, nobody buys tickets for a hamper, it's a hamper, it's groceries —"

"It's a *gourmet selection*, Gladys, it says gourmet on the —"

"Last year the voucher went in twenty minutes."

"Last year you sold the voucher to your own grandson."

Maggie laughed before she could stop herself, and Glad turned two identical unblinking eyes on her, and Maggie discovered that the laugh had been the right currency. "She did," Glad said, with enormous satisfaction. "Twice."

It went from there. Saffron drifted past in something pale and expensive with a stack of retreat flyers she was placing on tables that had not asked for them, and the small contractions of the people she passed — the half-turn away, the studied attention to a jar of honey — drew themselves on the air for Maggie as clearly as ink. A heavy old man with a magistrate's stillness stood at the foreshore doors looking up the hill toward the old cemetery, not buying anything, and two women near him lowered their voices as he passed and did not raise them again until he had gone, and Maggie filed that without knowing what it was: Calloway, someone supplied; *he keeps to himself, that one, always has, since before*. The *before* hung there, unfinished, and nobody finished it.

She started saying things out loud. Small things, at first, to the women who had stopped at her table and stayed because she was more interesting than the mugs. That Beryl had put the new craft stalls along the cold wall and the old families along the warm one and called it chance. That the foreshore stalls did better business because people bought more when they could see the sea. The women laughed, and looked where she was looking, and saw it, and that was the thing — that was the drug of it — the moment their faces changed because she had handed them a way of seeing their own town that they had stopped using years ago. A small crowd formed at her table that was not there for pottery. She sold two mugs almost by accident. She was, for the better part of an hour, dazzling, and she felt the light in the hall go sharp at the edges, faintly metallic, a high ring under the noise, and she drank the rest of the coffee and kept talking.

It was Cal who broke it, the first time. He came in from the foreshore with a length of timber on his shoulder for some repair Beryl had commandeered him into, and he stopped at her table and looked at her the way he looked at a shelf that was not level.

"You eaten?" he said.

"I've had coffee."

"That's not eating." He nodded at the paper cup, the third, crushed now in her hand. "Faye's got pies. Go and have a pie."

She heard it as the thing it was not. She heard a man telling a grown woman she was doing it wrong, in front of the small audience she had assembled, and something in her went tight and bright. "I'm fine," she said, in the voice she used to use on people who wanted to manage her. "I'm having a good morning, actually."

Cal looked at her a moment longer, then at the crowd, then he shifted the timber on his shoulder and went, and she told herself she had won something.

The far end of the hall she had been saving, because it was the most interesting thing in the room and she had known it for an hour. Down there, as distant from Faye's bakery table as the building physically allowed, almost in the doorway to the foreshore, was a stall of jam and preserves, lined up in rows so straight they looked measured: dark plum, amber quince, marmalade graded by colour like a paint chart. The woman behind it was upright and composed and labelled, and she did not once look the length of the hall toward the bakery, and the bakery did not once look at her. A clean diagonal of avoidance ran between the two tables and the whole town walked around it without seeming to know it was there. Maggie had asked, earlier, who that was. *That's Iris*, a woman had said, and changed the subject so smoothly that the subject seemed never to have existed.

It was a wound. Maggie could see it the way you can see where a branch has been taken off a tree, by the shape of what grew around the absence. The distance between the two tables was not an accident of the market plan; it was the most deliberate thing in the building. And the longer she looked at Iris Poole standing very straight behind her exact rows of jam, the more clearly she could read that the straightness was not pride. It was the posture of a person holding something that had cost her, holding it in public, year after year, in a room that had decided to call it a feud about jam because the truth underneath was nobody's to touch.

She should have left it there. She knew, afterward, that she had known even then.

"It isn't really about the show, is it," she said. The little crowd had thinned but not gone; Dot was near, and a knot of bakery women, and someone's husband. "The jam. That's just the thing they let themselves fight about. Whatever's actually between those two —" she nodded down the hall, lightly, the way you would point out a heron — "it's old, and it's private, and it hurt her badly, and she's been standing at the wrong end of every room in this town for years pretending it's about preserves."

She was right. She felt herself be right the way you feel a glaze come out true. And then she felt the room.

It did not gasp. Nothing so theatrical. It simply went quiet around her in a widening ring, the particular quiet of people who have all decided at once to study something else. Dot's mouth folded shut. A woman who had been holding one of the blue dishes set it down very gently, as though it had become hot. Down the hall, Iris Poole had not heard — she was too far — but the silence travelled toward her like a change in the weather, and a man near her glanced at Maggie and then deliberately away.

And Faye, at the bakery table, stopped slicing.

Maggie watched her friend's face do something she had no name for. Not anger. Something more careful and more closed, a door easing shut from the inside, the same expression Faye had worn weeks ago when Iris Poole's name had first come up over sourdough — except that this time it was pointed at Maggie. *You said that out loud,* the face said. *In here. About her.* And under it, fainter and worse: *You saw it, and you couldn't keep it.*

The metallic ring under the noise had become a real pressure behind Maggie's left eye. She put her cup down.

It was Mary Burrell who moved first, and Maggie, raw and exposed and watching everything, watched her too. Mary was at the servery hatch where she had spent the morning helping with the tea, and into the small frozen moment she simply went on working, as if working were a kind of mercy she was extending to the room. She took up a tea towel from the basket at her elbow and folded it — once, twice, the corners brought square with two flat passes of her hand, a fold so practised it was barely a decision — and laid it down. Then she reached along the shelf above the urn and began setting the washed cups back into their places, not looking at them, her hand finding each gap without searching, turning each handle to the same angle as the last. Cup, gap, cup, the row coming true under her fingers while she gazed mildly out at the hall as though her hands belonged to someone steadier than the rest of them.

Glaze chose that moment to make his contribution to the morning. He had got in somehow — he always got in — and he came out from under Mary's servery with the corner of a tea towel in his teeth, dragging it like a small ginger looter, and bolted for the basket of clean linen and dived into it, and a child shrieked with laughter, and the spell of the silence broke into ordinary noise again. Out on the foreshore a pelican that had been working the sausage-roll stall for twenty patient minutes finally judged its moment, lifted a whole roll off the warming tray with the unhurried confidence of a creature that has never once been caught, and was gone over the water before the woman with the tongs had finished her sentence. People laughed at that too. The market closed back over the moment Maggie had made and went on without her.

But it was not gone. She could feel it the way you feel a stain set. The women who had clustered at her table had business elsewhere now. The blue dish stayed where it had been set down.

Cal came back. Of course he came back; he had been watching from somewhere near the door. He did not say *I told you to eat*, which would have been kinder than the way he looked at her, which was not unkind at all, only level, and worried, and disappointed in a way that had nothing to do with shelves. He picked up the paper cup she had abandoned, the third one, and dropped it in the bin without a word, and that was somehow the worst thing anyone had done to her all morning.

He went to the servery. Mary handed him a tea without being asked, her hand sure on the urn tap, and the two of them stood with their backs half to the hall and Maggie could not hear most of it over the noise. But she had spent the morning making the room legible, and she could not switch it off now that she wanted to, and she read the shapes of it: Cal's small jerk of the head toward her table, the question in it. And Mary's reply, low, even, her eyes moving once to Maggie and away, a mouth shaping something that ended in a word Maggie caught whole because it was aimed, gently, to be caught — *watched* — and the shape of the rest of it assembling itself in the air whether Maggie wanted it or not. *She likes it too much, that one. Being watched.*

Mary folded another tea towel. Square, square. Set it down.

Maggie's eye throbbed. She wanted, with a force that frightened her, to be back in the dark of the studio with the doors shut. She began, clumsily, to pack the safe mugs into the crate, leaving the ambitious bowl for last where it had sat untouched all day, and she was reaching for it when Faye appeared at the end of her table.

Faye did not say anything for a moment. She had taken off her apron. She was looking at Maggie with something more complicated than warmth — something that was still trying to be kind and was not sure it could manage — and she opened her mouth to speak.

Then her phone began to ring.
