# Chapter 05

Maggie arrived early, which she regretted within thirty seconds. Early meant the stalls were still half-built and the hall smelled of damp canvas and yesterday's scones, and early meant she had to stand with her hands empty while people who had known each other for decades moved past her carrying trestle tables and crates of jam jars and calling to each other by first names. She had come because Faye had said come, and because a Saturday market in a coastal town was the kind of thing a person who was trying to live here would go to. She was trying to live here. That was the state she was in: trying, and not yet sure where to stand.

She found a spot near the door, between a rack of second-hand coats and a stack of plastic chairs, and she waited. The hall was a big timber room with a high ceiling and a concrete floor that had been swept but still smelled of the sea. Bunting had been strung from the rafters — handmade, slightly crooked, the kind of decoration that had gone soft at the folds from being put up and taken down too many times. Through the open doors at the far end she could see the foreshore: a strip of wind-bent grass, the gravel path, and beyond it the flat grey line of the bay. The market was supposed to fill both — the hall and the foreshore — but at this hour only the hall was occupied, and only barely.

Beryl crossed the hall carrying a clipboard and a folded trestle table under one arm, and the room straightened around her. She was a compact woman in a navy windcheater with the sleeves pushed up, and she moved with the economy of someone who had been running this market since before Maggie had lived in the city and would be running it after Maggie had left. She did not hurry. She did not repeat herself. She said "the honey goes on the left, the produce in the middle, Faye you're on the servery again" in a single pass and it was done. People moved. She adjusted a trestle leg with her foot as she went past, without stopping, without looking down. The clipboard was not decorative. Maggie could see items being ticked and small annotations being made. This was a woman who ran a town the way other people ran a filing system, and the town let her, because the alternative was chaos and nobody in Pelican's Crook had the energy for chaos.

Maggie drifted along the side wall, past the op-shop table where Dot and Glad had installed themselves behind a battery of mismatched crockery. They were seated, which gave the impression of a customer-service counter, but the arrangement was really an observation post. Dot was pricing a teacup with a sticker gun. Glad was watching the door. They were both wearing reading glasses on chains, which gave them the look of a pair of bank tellers from a branch that had closed in 1994.

"He was here at seven," Dot said, not looking up from the teacup.

"Quarter past," Glad said.

"It was seven. I saw the car."

"You saw the car at quarter past. I saw the car at seven and it was a different car."

Dot applied a sticker with the careful deliberation of a woman who was certain. "The Commodore was at seven. The Falcon was at quarter past. Don't tell me what I saw, Glad."

"I'm not telling you what you saw. I'm telling you when you saw it."

They did not appear to require resolution. The conversation continued in this fashion — each sister adding a detail the other contradicted — while between them they missed nothing. Maggie stood nearby pretending to look at a ceramic jug, and she could feel the attention coming off them like warmth off a woodstove. They clocked her. They did not acknowledge her. They would, she suspected, discuss her at length the moment she was out of earshot.

She moved on. Near the entrance, Saffron had set up a turmeric latte stand — a reclaimed timber crate, a row of glass jars of ground spice in descending shades of gold, a hand-lettered sign that read SOUL-SYNC in a font that suggested tranquillity was a deliverable. Saffron was in a marigold scarf and a linen tunic, pressing cups of yellow latte on people who mostly accepted them and walked away without drinking. Maggie watched her offer a cup to a farmer in a rugby jumper who took it with the flat, polite expression of a man humoring a child. Saffron smiled. The smile was practised. Then a woman at the honey stall said something to her neighbour — not loudly, not cruelly, just audible — about people who came to the coast to find themselves, and Saffron's hand tightened on the next cup. Just for a second. Then the serenity was back, and the cup was held out. Maggie noted it. She didn't know what it meant.

Calloway was by the produce. He was a tall, upright man in a country-tweed jacket, the kind that had been bought in a town two hours inland and meant to last a decade. He held himself the way retired magistrates hold themselves — as if the room might at any moment require order and he was the one to restore it. A woman Maggie didn't recognise appeared at his elbow with a plate bearing a single scone, split and buttered. She did not hand it to him. She set it on the edge of the produce table, said something about it being from the batch she'd made last Tuesday, and left. Calloway did not thank her. He nodded — a small, precise nod, the nod of a man acknowledging that a ledger entry had been made. He ate the scone standing up, slowly, and Maggie understood that the scone was a payment. She did not know for what. The woman's shoulders had been slightly hunched as she walked away, the posture of someone who had settled a debt and hoped the matter was now closed.

Outside on the foreshore the wind had picked up and the canvas awnings were snapping. Maggie stepped through the hall doors into the cold. The gravel path ran between two rows of stalls — honey, jam, wool and knitwear, the surf club sausage sizzle, a man selling driftwood mirrors nobody needed. Beyond the stalls the bay was grey and working at itself, small chop coming in at an angle to the wind. A dog was barking at a child who was barking back. The salt on the air was thicker out here, mixed with fried onion from the sausage sizzle and the wet-wool funk of a rack of hand-knitted jumpers. Maggie walked the line of stalls with her cardigan pulled closed and her hands in her pockets, and she felt the particular pleasure of being among people doing ordinary things on a cold day — the fried onion and the wind and the woman at the honey stall explaining that the dark one was from tea-tree and tasted of medicine.

Cobber was near the foreshore rail, leaning against a post with the unhurried posture of a man who had been leaning against posts for decades. A tourist in a clean parka was asking him what time the market closed. Cobber considered the question.

"Time's different down here," he said. "It's not the clock. It's the tide and the light. Market goes when Beryl says. But the day — the day goes when the day's done." He looked at the bay, squinting. "You can't rush a Saturday. It's the one day left that knows what it's for."

The tourist thanked him and walked away no better informed.

She went back inside. The hall had filled. The noise had thickened into the particular hum of a room full of people who were not buying much but were very committed to the social occasion. Faye appeared beside her with a plate of samples — small squares of sourdough with butter and quince paste.

"You're here," Faye said, as if this were an event. "Good. You're standing. Come and stand near me. Standing is what we do."

Faye steered her toward the servery. On the way past the op-shop table Dot said, without looking up, "The new one's with Faye," and Glad said, "I can see that, Dot," and Maggie, surprised, laughed. The laugh was louder than she'd meant. A woman at the knitwear stall looked over. Maggie felt the heat of it — the small, specific heat of being noticed in a room where everyone knew everyone and she knew no one.

Faye handed her a square of sourdough and said, "Tell me who made the lemon slice."

Maggie looked at the slice table. She looked for three seconds. "The one in the middle. The tall one with the icing. The woman in the green apron baked it."

Faye stared at her. "How do you know that?"

"The icing's been re-applied. She iced it at home, brought it, then touched it up here. She's been hovering. And she moved it to the front after the Anzac biscuits went, because she thinks it's better than the Anzacs. She's right, but she shouldn't let it show."

Faye beamed. Maggie felt the beam — the first thing she had felt all morning that was not loneliness or the effort of hiding it. Faye said, loud enough for the next table, "She knows the lemon slice." The woman in the green apron turned around and looked at Maggie with the expression of someone who had been caught being competitive about cake and did not mind.

From there it went. Maggie stood near the servery and the room came to her, and she said the things she saw. She told Dot that the woman at the wool stall had not spoken to the woman at the honey stall once all morning, and Dot said, "Marlene and Colleen. That's the dog. Colleen's kelpie got Marlene's chickens. '22." Maggie said, "Two years." Dot said, "Nobody counts. You counted." And she said it with a kind of respect that Maggie did not expect from a woman who had not yet made eye contact with her.

She told Faye that the man at the driftwood mirrors was pretending not to be cold because the woman at the candle stall had said he should've worn a jacket, and Faye said, "That's Bruce. He'd freeze to death before he'd admit Donna was right," and three people within earshot laughed, and one repeated it to the next table, and Maggie heard her own observation come back to her at second hand, and it was a small thing but it landed in her chest.

Beryl, passing with the clipboard, stopped. She looked at Maggie. She looked at Faye. She said, "The servery needs cups. If you're going to stand there you could be useful." She did not smile. But she did not move Maggie on, and Maggie understood that in the language of Beryl's clipboard, being assigned a task was a form of inclusion more reliable than a welcome.

Cal appeared at the sausage sizzle, collecting orders. He nodded at Maggie across the hall — a single, level nod, the nod of a man who measured things twice. She felt being seen by him in a room where she had been trying to be seen, and the feeling was different from the room's warmth because it was one person and it was specific and she did not yet know what to do with it.

It was after the sausages, when Maggie had helped stack cups and wipe the servery and was starting to feel that the morning had become a place she could stand in, that she saw the tea towel.

Cal's aunt — Maggie had heard the name, Burrell, from Faye, but the woman was still "Cal's aunt" in her mind — was at the kitchen servery. She had been there all morning, efficient and unobtrusive, the kind of volunteer who did not need to be asked. She was tidying. She moved through the servery the way Beryl moved through the hall — with a settled authority, the ease of someone who knew where everything went because she had always known where everything went.

The tea towel was hanging from the edge of the servery counter. Cal's aunt reached for it without looking at it. Her hand went to it the way a hand goes to a light switch in a dark room you have lived in for years — automatic, spatial, no search involved. She caught it, refolded it in three movements, and set it on the shelf under the servery in a particular spot, adjusted it a quarter-inch to the left, and turned away. The whole thing took four seconds. She had not glanced at the towel at any point.

Except. Except that the reach had no hesitation in it. The refold had no decision in it. The quarter-inch adjustment was the adjustment of a woman placing an object in the exact place it had always occupied, in a kitchen she treated as her own. Maggie knew the gesture. She had seen it in her own kitchen, with her own things, in the house she had left. It was the gesture of ownership. The body knowing where a thing lives because the thing lives where the body has always put it.

It was odd. That was all Maggie made of it. She filed it under odd and turned back to the cups. The morning was too good to let an oddness sit in. She wiped the counter. She let the tea towel go.

She was putting the last cup on the stack when Cal's aunt passed behind her. The woman stopped. She said, easily, "You've got the hang of the servery. Good. Beryl needs people who'll just get on with it." She smiled. It was a warm smile, and it was ordinary, and Maggie would not have thought anything of it except that it came half a beat late. The words arrived, then the smile arrived, as if the smile had been sent separately and taken a different route.

Maggie said, "I'm happy to help," and the woman moved on, and Maggie did not think about it for the rest of the market. She thought about it later, in the studio with her hands in clay, and she could not have told anyone what she thought. She only knew that something about the smile had not matched, the way a tile in an otherwise even floor does not match, and she had noticed, and she did not know why she had noticed, and the not-knowing sat in her quietly, like a cup set down and not yet picked up.
