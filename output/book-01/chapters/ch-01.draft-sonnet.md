The hatchback smelled of iron oxide, old cardboard, damp canvas, and clay dust — the particular clay dust that settles into everything you own when you have been throwing pots in a Brunswick kitchen for seven years and calling it a hobby. Everything Maggie had chosen to keep from the old life was physically present: three crates of glazed seconds wrapped in newspaper, a wheel cover washed so many times it had given up being any particular colour, a box of tools rattling in the boot on every bend. Everything she had refused to keep was absent in the way that absences announce themselves.

Brunswick had fallen away. Geelong had fallen away. Now the road had narrowed into coast, the Otways crowding in from both sides — eucalypts dark with recent rain, fern gullies exhaling cold air, the sea arriving and disappearing between trunks as if deciding whether to introduce itself. Every few kilometres a brown sign pointed towards a lookout and Maggie did not stop at a single one, because if she stopped too often she would turn the car around.

The coast, when it showed itself fully, was unreasonable — cliffs dropping straight into the water, swells breaking white far below, the sea that particular autumn silver that made everything look both important and cold. She was frightened. She was also awake in a way she had not been for years.

She had been to Pelican's Crook before: four years ago, a regional arts assignment, writing a polite piece about the pottery trail. She had stood too long in the doorway of this exact studio, watching water-light move over glaze, and felt something she could not immediately categorise — unusual for a woman who had spent twenty years categorising other people's feelings for a living. She had laughed at herself quietly and gone home to Brunswick, to Tommy, to the sensible life that had begun to fit like a shoe bought in the wrong size. The divorce had sorted that out.

After, she had searched listings along the whole coast: somewhere cheaper, busier, safer. None of those places had lodged under her ribs. Pelican's Crook had. The Wheelhouse had a real kiln, a flat upstairs so she could live over the shop, and enough distance from Brunswick that Tommy's version of her life could not keep walking in uninvited. The lease was risky but just possible with the settlement. And that afternoon four years ago was her private proof she had wanted this before the marriage ended — that this was not simply a post-divorce panic purchase.

She tried on versions of the decision as the road bent south: a sea-change (real estate agent), a breakdown (Tommy), an artist's retreat (someone with linen trousers). In her own words: a woman finally driving towards the life she should have noticed sooner.

*"A shop. With your savings. Christ, Mags, you do know hobbies are meant to cost less than a mortgage."*

She reached for the radio. Then she stopped herself and did not turn it on. She was not going to drown herself out.

The clay dust was still on her left sleeve from loading the car. Her hands had loosened on the wheel somewhere south of the cape. The sea kept flashing between trees, and then a break in the Otways opened up the bay — a crook of coast, a headland curling around a pocket of cold grey water, the town sitting on the thin shelf between sea and steep green hills as if someone had placed it there carefully and hoped no one would find it.

Pelican's Crook. It sounded, she thought, like a bird, an illness, and a crime. This was either a sign or very bad branding. She had not yet decided which.

---

It was ten past two when she drove up the main street. Off-season was legible in every shopfront: tourist brightness packed away, short-hours signs, a bakery with its awning tied back, a hardware shop that had seen no reason to change its approach since approximately the sixties, two pubs that were either closed or considering it. Gulls in the car park. The sea visible between buildings in a thin grey-green strip.

She drove the street once before parking — new enough to have missed the turn, proud enough not to do a three-point turn in front of the whole town. She thought of it as stakeholder reconnaissance, then banned herself from the phrase. She was no longer in HR. She was driving slowly.

No one waved. One woman coming out of the post office glanced at the hatchback and looked away. Maggie felt the relief of being unseen and the disappointment of it at almost exactly the same moment, which seemed accurate to her situation.

The Wheelhouse was where she remembered it. Salt-hazed glass in the front window, weathered timber frontage, the bones of a former bait-and-tackle shop still readable in the proportions — wide enough for gear, high enough for rods, a door that suggested it had once admitted people who did not need to be charmed. Not twee. Not a postcard. A working room that had been waiting.

She sat in the car for a moment. Through the glass she could see nothing but her own reflection and the light on the surface, and she thought of standing in this same doorway four years ago, notebook in hand, thinking with embarrassing clarity: *if I had a life, it would look something like this*. She had gone home and been sensible for four more years.

The key was in her hand.

---

The door stuck slightly — salt in the frame, old timber swelled by winter damp. Then it gave, and the cold inside was a different cold from the street: held, still, faintly sweet with clay and the mineral undersmell of a kiln that had been waiting properly sealed.

She stood in the doorway for a moment and did not immediately do anything useful.

This was hers. Her own door. Her own floor. Her own kiln, standing against the back wall with the authority of something that had opinions. Twenty years managing other people's exits — redundancies, restructures, diplomatic departures — and none of it had made the beginning of something feel like this, which was frightening and real in equal measure.

*"You always did like the idea of things more than the work, Mags."*

The voice arrived so completely formed that she half-turned towards the doorway before she caught herself. She opened the two windows, propped the door, and went to the kiln.

She checked the elements first — no cracked coils, no scorching on the brick. Door seal intact. She ran a hand along the interior shelf, checked the kiln wash, gauged the stacking space. A good kiln. She allowed herself to be glad about it without making a ceremony of the gladness.

Then she started unpacking. The defensive stock came in first: half-glazed seconds, safe speckled mugs, small bowls in a sensible earth palette. She arranged them on the floor where the new shelves would go and told herself they were approachable — a sound commercial opening. Underneath: if the town rejected them, they hadn't been her best work anyway. The stronger pieces stayed wrapped in newspaper. The HR binders stayed in the boot, because bringing them inside would have been surrender. The boundary was new and a little ragged, but it was beginning.

---

The note on the counter had said only: *Here at 3. C.* In the two weeks since, Maggie had assembled a picture of C from very little evidence and decided, on the basis of his handwriting, that she liked him. She recognised this as the behaviour of a woman who had been alone long enough to start filling in the details herself.

At three o'clock exactly she heard the engine — a ute with the throat-clearing sound of something that had worked hard for years and had no complaints. He was tall, in a canvas work jacket gone soft at the elbows, a small level in the breast pocket. The stillness of someone who measured things for a living — not disengaged, just already done assessing the room before she'd finished noticing him.

"Cal Burrell."

"Maggie Quill."

She had prepared three approaches to this introduction and had somehow defaulted to the one that sounded like exchanging evidence at a border checkpoint.

Then the Too-Much arrived, lightly — a faint metal taste at the back of her throat, sound edges going too crisp, details sharpening past what she'd asked for. She breathed through her nose and took stock of what was actually observable: no ring, but a pale line where one had been, the tan faded — single for a while, then, not freshly so. Jacket elbows worn from habitual work against something hard. Boots resoled twice. A shallow cut on his right hand, clean, recent. When she said her name, his attention caught for a half-beat — possible interest, possible simple courtesy, and the gift could not tell her which.

She set it aside. Cal Burrell was not a grievance file to be decoded. He was a local tradesperson who had agreed to fit shelves, and she was the outsider who had just arrived in his town. She would not read him.

Being present turned out to mean she missed half the conversation.

"Three exactly," she said. "Should I be impressed or worried?"

"Neither." Cal looked at the east wall. "Clock in the ute works."

"Good. I was hoping for reliable rather than ominous."

"Ominous costs extra."

She tried once more to extend this into warmth. He turned it practical — asked about clearance from the kiln, what she intended to stack. He was not unkind. He simply did not perform interest he didn't have, and she had the sense he had watched enough people arrive in Pelican's Crook with a story the town didn't match to be in no hurry.

She showed him the east wall. He took out the level.

---

Cal worked. Maggie unpacked around him in careful choreography — she would place something on the floor and he would move it, measure behind it, return it without comment. He announced the third bracket, the clearance, the grain direction. Each piece of information delivered without preamble, as if she'd asked.

She found she liked the quiet and resented liking it.

The seconds went on the floor where the shelves would stand. The stronger pieces stayed wrapped at the wall, their spines inward. She was aware of Cal looking at them once — just once — without remark. Then, not turning from the wall: "Those the ones you're selling?"

"Those are the approachable ones."

"Approachable's not the same as good."

It landed in a place she had not expected. Tommy belittled; Cal refused to flatter. Both punctured the same story, and she briefly couldn't locate the difference.

She recovered by being funnier than she felt. "I'm opening a pottery studio, not an emotional-growth seminar."

Cal fitted the third bracket. "Shelves are cheaper than those."

"You've priced both?"

"Only shelves. Emotional growth tends to go over budget."

She laughed. He looked at her briefly, as if confirming that had been the intended effect, and went back to the wall.

Later, when the shelves were up, he asked without particular emphasis whether she was opening a shop or hiding in one. She started three different answers and finished none of them. Instead she went to the wrapped row, chose one piece — a tall vase with a crackle glaze she'd spent six attempts perfecting — and set it on the counter. Not on the shelf. Not for sale yet. Just present in the room.

It wasn't a resolution. But it was a start.

---

Cal swept most of the sawdust when he finished, leaving enough at the skirting board to prove the work had been real. When she offered coffee he declined — logistics rather than disinterest. She was relieved, because she still hadn't located the tea bags. And then mildly annoyed, because relief was not what she'd been hoping to feel.

He said he could come back for the upstairs shelves whenever she was ready. Practical. She took it as practical and did not add to it.

She watched the ute reverse up the main street and disappear around the corner. The town settled towards evening: low cloud coming in from the south, the sea going grey-green, gulls drifting over the car park in the last of the light, the hill shadow reaching across the far end of the street. Salt wind came through the open door and moved around the room.

That was when the cat arrived.

He was a ginger tom with one ear that had clearly lost an argument some time ago, and he wore clay dust in his coat like a credential. He came in without ceremony, inspected the kiln in the manner of someone conducting routine compliance work, and made his way to the counter where Maggie's good apron sat folded on the corner.

She almost said *stakeholder* before she caught herself. Landlord, perhaps. Or quality assurance. He had the bearing for either.

He settled on the apron. She could move him — the canvas was good and this was obviously a cat that treated clay dust as a lifestyle choice. She looked at him: the serious expression, the ruined ear, the air of a creature who had assessed the premises and found management barely adequate. She did not move him. She called him Glaze, without having planned to name him at all.

The last light left the salt-hazed glass. Maggie stood in the middle of the room and took stock: shelves on the east wall that had raised more questions than she'd answered, a row of seconds that were not her best work, one vase on the counter that was, a kiln that worked, a cat on her good apron. Tommy's voice had visited twice and she had kept driving through it. Cal had not flattered her. The town had not welcomed her. There was still sawdust on the floor.

She crossed to the door and thought about closing it against the cold. Then she left it open instead. The salt wind moved through the room. The sea was outside. She was here.
