# Chapter Three

Maggie had been at the wheel for forty minutes before she understood the bowl was wrong.

Not cracked — wrong in a way that took longer to name. The walls were even and the rim was true, and she had thrown it with the particular care that comes from wanting a thing to be right so badly that the wanting gets into your wrists. That was the problem. The bowl was careful. The clay had recorded every ounce of the effort and none of the ease.

She cut it off the hump and set it on the board with the other two. Three bowls. None of them bad. None of them hers — not yet. She wiped her palms on her jeans and looked at them in a row, and the looking was gentle, which was worse than if it had been harsh.

The glazes were in jars along the back shelf, labelled in her own handwriting — optimistic, since she'd never remember which was the rutile and which was the tin-white when the time came. She had mixed three test batches the day before, small quantities in old yoghurt containers. The first was a pale celadon she was happy with. The second had come out closer to hospital green than intended, a lesson about cobalt. The third she had not opened yet. She peeled back the lid, and the colour inside was a deep, unseasonable teal she could not account for, and she stood with the container in both hands.

That was when the first shimmer crossed her left eye.

She set the jar down. She knew the shimmer. She had known it, in the way of a person who has had the same visitor for twenty-four years, since she was nineteen in a university library in Melbourne and the world had slid sideways and the fluorescent light had become unbearable and she had vomited into a waste-paper bin. She had not known then that the first one is never the last one.

---

The migraine took forty minutes to fully arrive, and it arrived the way it always did, which was not as pain at first but as an invasion of detail. The pain came behind the detail, following it like weather follows a change in the barometer.

She made it to the back room, closed the door against the studio light, pulled the blue-striped tea towel off the hook, and lay down on the cot with the towel folded over her eyes.

The pain arrived. It was not general. It was a point of specific pressure behind her left eye, a concentration of weight, as if someone had pressed a thumb into the bone and held it there with a patience that no actual thumb could sustain. It was steady, and it was bright, and behind it the world was pouring in.

This was the part she had never been able to explain to anyone, and she had tried — to a GP in Carlton who had given her a pamphlet, to a neurologist in Fitzroy who had asked about stress and moved on, to her ex-husband, Paul, who had been sympathetic about the fact of the pain and unsympathetic about the content of it. The pain was the headline. What lived inside it was harder to say.
Under the tea towel, in the dark, her eyes were open, and the dark was not dark. The light came in a thin precise blade under the door, a line of gold so specific she could have told you the angle — eleven degrees from horizontal, cut by the door's lower edge against the studio's concrete floor. It was not too bright. It was too detailed. The light had texture. She could see the dust suspended in it, each mote at its own altitude, and none of it stopped.

Glaze was on the concrete in the studio, his claws in the particular ticking rhythm of a cat settling — tick, tick, a pause, tick tick tick — and she could already read it: he was circling the kiln, would settle against the base in approximately ten seconds, was comfortable. The rhythm was information. It arrived with the authority of fact. She did not want the fact and could not put it down.

The skirting board beside the cot had three layers of paint. Now, in the migraine, each layer was separate and nameable: the original white, thick, applied with a brush that had left ridges; over that, a cream that had yellowed; and on top, a gloss too white for the room, too new, her own. She could see the boundary between each layer where the paint had chipped, and the boundaries were as legible as text, and the text said nothing, and she could not stop reading.

The thumb behind her eye pressed. The pain spread, bilateral now, settling into the right side. Her stomach clenched. She breathed through her nose in the way she had learned, the way that didn't help the pain but helped her stay in the room rather than going to the place the pain wanted to take her.

---

The call was the part she couldn't reconstruct afterward.

She had reached for the phone on the shelf, the old handset she kept because her mobile was upstairs charging. She had dialled the number on the card Faye had left on the counter — *Dr N. Hartigan, Wreckers Bluff Medical, appointments and home visits* — Faye tapping the card twice, saying, "He picks up." Someone had answered. She had spoken, or tried to, the particular difficulty of forming words when language becomes a drawer of objects in the wrong order. She may have said migraine. She may have said please.

What she knew for certain was that at some point — twenty minutes later, thirty, the time had lost its edges — there was a knock, and then the sound of the door opening, which she had not locked, and footsteps that were not Glaze's.

---

"Maggie?"

The voice was close. Not loud. A male voice, and the vowels had that slight narrowing that coastal Victoria does to speech. She pulled the tea towel from her eyes.

Dr Neil Hartigan was sitting on the edge of the cot. She had not seen him come in, which meant he had come in quietly, and she had not seen him sit, which meant he had waited — for how long, she didn't know — before the sitting became the thing to do. He was not in a hurry, and that was so unlike the doctors she had seen before that it took her a moment to categorise.

He was older than she had pictured. Late fifties, maybe sixty, with the kind of face that had been handsome at thirty-five and had become more interesting at sixty, the lines deepening in a pattern that suggested a lot of listening. Canvas jacket, the colour of dry eucalypt, wool jumper underneath, and a leather bag that was not new and not pretending to be. The bag was on the floor by his feet — set down without leaning it against anything, which meant he had done this before.

His eyes moved around the room — the glaze chemicals, the cot, the tea towel in her hand. He looked at her the way you look at something you are trying to understand.

"How long?" he said.

"Since I was nineteen."

"No — this one. How long has this one been going?"

She did the arithmetic. "An hour and a half. Maybe two."

He nodded. He did not write anything down. "Can you tell me the sequence? What happens first, what comes after."

Nobody had ever asked her that.

She tried. "The shimmer first. In my left eye. Zig-zag, at the edge. Brighter than bright. It lasts twenty minutes before the pain starts."

"And after the shimmer?"

"The pain. Behind the left eye first. Then it spreads."

He was listening. Not performing listening — actually doing it, his body still, his attention in one place. She had seen the performed version hundreds of times across the conference table in HR, and this was not it.

"What about the other part?" he said.

She went cold. "What other part?"

"You hesitated before the shimmer. There was something before it."

He had heard the pause she had put in to edit herself. She had been editing the mad parts out of her medical history for twenty-four years, because she had learned what happened when you told a doctor the full account. They leaned back. They asked about stress. They wrote on the pad.

"During the pain," she said, "everything becomes very detailed. Not clearer — more. I can see things I shouldn't be able to see. Layers of paint on a skirting board. The angle of light under a door. The rhythm of the cat's claws on the floor, and what it means. The texture of a sound."

She had said it. The true thing. She could feel the old reflex trying to pull it back, to attach the apology that says *I know how this sounds*. She did not attach the apology. She let the sentence sit.

Neil did not lean back. He did not ask about stress.

"How long has that part been happening?"

"Since the beginning. Since the first one."

"And you've told doctors about it?"

"I've tried."

"And?"

"They tell me it's stress. Or anxiety. Or that I'm overdoing things. One told me I should keep a journal."

"I'm going to disagree with all of them," Neil said. His voice was level. He was not performing warmth, he was simply present and unhurried, and the unhurried quality was its own kind of care. "What you're describing — the sensory component, the specificity of it — that's not stress or anxiety. It's part of the migraine, and it's real, and the fact that nobody has taken it seriously doesn't mean it isn't a recognised pattern."

He asked her more. Family history — her mother had had them, bad ones, days in darkened rooms. Triggers — she didn't know, though she suspected the light, the flat hard southern light off the water. After-effects — the exhaustion, the world too bright for a day after. He checked her pupils with a penlight, asked her to follow his finger. He did not touch her head or press her temples. He asked what she had taken — sumatriptan when she could get it, paracetamol when she couldn't — and nodded without criticising.

"I'd like to look into this further," he said. "Not today. But next week, come into the surgery. Bring whatever you have of your history. And in the meantime — " He paused. He was thinking, not performing thought. "Keep a log. Not a journal — a log. Date, time, what you were doing before it started, what you perceived during. The specific things, not how you felt about them. Can you do that?"

"I can do that."

He stood. He picked up the bag, and it made a small sound against his leg. He looked at the door, then back at her.

"You're not mad," he said. "I want to be clear about that. What you're experiencing is real, and it's neurological, and it has a pattern, and the pattern is worth documenting properly, which is what we're going to do."

He said it the way he said everything — without emphasis, the words landing where he put them. He left through the studio, closing the front door with a click that was quiet enough to be deliberate.

---

The room settled after he left. The migraine was still there, diminished, the thumb behind her eye easing to a dull pressure that would linger for hours. Glaze's claws ticked on the concrete, nearer now, and then the weight of him arrived on the end of the cot, settling with the gravity of a cat who has decided you need supervision. His fur was warm against her ankle. He purred, a low mechanical thing, and she could feel its frequency in the bone of her leg.

She lay there for a long time. The light under the door had moved — the angle shifted while Neil was here, the gold going thinner — and the detail was still there, but the volume of it had dropped, the way a radio drops when you turn the dial. The world was becoming a room again, a room with a cat and a tea towel with a blue stripe and three careful bowls that were not yet hers.

She thought about the sentence. *You're not mad.* Three words. Twenty-four years of the shimmer and the detail and the pain, and the word stress, and the word journal, and not one of them — not one — had said the three words that cost nothing to say.

She sat up. Glaze adjusted without complaint. She reached for her bag, the canvas one with the notebooks, found a pen, opened to a blank page.

She wrote the date. She wrote the time — approximately, because she had not checked the clock when the shimmer started, and she would do better next time. She wrote: *glazing, third test batch, opened the container.* She wrote: *shimmer left eye, 20 min, then pain bilateral.* She wrote: *light under door, 11 degrees. Paint layers on skirting — three. Glaze rhythm — tick tick pause, three ticks, settle.*

She looked at what she had written. It was not a journal. It was a record — the specific things, not the feelings about them. Neil had asked for what she saw, and she had told him, and between the two of them the thing she had been carrying alone for twenty-four years was, for the first time, shared.

She closed the notebook and hung the tea towel on the hook. Glaze was asleep. The kiln clicked as it cooled, a sound like a clock adjusting.

Maggie sat on the edge of the cot with the notebook closed beside her. The migraine was still there, and the room was still too bright, and she was not fine, not yet. But the sentence that had been the whole of it for twenty-four years — *this is what you are, and it will not change* — had, for the first time, a crack in it.

A crack was enough. Clay taught you that. A crack was where the next thing came through.
