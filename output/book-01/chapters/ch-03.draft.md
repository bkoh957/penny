# Chapter 03 — The Doctor Who Listens Too Late

The light off the water came in low through the front of the studio that afternoon, and Maggie knew before she could name it that something was wrong with the way she was seeing it.

She had been glazing the second batch of mugs, the safe ones, the ones she could sell without having to defend, and the work had been going well in the particular way that work goes well when you have stopped thinking about whether it is going well at all. She wanted nothing more than to finish the batch before anyone wandered in off the street. A clean afternoon. No witnesses to the unglamorous middle of the thing, only the finished pots later, lined up as though they had always intended to be that good.

Then the edge of the window caught the sun and the sun came back at her as a thin metal line, bright as a struck wire, and she set the brush down because she had been here before and she knew what the thin metal line meant.

The migraines had started when she was nineteen. She had learned, over twenty-four years, the small civilities of their arrival — the way the world announced itself a few minutes ahead, sharpening at the corners, offering one last clear look before it took the lights down. She had time. Not much. Enough to find the back room, the cool dark of it, and to be unwell where the main street could not see her doing it.

She did not make it to the back room.

The light went from a line to a sheet. Every white surface in the studio — the lime-washed wall, the bag of plaster, the belly of the kiln — turned faintly to foil, and the foil hummed. She got herself onto the stool by the wheel and put both hands flat on the cool of the bench and breathed, and the breathing was very loud, and somewhere behind her Glaze chose that moment to cross the concrete floor.

She had never once, in three weeks, heard the cat walk. He moved the way ginger toms move, which is to say he arrived places without appearing to travel. But now each of his claws met the concrete separately, a small hard tick, four of them to a footfall, and the ticks went into her skull like upholstery tacks driven by a careful hand. She heard him stop. She heard him sit. She heard, she would have sworn, the soft collapse of his weight settling.

It was the glazes that undid her.

They were ranged on the shelf at her left, the test tiles she kept for matching, forty little squares of fired colour, and she had looked at them ten thousand times and seen pleasant differences of taste. Now she could not stop the colours from meaning something. The cobalt was not blue, it was a held breath; the iron-red was a thing said and regretted; a streaky tin-white near the end of the row was grief, plain grief, she had no idea why, and she could not make it stop being grief and go back to being a tile. The meanings poured off the shelf faster than she could sort them, none of them labelled, none of them hers, and she understood with the small cold part of her that always stayed standing that she was recording far more than she could ever hope to read.

She did not hear the front door. She heard Faye's voice, and then Faye's voice stopped being cheerful.

"Maggie? Maggie, love—" A bag set down too fast, paper crumpling. "No, don't get up. Don't. I'm getting Neil."

She wanted to say there was no need. The words were a long way off and very heavy and she let them lie.

Time did the thing it did inside the pain, folding so that the next part seemed to follow immediately: a man's tread, unhurried, and a voice that did not soften itself for the occasion.

"Curtains," the voice said. "Both of them. And the door." A pause. "The cat can stay, he's not the problem."

The foil dimmed. Someone had drawn the calico across the front windows, and the relief of it was so total that Maggie made a sound she was not proud of.

"Don't apologise," the voice said, which she had not done, but had been about to. "How long have you had these?"

She got the number out. Since nineteen.

"And in those—" she heard him do the arithmetic and decline to round it kindly "—twenty-four years, has anyone asked you what you see, as opposed to what you feel?"

No one had. She said so.

"No," Dr Neil Hartigan said, as though this confirmed something he already held a low opinion of. "They wouldn't."

He sat down. Not on the stool — he took the second stool and brought it close and sat where she could have reached him, and he began to ask questions, and the questions were not the questions. They were better than the questions. He did not ask whether she was stressed. He did not ask whether she was sleeping, or drinking enough water, or whether things at home — there being, mercifully, no home and no things — were difficult just now. He asked whether the bright part came before or with the pain. Whether sound and light arrived together or in sequence. Whether she had ever, afterwards, remembered a detail she had no memory of noticing. He asked it flatly, watching her, and when she said yes to that last one his attention sharpened in a way she would think about later, when she could think.

"Interesting," he said, and did not explain why, and she found she resented being made curious while she was in pain.

He was not kind. That was the strange thing, the thing she turned over afterwards. He was thorough, and thoroughness from him felt almost exactly like being taken seriously, but there was no warmth in the cup he was offering. When she got an answer slightly wrong — confused the order of two things — he said, "No, listen to the question," with the impatience of a man who has spent thirty years being the only person in the room who is paying attention and has stopped pretending otherwise. He told her what she likely had, in two clean sentences, and what could be done, in three, and then he told her the name of a specialist in Geelong and made her repeat it because, he said, she would not remember it in this state and people never wrote things down.

She did not like him. She was also, helplessly, grateful, and the two feelings sat together without resolving, the way the glaze colours had refused to sort themselves into anything she could file.

The pain crested while he was there and began, slowly, to go out with the tide. By the time she could keep her eyes open against the dimmed light he was standing, and she was embarrassed in the ordinary way again, which was its own kind of recovery.

It was then that he saw the vase.

It sat on the floor by the slop bucket, where she had put the things that were going out — a tall, heavy, graceless thing she had thrown in a bad week, a vase that had slumped on one side and that she had tried to save with a glaze she was testing, an oxblood that had gone wrong in the firing and pooled thick and dark at the base like something congealing. It was ugly. She knew it was ugly. It was on its way to the bin and she had only been putting off the walk to the skip.

Neil picked it up. He turned it in his hands, slowly, against the dim window light, and he looked at it for longer than a thing like that had any right to be looked at.

"How much," he said.

She thought she had misheard. "That one's not for sale. That's a—" she searched for the honest word and found it "—a failure. I'm throwing it out."

"How much," he said again, and there was no negotiation in it.

She named a figure too low to mean anything, the price of admitting it was rubbish, and he paid it in coins counted out exactly onto the bench, and he took the vase, and at the door he turned the heavy thing once more in the last of the light.

"Most people," he said, "spend their lives trying to hide where they came apart." He looked at the dark pooled glaze at the base. "This doesn't bother." Then, dry as the plaster on the shelf, unreadable: "I'll have the ugliest thing in your shop, Mrs Quill. It's the only honest piece in the room."

And he left, with the vase under his arm, and Maggie decided — because she wanted to, because it was a kinder story and she had had a bad enough afternoon — that this had been a generosity, a doctor's clumsy way of saying she was not as bad as she feared. She put the coins in the drawer. Her first proper sale, and it had nothing commercial about it at all.

Faye came back in once he had gone, and so did the street.

Two women had stopped on the footpath outside — Maggie knew one of them by sight, a sturdy person from the surf-club committee — and they watched Neil walk away up the main street with the vase, and they did not lower their voices the way people lower their voices when they think it matters whether they are heard.

"There he goes," the committee woman said. "Good of him to come, I suppose."

"Suppose," the other said, in a tone that withdrew the suggestion.

Faye busied herself with the kettle, which Maggie was learning was what Faye did when she did not want to be drawn. Maggie, still raw, still seeing the world a half-shade too bright, watched the two women through the gap in the calico and understood that whatever Neil Hartigan was to this town, it was not the simple thing his thoroughness had nearly persuaded her of.

It was the older of the two who said it, settling her cardigan, watching the doctor's retreating back with an expression Maggie could not place — something between grievance and grief.

"He used to be lovely, you know," the woman said. "Once. You'd not believe it now, but he was."

Maggie filed it where she filed things in a new town: under gossip, under the kind of remark every small place keeps in stock about everyone, true and untrue at once and not her business to sort. She had enough that she could not sort. She let it go.

Faye pressed a mug of sweet tea into her hands and did not ask questions, which was the single most generous thing anyone had done for her all day, and Glaze, who had taken violent objection to the medical authority in the room and decamped to the top of the kiln, came back down now that it was safe and arranged himself across her feet, dusted and disreputable and warm.

Maggie drank the tea. The metal had gone out of the light. The glazes on the shelf were only glazes again, forty small squares of fired colour that meant nothing but themselves, and she could not have said now which one had been grief.

She thought she had got through it. Privately, more or less. Seen as fragile by only one doctor and one friend, which in a town this size was practically anonymous.

She did not hear Neil come back. She heard the door, and looked up, and he was standing in it without the vase, having presumably deposited it somewhere up the street, and he had the look of a man who had walked half a block and turned around over something.

"That last detail," he said. No greeting. "Remembering things afterwards you never knew you saw." He studied her in the dim, and for once there was no impatience in it, only a kind of careful arithmetic. "I'd like you to come and see me. Properly. Not for the headaches — those will keep. There's something in your history I want to look into, and I'd rather do it when you're well enough to be useful." He named a day. "A week. Can you manage that?"

She said she could.

"Good," Neil Hartigan said, and this time he did not explain himself either, and then he was gone for the second time, up the main street, leaving Maggie holding a cooling mug of tea and the distinct sense that she had agreed to something she did not understand the shape of.
