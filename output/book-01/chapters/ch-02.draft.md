# Chapter Two

The knock came at nine exactly, which Maggie later decided was very Faye: the kind of person who arrived on time not because she was punctual but because she had already planned everything that needed doing before nine and everything that needed doing after, and nine was simply the seam.

Maggie opened the door of the flat above the studio to find a woman holding a loaf of sourdough in the crook of one arm and wearing an expression that said she had already made up her mind about Maggie and the verdict was cautiously optimistic. She was mid-forties, round-faced, with flour in the creases of her knuckles. The loaf was still warm.

"Faye Denton," the woman said. "I run the bakery on the main street. Welcome to Pelican's Crook. You'll want to eat that today."

Maggie accepted the loaf. "Thank you. I was going to find the bakery this morning."

"I know," Faye said, in a way that made it clear she knew a great many things. "But I thought I'd save you the trouble. You've got enough on your plate. Can I come in?"

She was already coming in.

---

Maggie put the kettle on. By the time it boiled, she had a rough map of Pelican's Crook, who owned what, and the fact that the op-shop on the main street was run by two sisters named Dot and Glad who had not, according to Faye, agreed on a single verifiable fact since the 1987 agricultural show.

"What happened in 1987?" Maggie asked.

"Oh, nobody really knows. Dot will tell you one thing and Glad will tell you the opposite and they'll both be completely convinced." Faye accepted her tea and tore off a piece of bread. "They're not fighting about it, mind you. They just have two separate versions of reality and they get along fine inside both of them." She paused. "The op-shop is also the general store, technically. They sell bread and milk along with the second-hand crockery. You'll want to go in — everyone does eventually, and they'll remember you haven't if you don't."

Maggie made a note of this, not literally. "Who else should I know about?"

Faye appeared to be genuinely considering the question, which meant she was deciding what order to deliver the information rather than whether to deliver it. She set down her mug and began.

The street, as Faye described it, had a logic. The bakery was three doors down from the op-shop, which was next to the post office, which was across from the newsagent that hadn't sold newspapers since 2019 but kept going on account of the scratch tickets and the fact that Reg Montrose, who ran it, had nowhere else to be. Cal Burrell's carpentry shed was at the far end, past the pub. The medical centre — "Dr Hartigan's been here forever, everyone goes to him, he's the sort of doctor who actually picks up the phone" — was set back slightly off the main drag, near the carpark.

"You've met Cal?" Faye said, at a certain point, in a particular tone.

"Yesterday," Maggie said. "He helped me with a delivery."

"Of course he did." The tone was still particular. It did not elaborate on itself.

Maggie let it go.

She found out about the jam feud almost accidentally, when Faye used the phrase *this year's entry* and then caught herself and changed the subject with the smooth efficiency of someone who had changed that subject many times. The feud, whatever it was, clearly had a long history and a nemesis somewhere in the agricultural show judging circuit. Maggie stored the fragment away. It would become clear eventually, or it wouldn't, and either way it was not her business.

By the time the loaf was half gone and the second pot of tea was cooling, Maggie knew: that the holiday houses on the south end of the street stood empty from April through September and their owners were regarded with the particular blend of gratitude and resentment that seemed universal to small towns reliant on seasonal money; that there was a morning walking group that met at the car park near the beach and that membership was in some way political; that Beryl Foss, chair of the Country Women's Association and what Faye called "the town's unofficial social architecture," was the person one needed to be on the right side of, or at least not the wrong side of, and that this distinction was finer than it appeared.

"Is she — " Maggie started.

"She's fine," Faye said, quickly. "She's very dedicated. She does an enormous amount for the town. She just." She picked up her mug. "She'll want to meet you. She'll find you, don't worry about that. Just be straightforward with her. She can spot when someone isn't, and she remembers."

Maggie looked at the bread. "I'm always straightforward."

"Good," Faye said, and there was something in her voice that made Maggie think being always straightforward might be more complicated in Pelican's Crook than it had been in her previous life.

---

She went out at eleven, once Faye had left — swept out, really, with the same efficient warmth she'd arrived with, pausing in the doorway to tell Maggie that the bakery opened at six and the sourdough sold out by eight-thirty, so if she was serious about bread she'd need to be there by eight. Maggie wrote this down for real, on a Post-it note stuck to the inside of the studio door.

The main street in the late autumn morning had a stripped quality, like a room after a party. The bunting from something — a festival, a market, she didn't know — was still strung between the lamp posts, salt-faded. A couple of cars parked outside the newsagent. The sea showed itself at the end of each cross-street, a flat grey-green panel, non-committal. The air smelled of salt and something that might have been frying.

She had walked most of it before she realized she'd met Cobber.

---

That had been this morning, before Faye arrived. Before eight, actually — she'd woken at six with the particular alertness that follows the first night of sleep in an unfamiliar place, the body not yet settled, and she'd pulled on her coat and gone down to the beach to see what it looked like in the early light.

It looked enormous. The tourist season had taken its chairs and its umbrellas and its families and gone, and what remained was the beach itself: wide, grey-white sand, kelp at the tideline, the water a colour that shifted between green and slate depending on the cloud. The wind came off the water at a cold sideways angle. She had the whole long stretch of it to herself, she thought, and then she didn't.

The man was sitting on a piece of driftwood at the far end of the beach, looking at the water. He was wearing a long-sleeved top that might have been a wetsuit underlayer and a beanie that might have been any colour under the salt and the weathering. He didn't turn around when she walked toward him, but she had the feeling he knew she was there.

"Morning," she said, when she was close enough.

"Yeah," he said. He was somewhere in his fifties, unhurried in every aspect. His face had that coastal texture that comes from decades of being outside without ever burning, just tanning to leather. He looked at her with genuine and unrushed interest. "New one," he said.

"Moved in yesterday," Maggie said. "I've taken the studio on the main street."

"The Wheelhouse." He nodded, as if this confirmed something. "Good spot. Stayed empty a while." He looked back at the water. "Dennis. But nobody calls me that."

"What do they call you?"

"Cobber." He didn't seem troubled by this. He moved over on the driftwood to make room and Maggie sat, because there was nothing else to do and nowhere else to be.

They watched the water for a while. There was a particular ease to sitting next to someone who clearly had no agenda at all. The surf was small and regular, the sound of it filling in the gaps.

"You'll like it here," Cobber said, eventually. "It's a bit quiet off-season but that's its own thing, you know. Town's different. You see more of what it actually is."

"Do you live here year-round?"

"Always about to move on," he said. "Been about to for a while now." He seemed to find this genuinely amusing, without urgency. "Been here thirty years. Every year I think, yeah, maybe next autumn. Then autumn comes and I stay." He lifted one shoulder. "Good beach. Good people, mostly."

Maggie looked down the length of the beach. Empty all the way to the point. "Is it always this quiet?"

"Morning, yeah. This time of year." He considered. "I'm here most mornings. Sometimes I see things." He glanced at her, mild, incurious. "Cars moving around at odd hours. Lights. People coming and going." He looked back at the water. "Town's got its business, like anywhere. I don't pay much attention."

Maggie filed this away as local colour. The things people mentioned when they were being companionable, filling the space. She didn't ask what he meant by odd hours. It didn't seem to call for a follow-up.

"Anyway," Cobber said, "you'll settle in."

She had thanked him and walked back along the waterline. When she came back down the main street at eleven, she glanced at the beach from the end of a cross-street. He was still there.

---

The op-shop was between the bakery and the post office, as Faye had described, and it smelled exactly as Faye had predicted: old fabric, lavender water, and something underneath that she couldn't place, the smell of things that have been kept a long time. The window was crowded with donated crockery arranged in a way that managed to be both random and somehow curated. A hand-lettered sign read: WE BUY, WE SELL, WE TAKE.

Maggie stopped to look at a blue jug in the window. She was considering its proportions — it was a good shape, whoever had made it — when she became aware of being watched.

Not from the street. From behind the display.

Two faces, identical in every particular she could determine through glass, between the jug and a stack of dinner plates. The same white hair, short and neat. The same expression, which was interested without being warm, the expression of two people watching something that had not yet proven itself. They were completely still.

Maggie raised a hand.

Neither face moved. Two pairs of eyes tracked her, in perfect synchrony, like security cameras that had learned to blink.

She went into the shop.

"Settling in?" said the one on the left.

"Just arrived yesterday," Maggie said.

"That's what we heard," said the one on the right.

"The Wheelhouse," said the left.

"We knew the last one," said the right. "Julia Marchetti."

"She left in March," said the left.

"February," said the right.

They looked at each other with the tranquil certainty of two people each holding an incontestably true fact, and then looked back at Maggie.

"I'm Dot," said the one on the right.

"I'm Glad," said the one on the left.

Maggie was not at all confident this was the correct assignment. She bought two cups that matched and went back out into the street.

---

Beryl Foss found her outside the post office.

Maggie knew it was Beryl before the woman spoke, partly because Faye's description had been precise and partly because there was something in how the woman walked — clipboard tucked under one arm, moving at a measured pace with no wasted motion — that announced her as a person who organized things and found the things that needed organizing.

She was perhaps sixty, perhaps a little older, with silver hair kept well back from her face and the kind of posture that takes effort to maintain and becomes, over time, simply what you look like. She wore a jacket the colour of good upholstery.

"You'll be the new one," she said, stopping in front of Maggie.

"Maggie Quill," Maggie said. "The Wheelhouse."

"Beryl Foss." She didn't offer her hand; she didn't not offer it. She was simply gathering data, and Maggie had the sense that Beryl was very good at it. "How are you finding us?"

"Still finding," Maggie said.

Beryl nodded, as if this was reasonable, or at least honest. "It takes a while. People think a small town is simpler." She paused. "It's not simpler. It's just smaller."

"I'm beginning to see that."

"Good." Beryl looked at her for a moment — not unfriendly, not quite friendly, the look of a woman who was noting a data point for later. "You'll want to come to the CWA morning tea. Third Thursday. I'll leave a note with the details." It was not framed as an invitation. It was framed as an administrative action that would occur.

"Thank you," Maggie said.

"Don't mention it." Beryl shifted the clipboard. "Any problems settling in, you can speak to Roz Hennessy at the council. She handles newcomers." She paused. "Officially."

The word *officially* sat there for a moment, doing something.

"Right," Maggie said.

Beryl Foss took two steps and then stopped, as if she had just remembered something, though Maggie had the impression that Beryl Foss never just remembered things. She turned back with the precision of someone performing a movement she had rehearsed.

"We'll see how you go," she said.

She said it pleasantly. She meant something by it. She moved on down the street with her clipboard before Maggie could decide what.

---

Maggie stood outside the post office with her paper bag of op-shop cups and watched Beryl Foss round the corner and disappear.

*We'll see how you go.*

She turned it over on the walk back to the studio. It was a phrase that could mean *I hope you do well* and it was a phrase that could mean *I will be watching to determine whether you do well* and it was a phrase that could mean *the jury is still out and I am the jury*, and she could not tell, from the delivery, which of these it was, or if it was all three at once.

By the time she got back to the studio the afternoon light had gone flat and grey and the beach was invisible from the street. She put the cups on the workbench and stood for a moment looking at the clay she'd left covered with a damp cloth since yesterday. Day two. She hadn't touched it.

She thought about Faye's voice filling the kitchen, warm and quick. She thought about Cobber at the tideline, still there at noon, going nowhere. She thought about two identical faces watching her from behind donated crockery.

She had come here to be anonymous, or as close to it as she could get at forty-three, with a studio on the main street and her name in the lease and her kiln showing in the window. She had intended to be a surface without much depth to read.

She was already someone people knew about.

She picked up the damp cloth and pulled it back from the clay.

*We'll see how you go.*

The clay was cold. She pressed her palms into it and began.
