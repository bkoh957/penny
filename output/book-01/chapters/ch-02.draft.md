# Chapter Two

The gulls woke her at six, claws on the roof iron, and then a ute passed below the window too slowly, as though the driver were checking whether the new tenant had survived the night. The sea was not a view from upstairs but a low pressure in the walls, a heartbeat the house had not told her about yesterday.

She came downstairs into morning light and found The Wheelhouse altered. The shelves Cal had hung were furniture now — three runs of timber holding the safe stock she had arranged and rearranged until her hands stopped asking for instructions. Sawdust lingered by the skirting board. Glaze had installed himself on the good apron overnight with the air of a tenant whose lease had been notarised in his sleep.

She wiped the counter, moved the mugs three times, wrote price tags in her neatest hand and then re-wrote them because the neatness itself looked anxious. The damp salt on the front glass blurred the street into pale movement, and each time she looked up she had the irrational sense that the town had pressed its face to the window and was waiting to see what she would do next. She put two of her stronger bowls on the middle shelf, then took them down again and replaced them with the safe blue mugs, then reversed that decision because the safe blue mugs looked like an apology.

She checked the kiln though nothing had changed — elements intact, door seal good, kiln wash clean. She checked it because competence was a thing you did, and doing it calmed the pitch in her sternum that said she did not know when the bakery opened or how to be new without being conspicuously new.

She practised "Good morning" in the empty room, heard it land with the brisk false warmth of a corporate icebreaker, and abandoned it.

---

The knock came at nine — three soft raps and a fourth, as though the fourth were a correction. She opened the door to a woman holding a loaf of sourdough the way other people held babies.

"Faye Denton. I run the bakery. This is a good loaf but it's not a patient one — you'll want it today." She looked Maggie over with frank, unembarrassed interest, the loaf cradled against an apron dusted to the elbow with flour. "You're the potter."

"Maggie Quill."

"I know." Faye said it kindly, the way a person tells you the weather. "Are you going to let me in, or are we doing this on the step? I've left Tania minding the ovens and Tania panics."

By the time the kettle had boiled, Faye had established herself at the workbench as though she had always sat there, broken the loaf with her hands, and begun to hand Maggie the town. The bread was warm enough to fog its paper, and the crumbs landed on the freshly wiped counter with the casual precision of something that meant to undo what Maggie had done.

The names came in a particular order, and Maggie understood after a while that the order was not random. Faye was deciding what Maggie needed first. Dot and Glad ran the op-shop and between them saw everything that moved on the street and agreed on none of it — "Never ask them a factual question unless you want three answers and a fight." There was Cobber, who lived on the beach and had been about to leave town since before Faye could remember. Beryl Foss ran the CWA and, by extension, the moral weather — "She'll find you. Don't go looking. And be straight with her — she can smell a person managing her from across a hall." There was Saffron with the wellness retreat up past the headland, and Mara who did the dune work, and Mary Burrell, who Maggie would like, Faye said in a softer register, a woman who had brought up half the town's strays and who would probably turn up with something from her garden.

"Burrell," Maggie said. "Like Cal."

"His aunt." Faye's mouth did something amused. "You've met Cal, then."

"He hung my shelves."

"Of course he did." Whatever the tone meant, it did not explain itself, and Maggie had learned long ago that the fastest way to lose information was to chase it. She let it sit.

And there was Iris Poole, who did the jam and the preserves at the markets, and who Maggie should — here Faye paused.

It was a small pause. If Maggie had not spent her working life reading the half-second between a question and its answer, she would have missed it. Faye's face did not fall, exactly. It closed. It went smooth and pleasant and shut, the warmth still sitting on the surface like oil on water and nothing underneath it.

"Iris does very good jam," Faye said. "Everyone says so."

Then she reached for another piece of bread and the subject was sealed over. Maggie watched the seam close and decided not to put a finger in it. She was a guest in someone's town and someone's morning, and there are things you do not ask the person who has just handed you bread. She filed Iris Poole away — a thing she had noticed and would keep.

---

"Right," Faye said, brushing crumbs onto Maggie's clean floor without noticing. "Come and walk. You can't learn a town sitting in it."

Maggie almost said she should stay in case a customer came, which was ridiculous on several levels and would have sounded, even to her, like a woman waiting beside a cake she had baked for guests who had not been invited. Faye only looked at the untouched apron, the locked till, the cat blinking among the canvas folds, and the row of bowls that had survived three arrangements and still appeared to be holding their breath.

"They'll come when they come," Faye said. "And if Dot and Glad see you leave with me, the town will know you're open before you get back. Cheaper than advertising."

At the bakery — Faye's own — the doorbell rang and the room was already a social machine in motion. Beryl Foss was holding court near the sourdough racks, a clipboard under one arm, redirecting a query about autumn market rosters at a man in tradie boots who had come in for a pie and found himself volunteered. She spotted Faye, spotted Maggie, and turned the full beam of her attention on them.

"New potter," Beryl said. Not a question.

"That's right," Faye said.

"I'll come by your shop this week. We'll have a proper talk." A summons delivered as a courtesy. Maggie nearly said "I look forward to it" — the HR reflex — and caught herself. "I'll be here," she said, which was at least true.

Faye gave the smallest approving tilt of the head, no more than a baker might give a loaf that had risen properly. Maggie felt absurdly grateful for it and immediately disliked herself for needing the approval. Beryl, meanwhile, had already redirected the tradie with the pie towards the surf-club working bee, extracted a promise from a woman buying rye that the raffle tickets would be dropped at the hall, and noticed, without apparently looking, that a schoolboy had pocketed two extra jam drops from the sample plate. The bakery was not a shop. It was a parliament with cinnamon scrolls.

Outside the op-shop, two elderly women were seated behind a table of secondhand books. One of them — Dot — leaned forward and pointed at Maggie's frontage.

"That the old bait shop?"

"Bait-and-tackle," Glad corrected, without looking up from her crossword. "And the sign said 'bait and supplies,' not just bait."

"It said 'bait and regret,'" Dot said. "I saw that sign every day for thirty years."

"You saw it on Tuesdays. I saw it every day."

Faye leaned in. "Split the difference."

Cobber was on the footpath outside the post office, barefoot despite the autumn chill, holding a paper cup of tea and looking at the sky as though it owed him an explanation.

"Morning, Cobber," Faye said.

"Faye." He looked at Maggie with mild, waterlogged interest. "You're the one in the wheelhouse."

"I am."

"Heard the kiln was a good one." He sipped his tea. "Tide's turning tomorrow. You'll see it from your window if you're up for it."

Maggie thanked him, and by the time she had parsed whether this was useful, friendly, or simply a statement about water, Cobber had turned his attention back to the sky.

Faye steered her towards the foreshore gap between buildings, where the bay opened flat and pewter under low cloud. "I'll tell you what," she said. "People are already asking about you. I'll send them your way — the ones who'd actually buy a bowl. But not yet. Learn the place first. Don't come at it with a story about yourself ready-made — the sea-change and the new beginning. They've seen the story. Let them find out who you are slowly, and they'll mind the shop for you when you're crook. Come at them with the brochure and they'll be polite and buy nothing."

The advice should have stung. It did sting, a little, because Maggie had a folder on her laptop called Launch Copy and three versions of a sentence about handmade functional ware inspired by the coast, each worse than the last. But Faye had said it while tucking her hands into her apron pockets against the wind, not as a woman demolishing a pitch but as one trying to spare Maggie the cost of making it in public. That was worse, in the way kindness could be worse: it left no useful resentment to hide behind.

---

Near the foreshore path, where the dunes had been fenced with low posts and orange mesh, a woman in waders and a high-vis vest was crouching over a tray of seedlings, talking to a man in a council-grey polo shirt. Mara — it had to be Mara — was speaking quietly and steadily, the way a person speaks when she has had the same conversation many times and intends to keep having it.

"Not a vacant block," Mara said, pressing a seedling into the sand. "It's a dune system, and it's holding the road up."

The man said something Maggie didn't catch, and then a fragment she did: a name she didn't know, and "expressed interest in the headland," said in the smooth way that meant money was behind it.

"They can express what they like," Mara said, without heat. "It's been here longer than the road and it'll be here after."

Maggie kept walking. It was the local weather of land and money, and a woman new to the place had no part in it. Still, the phrase followed her — expressed interest — as bland as a real-estate brochure and as hungry as a gull at the bins. Mara pressed another seedling into the sand with both thumbs, firming the soil as if it were a promise that could be made to hold.

Further along the path, near the low wall above the jetty, a woman was standing with the composed stillness of someone waiting for a photograph. She wore a linen scarf the colour of wet quartz, a long coat over clean boots, her silver-blonde hair arranged — and "arranged" was the word, not "done." She saw Maggie, smiled, and walked towards her with the practised, unhurried warmth of a person whose welcome was also a performance.

"You must be the potter. I'm Saffron — I run the retreat up past the headland. We've been hoping someone would take that lovely space." Her handshake was warm, her nails impeccable, and her eyes moved once across Maggie's face and then the foreshore behind her, as though cataloguing the light.

"That's kind of you," Maggie said.

"Not at all. We're a small town — we look after our own." The phrase settled like a clean blanket that was also, faintly, a measurement. Maggie was tempted to read her as a type — the polished tree-changer, the wellness brand — and then checked herself. She was new. One scarf and one controlled smile were not evidence.

Faye appeared at Maggie's elbow with the timing of a woman who had been watching from a distance she would never admit to. "Saffron. Good. You've met."

"I was just welcoming Maggie."

"Good." Faye's voice was exactly as warm as it had been all morning, and Maggie noticed it did not get warmer. Saffron nodded, adjusted her scarf against the wind, and walked back the way she had come, unhurried, the coat swinging.

---

Mary Burrell came at four, as Faye had said she would, and brought the garden with her.

By then Maggie had returned to The Wheelhouse with too many names in her head and the peculiar exhaustion that came from being polite before lunch. The shop had held its nerve without her. No customers had come in, unless one counted the smear of a cat nose on the front glass and a damp half-moon of paw print on the counter, and Maggie had just decided this was either failure or mercy when the afternoon light thinned to pewter and the second knock of the day arrived.

There was a knock, gentler than Faye's, and Maggie opened the door to a woman in her sixties holding a terracotta pot in both hands. She was neat in the way of women who have always been neat, grey hair pinned back, a cardigan the colour of wet sand, and in the pot was a young lemon tree, its leaves dark and glossy, a slip of something older grafted at the base. From a basket over her arm she produced a paper bag of lemons, six of them, the deep uneven yellow of fruit that has grown on a tree and not in a box, and tipped them into the chipped blue bowl Maggie had left out for keys.

"Mary Burrell. I won't keep you. I've brought you a tree." She turned the pot a quarter so the best of it faced the light. "It was my father's. He planted the parent the year I was born and it's still going, and I take cuttings off it for people who come to stay." She touched one dark leaf with a fingertip, briefly. "It likes the wind off the water, which most things don't. It'll tell you when you've got it right."

Maggie looked at the small tree and felt something move in her chest she had not planned on. "That's an extraordinary thing to give someone you've just met."

"I give it to people I think might stay." Mary said it lightly. Her eyes, though, were not light; they were doing something careful and steady, the way Cal's had done over the level, and Maggie recognised the family resemblance before she had decided whether to.

"I'm told your nephew hung my shelves."

The carefulness sharpened. It was not unfriendly — it was the opposite: the look of a woman who loved someone and had just heard his name in a new mouth and was working out what the mouth intended by it.

"Cal does good work," Mary said. "He measures everything twice. His father was the same." A small pause, filled with the magpie outside and the sea under it. "He's had a lot of people pass through. People come down here to fix their lives and then they go back to the city, and Cal's the one who builds the shelves they leave behind." She smoothed the front of her cardigan. "I'm not saying that's you. I'm only saying he's a steady sort of person, and steady people remember."

Maggie understood she had been told something, and that the gentleness was the point. "I'll take good care of the tree," she said, which was the only true thing she had.

"I know you will." And Mary smiled, and it reached all the way up, warm as the bread had been warm, and for a moment there was nothing in the room but kindness. Glaze, who had been observing the cutting from the workbench with the professional concern of a compliance officer, batted one paw at a low leaf and was gently rebuffed by Mary's hand. She gathered her basket, moved to the door, and turned back with her hand on the frame.

"How long are you thinking you'll stay?" Mary asked, pleasantly, looking at the tree and not at Maggie. "With the studio, I mean."

The lemons sat in their chipped blue bowl, golden in the failing light, and Maggie found she did not have an answer ready.

---

After Mary left, Maggie set the lemon cutting on the sill where the afternoon light would reach it, moved it away from Glaze, watered it too much, and tipped a little out. The shop was warmer now with evidence of other people in it: sourdough crumbs, new names turning over, Mary's lemons golden in the bowl, Glaze reclaiming the apron. The town had entered the room before customers had.

She reviewed the day through sensory anchors: Faye's pause at Iris Poole; Dot and Glad's competing histories; Cobber's tide forecast; Beryl's clipboard; Saffron's scarf; Mara's seedlings pressed into sand; Mary's too-careful kindness and her question, still hanging.

*You always did need a room to approve of you before you could breathe.* Tommy's voice, not called, not welcome, just there. She steadied herself through the physical gift: soil under her nail, lemon oil on her fingers, the weight of the bowl, the leaf turning towards the light.

She had not solved her life. She had a foothold, a friend-shaped possibility in Faye, and a warning she could not yet translate. She also had, if she was honest, the first outline of a use for The Wheelhouse that was not in any business plan: people would come here with cracked handles, birthday commissions, gossip folded into tissue paper, and questions they pretended were about bowls. If she listened before she sold, perhaps she would learn the shape of the place by the marks it left on the counter.

The welcome was conditional, watched, and threaded with histories she had not earned the right to know. The sea darkened behind the salt-hazed glass, and Maggie did not close the door.
