# Chapter 04 — Everybody Hates the Doctor for a Reason

The bowls existed only in graphite so far, eleven of them ranked down the margin of her sketchbook in ascending order of nerve, and Maggie had been looking at the eleventh long enough to be sure it was the one. It was the simplest. A low, wide form with a rim that turned out a fraction at the last moment, the way a wave does before it decides not to break — generous without announcing it. A hundred of them, one for each year the surf club had been pulling people out of the Southern Ocean, and her own name pressed into the foot of every single one. She had not let herself say the word legitimate out loud, because saying it would make the wanting visible, but the word was there on the bench beside the pencil, and it had been there since Beryl Foss telephoned.

She was rubbing out the foot ring for the fourth time when the bell over the door went and Cal Burrell came in with a spirit level under his arm and the particular silence of a man who has noticed a fault and intends to correct it before he speaks.

"Top shelf's bowed," he said.

It was not a greeting. He set the level against the run of shelving he had fitted three weeks ago, the shelving she had been loading with greenware and had thought perfectly fine, and the little bubble drifted off-centre with what she could only describe as personal disappointment. He looked at it the way she imagined a surgeon looks at a wound he is annoyed to have left — not at her, at the work.

"It's holding fine," Maggie said.

"It's holding now." He was already unshouldering his bag. "Load it wet, it'll dish. Then it cracks the pots, and you blame the kiln."

He measured it twice. She had watched him do this in the first week and assumed it was for her benefit, a tradesman performing thoroughness, and had since understood that the second measurement was for him, and that he would have done it in an empty room. He shimmed the bracket, drove two screws she had not known were missing, and stood the level back up. The bubble settled into the middle and stayed there with the smugness of something that had always been right.

Then, without remark, he crossed to the glaze cupboard, took the door off its lower hinge, and reset it. She had not mentioned the door. She had stopped noticing the door months ago, the way you stop noticing a sound that has decided to live in your house — it dropped half an inch and dragged on the boards, and she had simply learned to lift it as she opened it, a small daily apology to the building. Now it swung clean and shut on the latch with a click she did not recognise as her own studio's.

"You didn't have to do that one," she said.

"No." He put the screwdriver away. "Door was annoying me."

Glaze the cat, who regarded the cooling kiln as a personal endowment, opened one eye at the carpenter and shut it again, satisfied that the noise had been productive.

It was while Cal was packing the bag that the committee arrived — three of them, in the matching navy polo shirts that the Pelican's Crook Surf Life Saving Club wore to mean business, smelling of sunscreen and the inside of a clubhouse. Dennis Roe led, who had been club captain since before the pier was rebuilt and said everything twice in case the sea took the first attempt. Behind him came a younger woman with a laminated agenda, and a man named Trevor who appeared to be there mainly to nod.

"The centenary bowls," Dennis said, and then, "the bowls, for the centenary. We've had a look at your bits in the window." He turned one of her glazed mugs over in his hand with the gravity of a man valuing it for probate. "We'd like you to do them. All hundred. Club crest on the foot. You're the only potter we've got, so."

She should have been ready for it. She had wanted exactly this for a week. And what came out of her, fast, before the wanting could organise itself into anything resembling a businesswoman, was the price — and she heard herself shave it as she said it, heard herself round it down, not up, to a figure that would cover her clay and her firings and almost nothing of the three hundred hours, a figure pitched low enough that no one in a navy polo shirt could possibly say no, because the thing she could not afford was for them to say no.

Dennis brightened. "That's very reasonable."

"Very reasonable," said Trevor, nodding.

The laminated woman wrote it down before Maggie could hear how reasonable it was, and there were handshakes, and a date, and the smell of sunscreen went out the door, and the bell rang, and Maggie stood in the sudden quiet feeling that something enormous and good had just happened and not understanding why her stomach had dropped.

Cal was looking at the spirit level in his hand as though it had told him something.

"What," she said.

"Nothing."

"You've got a face."

"I've got the face I always have." He zipped the bag. "What's a hundred bowls of work, to you. In hours."

"I don't know. Three hundred. Maybe more, with the seconds I'll lose."

He nodded slowly, the way he had nodded at the bowed shelf. "And you charged them for the clay."

"I charged them a fair price."

"You charged them what makes it easy for them to say yes." He shouldered the bag and looked at her, finally, straight on, which he did not do often and which had the effect of a hand laid flat on a table. "You priced it so they couldn't turn you down. That's not a price. That's an apology."

It was the apology that did it — the precision of it, the way he had reached past the money and put his thumb directly on the thing she had been hiding from herself since the phone rang. She felt the heat come up her neck. The worst of it, the genuinely unforgivable part, was that he was right, and that he had fixed her shelf and her cupboard door without being asked and was now fixing her, and that she was going to have to be grateful for all three.

"I didn't ask," she said.

"No." He went to the door. "You never do. Doesn't make the shelf less bowed."

The bell rang behind him, and he was gone, and she stood there hating the click of the latch he had mended.

She went to the bakery because she always went to the bakery when a thing had happened that she needed talked out of her, and because Faye's was the one room in Pelican's Crook where a newcomer could stand at the counter and be slowly, gloriously informed.

It was mid-morning, the dead hour between the early loaves and the lunch pies, and the place was warm and yeasted and half full of people not buying much. Faye was wiping down the glass with the unhurried thoroughness of a woman who used cleaning as a stage. Beryl Foss had colonised the corner table with a clipboard and a pot of tea, presiding. And there was a knot of others — a fisherman in a fleece gone shapeless with salt, two CWA women, and, by the cake stand, a glamorous stranger in linen the colour of oatmeal, holding a glass jar of something green up to the light as if it were evidence.

"Sourdough's out till twelve," Faye said, before Maggie had got the door shut. "You can have a cheese scone and the latest, love. The latest's free."

The latest, it turned out, was the doctor.

It began gently, the way these things did, with someone mentioning that Dr Hartigan had been short with old Col Maddox down at the co-op — and then it stopped being gentle, because the fisherman in the salt-shapeless fleece put his mug down and said that short was a kind word for it.

"Told me in front of the whole queue," he said. "Whole queue, Faye. That if I wanted to keep coughing like that into my seventies I was doing all the right things, and if I didn't, he could write it down for me so I'd remember. Like I was a kid. Forty years I've pulled crays out of that water and he talks to me like I left the gas on."

"He's blunt," one of the CWA women allowed.

"He's a surgeon's bedside manner on a country GP's wage," said the other. "My sister's boy — well." She looked at her tea. "Neil told him to his face he'd report him to the pharmacist board if the scripts kept walking. Reckons there's a difference between a sore back and a habit. There might be. You don't say it where the post office can hear."

"He told my Geoff nothing," said the first. "Wouldn't give me a word about my own husband's results. His own wife. Said it wasn't mine to know. Wasn't mine to know. I cooked for that man at the hall for fifteen years."

The woman in oatmeal linen set her green jar down. "He has been actively discouraging his patients from coming to me," she said, in the carrying, faintly wounded voice of someone who has rehearsed the grievance in a mirror. "I run a wellness retreat. I offer rest. Sound, breath, magnesium. And he stands in his surgery and tells frightened people it's a waste of their money, as though frightened people are his to manage. As though I'm the danger." She had, Maggie noticed, beautiful hands and a tremor in the smallest finger that the linen could not quite afford to hide.

"That's Saffron," Faye murmured, leaning in over the cloth. "Up at the old guesthouse. Don't get her started, you'll be here for the breathwork."

Maggie said nothing. She was thinking, with a small disloyal warmth she had no intention of voicing in this room, of the man who had sat on the edge of her bed three weeks ago and asked about her migraines without once reaching for the word stress — who had taken her seriously when no doctor in twenty years had bothered to. He had been the first. She did not know how to hold that beside a queue at the co-op and a wife who wasn't allowed to know her own husband's results, and so she held it quietly and let the room go on.

Beryl Foss had not gone on. Beryl had been writing something on her clipboard with great attention and had now stopped writing, the pen held just off the paper, and when the first CWA woman said, comfortably, that of course you couldn't get a thing past Neil where your health was concerned, he'd tell you straight even when you'd rather he didn't — Beryl's face did something brief and shuttered, and she said, "Some of us would prefer our business stayed our business," in a voice that closed the subject like a door, and went back to her clipboard, and her hand was not quite steady.

"Say what you like." It was a younger woman by the till, defending him out of some reflex of fairness. "He's a good doctor. He'd do anything for a patient. Anything. You can't take that off him."

That was when Maggie noticed Mary Burrell at the end of the counter.

She was in her sixties, broad and capable, with the kind of face that had been kind for a long time and was holding very still now — a woman who had been buttering a teacake and had simply stopped, the knife laid down flat, both hands pressed to the counter either side of the plate as though she needed it to keep from tipping. Maggie had not seen her come in. She had the stillness Maggie had spent twenty years learning to read across a meeting table, the stillness of a person who has heard something land in a place no one else in the room can see.

Faye followed her look. "Leave Mary be, love," she said, low. "She's not — she's been a bit off lately."

Maggie remembered the lemon-tree cutting, the chipped blue bowl, the careful way Mary had watched her when Cal's name came up.

"He'd do anything for a patient," the young woman said again, warming to it, pleased to be the one being generous.

And Mary Burrell said, without turning, in a voice that was perfectly even and that the evenness made worse: "He always believed he knew what was best for people."

The room took it as agreement. Someone said mm, and someone said that's true enough, and Faye refilled a pot, and the talk slid on to football and whether the club would get its new boat before summer.

But Maggie stood with her cheese scone going cold in her hand and watched Mary pick the knife back up and finish buttering the teacake with enormous care, and she could not make the words fit the size of what had come off them — because there had been something in *he knew what was best* far older and far angrier than a queue at the co-op or a script that walked, something that had nothing to do with the doctor everybody in the room had just spent the morning hating, and everything to do with a thing this woman had decided about him a long time before today.
