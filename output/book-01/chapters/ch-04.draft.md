# Chapter Four

The envelope arrived on a Friday, hand-delivered by a man Maggie had never met. He was in his sixties, built like a retired lifesaver, which turned out to be exactly what he was. The envelope was grease-stained brown, folded over twice, and smelled of the pub kitchen.

"Wally Papadimitriou," the man said, leaning on the door frame. "Club secretary. You're the pottery lady?"

"Obviously."

He looked at her for a moment and she immediately regretted her tone — no one had warned the surf club secretary that sarcasm was Maggie's nervous mode. But Wally smiled, the wide grin of a man who judged the surf carnival and so had met worse than her.

"We're after twelve bowls," he said. "Centenary next year. Big do. We want something local. Not imported, not stapled out of a catalogue. Something with the town's name on it that someone's actually made with their hands. Dot from the op-shop said you were worth a try."

Maggie took the envelope. Inside was a single sheet: *Pelican's Crook Surf Life Saving Club — Centenary 1925-2025 — Commemorative Presentation Bowls (12) — Design TBC — Budget: modest — Deadline: flexible but not too flexible.* At the bottom, in ballpoint, Wally had added: *Happy to come past and talk shapes. Wednesday ok?* Wally's handwriting was broad and round and unhurried.

She read the sheet twice. Twelve bowls. The town's name on them. Someone's hands — her hands.

She had not had a piece of work that asked her to be a specific person in a specific place since the divorce. The bowls from the first week had been practice — the work of a woman teaching herself to begin again. This was different. This was a request, from a town, directed at her. She stood at the workbench with the sheet in both hands and something shifted in her chest that she could not have named and did not try to.

---

She fired the test batch on Monday.

The kiln was an old oval kiln she had inherited with the lease — not programmable, a manual dial and a pyrometer that read optimism as accurately as temperature. She had loaded three test bowls the night before, each thrown to the same centenary weight, each glazed with the teal she had opened the week before, diluted to a test ratio in a yoghurt container.

She ramped the kiln at five in the morning. By seven the studio was warm and the pyrometer read 1040 and climbing. She made tea and waited and tried not to hover and hovered anyway.

The first bowl came out of the kiln warped. Not ruined — warped, which was worse. The foot had slumped a quarter-inch, canting the rim out of true. She set it on the bench and looked at it and understood the cause: she had thrown the base too thin and the stoneware couldn't hold its own weight at peak. This was a clay problem. This was her problem. She had rushed the foot.

The second came out cracked. A clean, straight thermal shock crack running from rim to base — the fault of her own impatience, lifting the piece while it was still too warm, the difference between the bench and the kiln too sudden. She had known better and she had done it anyway. She set the cracked bowl beside the warped one and looked at the two of them, side by side, a small gallery of preventable failures.

The third bowl she left in the kiln until two in the afternoon. She drank three cups of tea. Glaze sat on the warm lid and watched her with the tolerant boredom of a cat who has seen humans wait for worse things.

At two she opened the lid. The bowl was upright on the shelf, intact, the teal pooled in the base and breaking to a thinner green at the rim where the glaze had run. The colour was not the deep teal of the wet test sample — the kiln had taken it lighter, which was the kiln's prerogative, and the lighter colour was better. It was the green-blue of the sea on a day when the cloud is high and the wind drops and the water goes honest. She had not made that colour. The kiln had made that colour, and she had let it, and that was the thing she had forgotten — that firing is collaboration, and collaboration means allowing the heat to be a partner.

She set the third bowl on the bench. It sat between the two failures, perfectly level, the rim true, and she sat on her stool and looked at the three of them in a row and felt a small, definite click in her sternum, the click of something coming into focus that had been out of focus for a long time.

---

Cal arrived at four, a day early. He knocked, which was unnecessary because the door was open and she could see his ute from the bench, but Cal knocked.

"Got your shelf," he said. He was carrying a length of Oregon, dressed and eased, the varnish still tacky at the ends. He set it against the wall and pulled out his level.

"I didn't ask for a shelf."

"No." He paused. "You forgot to ask for this one. I noticed the gap when I was hanging the others."

There was a gap. She had been ignoring it for a week, because the gap was at the height she couldn't decide — above the glaze shelves or below, display height or storage — and deciding would have meant committing to a purpose for the wall that she had not committed to yet. Cal had noticed the gap and decided for her. She should have minded. She minded rather less than she should have.

"I was thinking display height," she said.

He measured. He marked. He held the level against the wall and waited, which was when she saw it.

"That's too high," she said.

He looked at the mark, then at her. "It's standard."

"It's too high for me. I'll need a chair every time I want to reach a bowl."

"You'll want the bowls at eye-line. People looking at them don't bend down. You're thinking like a maker, not a seller."

He said it without rudeness. He said it the way he said everything — as a fact he had measured and was prepared to defend — and she found herself annoyed not by the observation but by the fact that he was probably right and she did not want to concede it.

"Halfway," she said.

"Halfway isn't a measurement."

"It is if we both move."

He looked at her for a beat. The level stayed against the wall. She watched him decide something, and she was not entirely sure the something was about the shelf.

"A hair lower," he said.

"A hair and a half."

"That's not a unit."

"It's a negotiation."

He marked the wall. She watched him mark it and she watched him measure twice, and the measuring twice was the thing — the same thing she had seen on the first day, the patient redundancy of a man who checks because checking is a form of care, and she understood that the shelf height was not a shelf height, and she did not say so, and he did not say so, and the wall was marked at a compromise that was neither display nor storage but the precise height at which two people had met.

---

Faye blew in at noon on Tuesday, trailing flour and urgency and the particular glow of a woman with a campaign.

"He's here," she said. "The judge. He's at the pub. He had the lentil soup and he didn't finish it, which means either he's watching his weight or he's already disappointed. Lentil's a bad sign. Lentil means he's saving himself for the pies, and if he's saving himself for the pies he's taking the show seriously, and if he's taking it seriously I'm in trouble."

"Your jam is good, Faye."

"My jam is good. My jam has been good for twenty-two years. My jam is consistently, frustratingly good. Good does not win. Good is what you lose to." She leaned on the counter. "Last year he gave me a ninety-two. Ninety-two! Who gives a ninety-two? You give a ninety-one or a ninety-three. A ninety-two is a man who's already decided but doesn't want to tell you."

"Have you tasted the competition?"

"There is no competition. There's Bev McAllister from Camperdown, who puts ginger in hers, and ginger is not a jam decision, it's a personality statement." Faye pulled a jar from her bag and set it on the counter with the authority of a woman laying evidence before a court. "Apricot. This year's. Tell me it's not a ninety-two."

Maggie opened the jar. The apricot was a deep amber, the set firm but not stiff. She tasted it on her finger. It was clean, bright, the fruit ahead of the sugar, with a faint resinous note from the kernels that told you someone had paid attention.

"That's not a ninety-two," she said.

"Thank you." Faye took the jar back. "You're now my tasting panel. Don't tell anyone."

Faye left, briefly victorious, and Maggie stood at the bench with the taste of apricot jam on her finger and the pleasant realisation that she was now inside something — a feud, a panel, a small private conspiracy — and had not had to apply.

---

Saffron came through at three.

She did not so much enter as materialise, trailing a scent that was sandalwood and something greener, more medicinal, the smell of a certain kind of aspiration. She was tall, sun-bronzed, dressed in the loose layers of someone who has decided that weather is a lifestyle. She carried a stack of glossy flyers.

"Hello, Maggie." She placed a flyer on the counter with the ceremony of someone laying a tarot card. "I wanted to invite you personally. We're running a Soul-Sync weekend. It's about aligning your internal frequency with the coastal rhythm. The early-bird rate ends Friday."

The flyer was expensive. Thick card, good design. *Soul-Sync: Find Your Frequency.* Maggie looked at it and then looked at Saffron, who was smiling with the particular steadiness of a woman who has practised the smile in a mirror and is now committed to it.

"I'll think about it," Maggie said, which was what she said when she would not.

"You should. Your energy feels a little discordant today." Saffron tilted her head. "Like you're carrying something that doesn't belong to you."

The observation landed closer than Maggie expected. She had a flash of the migraine two days ago — the tea towel, the light under the door, Neil's voice — and the fact that Saffron, who did not know her, had arrived at something adjacent to true by means of a wellness flyer and a scent. It was the same move Maggie had seen in HR for twenty years: a person reading the room and calling the reading intuition.

"Thanks, Saffron."

Saffron glided out. The flyer sat on the counter. Glaze jumped up, sniffed it, and sat on it. Maggie looked at the cat and the flyer and let the cat win.

---

She found out about Neil twice, both times without asking.

The first was at the bakery, when she went to return Faye's jar. Cobber was on the bench outside, eating a pie, and Faye was pouring him a second tea.

"Cobber messed his shoulder up surfing at sixty," Faye said, as Maggie sat down. "Sixty! I ask you. Neil stitched him up in the surgery on a Saturday morning and didn't charge him. Said Cobber'd done more for the town's morale than the town had ever done for him."

"He wasn't wrong," Cobber said, mildly, around the pie.

The second was at the op-shop, when she went in for newspaper to pack test bowls. Dot and Glad were both behind the counter, in the particular suspension of activities that meant a customer was being assessed.

"Mrs Papadimitriou's still walking," Dot said, apropos of nothing, to Glad.

"Still walking," Glad agreed.

"Because of Neil," Dot said.

"Because of Neil," Glad agreed.

It was said with the weight of a fact that had been said many times and would be said many more, a fact that required no elaboration because everyone in Pelican's Crook knew the story of Mrs Papadimitriou's hip and Neil's refusal to let her leave it alone.

Maggie paid for her newspaper and left. Walking back along the main street, the water showing itself at the end of each cross-street, she thought about Neil Hartigan. Not as a doctor — as a fact the town had agreed on. He had fixed Cobber's shoulder on a Saturday and hadn't charged. He had kept Mrs Papadimitriou walking. The town mentioned him the way it mentioned the water or the wind — as a condition of the place, something you stopped noticing because it was always there.

She thought about him, and about the notebook by her cot, and about the three words he had said that she was still carrying. *You're not mad.* She had not used the word tool. She had not decided the migraine sight was anything other than what it was — a thing that rode in on the pain and showed her more than she wanted and did not explain itself. But Neil had believed it was real, and the belief itself sat in her like the warmth of the third bowl sitting on the bench — not an answer, but the fact that an answer might exist.

---

That evening she sat at the workbench with the three test bowls and Wally's letter. She had pinned the letter above the wheel. She had opened her notebook to a blank page and written the date, which was a small thing, but it was also, she understood, a beginning.

She would throw twelve bowls. She would fire them and ruin some and fire them again. She would give them to a surf club that had been on this beach for a hundred years, and they would put the town's name on them, and the bowls would be hers, and this was the thing she had driven six hours to find, and she had found it, and the finding did not require a migraine or a gift or anything other than her hands and a kiln and the willingness to let the heat be a partner.

Glaze settled on the warm lid of the kiln. The evening light came flat through the window, salt-hazed, and the third bowl caught it and held it, and Maggie looked at the bowl and the light and the letter and she thought: *this.*

Just that. *This.*
