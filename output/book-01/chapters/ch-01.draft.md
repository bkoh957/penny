The hatchback smelled of iron oxide, old cardboard, damp canvas, and clay dust — the smell of seven years spent throwing pots in a Brunswick kitchen and calling it a hobby so that nobody, least of all herself, would have to take it seriously. Everything she had chosen to keep from the old life rode with her: three crates of glazed seconds swaddled in newspaper, a wheel cover washed so often it had forgotten what colour it began as, a box of tools that rattled in the boot on every bend. What she had refused to keep was absent, and the absences took up more room than the things.

Brunswick had fallen away, then Geelong, and the road had narrowed into the real thing — coast on one side, cliff and wet eucalypts on the other, gulls riding the updraughts. Brown signs promised her a view every few kilometres, and she stopped at none of them, because she had learned that if she stopped too often she gave herself the chance to turn the car around, and the car, like Maggie, was not to be trusted with chances.

Still, the coast gave pleasure. The sea flashed silver between the tree trunks, the Otways crowded down to the road as if leaning in to read it, and the fern gullies sat dark and dripping from the night's rain. Salt came through the vents. She was frightened, and also awake in a way she had not been for years.

She had not chosen *a coastal town*. She had chosen this one, four years ago, when she had come through on an arts-trail assignment and stood too long in the doorway of one particular studio, held there by the way the afternoon light moved across a shelf of glazed bowls. She had been married then, and sensible, and good at filing inconvenient desires under *later*. She had gone home. But after the divorce, when she sat with a laptop and a list of practical coastal listings, every other town read like reinvention performed for an audience, and Pelican's Crook read like a room she already knew. A real empty studio, a kiln already in place, a flat upstairs so she could live over her own work. The lease was a risk the settlement could just absorb; the off-season meant she could begin before anyone expected her to be any good.

She tried the decision on in its various sizes. *Sea-change*, if you said it like a real estate agent. *Breakdown*, if you said it like Tommy. *Artist's retreat*, if you said it in linen trousers with old money behind you. Or, in her own words: a woman finally driving towards the life she should have noticed years sooner. Twenty years of HR had taught her to manage everyone else's exits; the marriage had taught her to take up less space each year; the pottery was the thing she came back to for proof her hands were still her own.

She let herself feel proud of it, just for a moment, the lease signed and the town an hour off.

*A shop. With your savings. Christ, Mags, you do know hobbies are meant to cost less than a mortgage.*

She flinched. She kept driving. She turned the radio up, then turned it off again, because drowning herself out was a thing she had done for two decades and had promised, somewhere around Geelong, to stop.

The road came round a shoulder of hill and the bay simply opened, tucked into a crook of the coast with a headland curled around the water like an arm thrown over something sleeping. Pelican's Crook. It was a name that managed to sound like a bird, an illness, and a minor crime all at once, and she decided this was either an excellent omen or very bad branding.

She came in at ten past two, and the off-season showed everywhere. The bakery awning was tied back, the post office advertised reduced winter hours, and a man walked two wet dogs along the foreshore. Hardware shop. Two quiet pubs. A general store that might also be the op-shop. Gulls owned the car park. The main street ran parallel to the bay, the sea showing grey-green in the gaps between buildings, and a single white shape stood on the headland that she chose not to examine too closely yet.

She drove the length of the street once before parking, telling herself it was to learn where everything was, which was a lie. She was too new to find the studio on the first pass and too proud to perform a three-point turn in front of an entire town. *Stakeholder reconnaissance*, said the part of her brain that had survived twenty years of meetings, and she banned the phrase before it could settle. Nobody waved; nobody knew her yet to wave at. One woman in a doorway looked at the loaded hatchback, then looked away, and Maggie was relieved and disappointed in exactly equal measure.

Then the building came up on her left, and her chest did something it had no business doing. The Wheelhouse: salt-hazed glass, a weathered timber frontage that still carried the bones of its life as a bait-and-tackle shop, living quarters sitting modest above the work below. Not twee. Not a postcard. A working room that had been waiting. She had stood in that doorway four years ago and thought, with an embarrassment that stayed with her for years, *if I had a life, it would look something like this.* She had laughed at herself then; she had stopped laughing on the drive down.

The key stuck, then gave. The door dragged on its bottom edge and the smell came out to meet her — salt, cold, the mineral ghost of old clay, timber shut up and breathing slowly to itself. She stood inside and did not work. She let herself feel the size of the relief: that here, no one had yet asked her to be reasonable. Her own door. Her own kiln. Her own future mess.

*You always did like the idea of things more than the work, Mags.*

That one she felt under the ribs, because it went straight for the fear she had told no one: that all of this was a performance of a vocation rather than the thing itself. She did not answer it. She propped the door, opened the high windows, and went to the kiln.

It was better than the photographs had promised. The elements looked sound, the door seal held a sheet of paper when she tested it, and someone had left a tub of kiln wash not entirely dried out. She checked it the way she had been taught, and competence moved through her hands like warmth returning to a cold limb.

She started unpacking, and noticed which crate she reached for first. The seconds. The half-glazed, the inoffensive, the bowls a person could buy without having to feel anything. *Approachable*, she told herself. *Starter stock.* Underneath sat the real arrangement: if the town turned its nose up, at least it would not be at her best. The HR binders stayed in the boot. She had brought them because throwing away twenty years of competence felt like tempting fate, but she would not carry them inside. A boundary had to start somewhere, and it could start at the kerb.

The ute announced itself before it arrived, an engine clearing its throat like a boat. The note had said only *Here at 3. C.*, and from two words and a clock she had, the previous evening, built an entire person and decided she liked him, which she knew was ridiculous and did anyway. He was tall, in a canvas work jacket gone soft at the elbows, a spirit level riding in one pocket, and he came into the studio with the stillness of a man who measured things for a living.

"Cal Burrell," he said.

"Maggie Quill." She had prepared three versions of hello and produced the one that sounded like two parties exchanging evidence at a border checkpoint.

That was when the Too-Much came on. It was the name she had given it years ago, for want of a better one — the way the world stopped offering her a tidy summary and handed her everything at once instead, unranked and urgent, more than any one mind was built to carry. Not a migraine, not yet; the warning flicker of one, a taste of metal at the back of her teeth, the gulls outside arriving with one too many edges. The details came in a flood, and none of them waited their turn. Short pale dog hair caught in the weave of his cuff — a working dog, kept close. The twice-over smell of coffee on him, a second cup taken late and black. Sawdust in the crease of his watchband. A callus worn smooth where a pencil rode behind one ear. Boots resoled rather than replaced, a shallow new cut along one thumb. It all flew past too fast to keep, the way it always did — and then one detail snagged and would not let go, the way one always did. No wedding ring. No pale band where one had ever been. Single, and not freshly so.

He looked once at the wrapped piece she had not yet shelved, and once at the seconds, and seemed to draw a conclusion from the gap between them. That was enough. She made herself stop. Cal Burrell was not an employee, not a candidate, not a grievance file, not a husband to be decoded before he could do damage. She did not want to read him. She knew too well what it was like to be judged on the smallest details. The trouble with nobly declining to read someone is that it takes all your attention, and the actual conversation runs on without you.

"Three exactly," she managed. "Should I be impressed, or worried?"

"Neither." He set the level on the windowsill. "Clock in the ute works."

"Good. I was hoping for reliable rather than ominous."

"Ominous costs extra," he said, to the wall, and went to look at the east side of it as though the wall had requested him personally.

She tried to extend the warmth — coffee, the names of the good beaches, some joke about every shelf in town hiding a secret — and watched it die against his back. He was not unkind. He simply declined the performance, having watched any number of arrivals romanticise the place and leave by Easter. "Where do you want the shelves," he said, and it was not a question, and the flirtation expired down by the skirting board while she recovered the only way she knew, which was to become useful.

He worked. She unpacked around him, and his talk was all third bracket and clearance and airflow and grain, and he measured everything twice, and she liked the quiet of it and resented liking it. She set the seconds where the new shelves would carry them and left the stronger pieces wrapped — the ones with an actual point of view, the ones that frightened her.

He noticed. He did not make a speech of it.

"Those the ones you're selling?"

"Those are the approachable ones."

"Approachable's not the same as good."

It landed too close to the bone, and for half a second she heard Tommy in it — *best not lead with the strange ones, people like to know what they're buying* — until she understood the difference. Tommy had belittled her to keep her small. This man simply refused to flatter her, which was a different injury. She pushed back, because pure defensiveness only ever made her smaller.

"I'm opening a pottery studio, not running an emotional-growth seminar."

"Shelves are cheaper than those."

"You've priced both?"

"Only shelves." He sank a screw. "Emotional growth tends to go over budget."

He drove the last bracket home and looked at her properly for the first time. "You opening a shop," he said, "or hiding in one?"

She did not answer, because she did not have an answer she was willing to say out loud to a man holding a drill. But when he turned back to the wall she crossed to the wrapped crate and took out a piece she had meant to keep buried — a vase with a crackle glaze that had broken the wrong way and the right way at once — and unwrapped it, and did not put it back. A small thing. Not a cured flaw. Just a vase, set down on a bench, in the light.

He finished as the light began to go, swept up most of the sawdust, and declined her offer of coffee, which relieved her, because she still had no idea where the tea bags were, and annoyed her, because she had wanted him to want to stay. "I'll come back for the upstairs shelves," he said, pulling the door, "whenever you're ready," and there might have been the faintest softness in it, and she made herself not examine it, having lectured herself on that error three hours before.

The ute reversed up the street and the town folded itself towards evening. Low cloud came in off the water, the sea went grey-green and then simply grey, and the bakery across the way stood dark. Salt wind moved through the open door.

A cat walked in as though he had an appointment. A ginger tom with one ear long since surrendered to experience, wearing clay dust across his back like a credential, he proceeded directly to the kiln and inspected it with the unhurried thoroughness of a man conducting a compliance audit. "Findings?" she asked him, and then heard herself, and revised. Not a stakeholder. A landlord, perhaps. Quality assurance, at the very least.

He concluded his inspection, found the premises barely adequate, and registered his verdict by stepping onto her good apron — the proper one, from the Fitzroy class seven years ago, where she first understood that the clay was the actual work and everything else the costume — circling once, and lying down with the finality of ownership. She could move him. She could be sensible. She let him stay.

She had not solved her life. Tommy's voice was still in the car somewhere, Cal had refused to flatter her, the town had not welcomed her, and the new shelves held far too many seconds and one single honest vase. But the room was real. The kiln was real. The cat, regrettably, was real. The sea was just outside, going dark, and she was here, in it, awake.

She left the door open. It was a small act of defiance, and enough to be going on with.
