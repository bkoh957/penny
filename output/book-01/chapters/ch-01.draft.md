# Chapter One

The hatchback smelled of iron oxide and old cardboard, which Maggie had decided was fine. Everything she was keeping from her previous life fit in the boot and the back seat with room left over for a half-bag of grog-proof clay she'd bought on impulse outside Geelong, and she had spent the last three hours telling herself that travelling light was a virtue and not a diagnosis.

The diagnosis she was ignoring was simpler: she was forty-three, she had no job, no marriage, and no particular plan except a lease she'd signed on a Tuesday morning in a state she would later describe as quietly deranged, and the drive from Brunswick had taken six hours because she'd stopped twice — once for petrol, once in a rest stop on the edge of the Otway Ranges to sit with the engine off and watch the eucalypts do nothing in the wind for twenty minutes. The eucalypts had not offered any insight. She had started the car again.

She found Pelican's Crook at ten past two on a Thursday afternoon in late April, when the tourist season had given up and gone home and taken the sunshine with it. The main street ran flat between the water and the escarpment: a post office, a bakery with its awning tied back, a hardware shop, two pubs, and a long gap of nothing much before the road bent inland again. The Great Ocean Road hadn't bothered with a proper sign for the turnoff. You had to know, or you had to be slow enough to notice a hand-painted board leaning against a power pole.

She had been slow enough. Just barely.

She drove the length of the street once before she parked, getting a look at it. The bakery was closed; its window displayed a sign that said CLOSED FOR THE SEASON, MAY–SEPT in hand-lettered cardboard that had been faded by enough seasons that the capital letters had gone pink. A woman in a yellow jacket walked a dog around the corner toward the car park and disappeared without looking up. The water was visible at every gap between buildings — flat and grey-green under the cloud, not dramatic, just very large and very much there, the way the sea is when it decides to be the main fact of a place.

The Wheelhouse was in the middle of the block, ground floor of a narrow terrace that ran upstairs to a flat she hadn't seen yet except in photographs. Large front window, salt-hazed glass, the interior very dark until she got out of the car and pressed her face to it with both hands cupped around her eyes. Bare shelves. Concrete floor. A wheel in the corner under a canvas cover, and at the back, the kiln — larger than she'd expected, good, she'd need that — and the particular quality of emptiness that a room has when it's waiting rather than abandoned.

She stood there for a moment with her breath fogging the glass, rain-smell coming off the ocean behind her, and let herself feel it.

She had been in HR for twenty years, the last decade of them as a director. She had sat across from people at the worst moments of their professional lives and kept her face very still, and she had been good at it — genuinely good at it, which was part of the problem, because the face had worked so well in the conference room that she'd kept it on at dinner and kept it on through the weekend and kept it on right up until the moment she'd understood that the face was all that was left. The rest of her had gone somewhere else, and she hadn't noticed until it had been gone quite a long time.

She'd visited the town once, four years ago, for a regional arts piece she'd been working on. Pottery trail. Eight studios in three days. She remembered the studio she was now standing outside: the way the light came off the water through the salt-haze and landed on thrown pieces on the shelves, and how she'd stood in the doorway for longer than was professionally necessary, held there by the light on the glaze, and not known what to do about it.

She'd filed the feeling away and gone back to Brunswick and not thought about it again until the marriage was over and the filing had, in the way of things, come undone.

Now she was here. It was exactly right in a way that she didn't have words for yet and didn't particularly need.

She unlocked the door. The salt smell was stronger inside, almost animal, the way it gets when a building has been breathing the sea for long enough that it's become part of the walls. She stood in the middle of the empty shop floor and turned around slowly.

The shelves were brackets and boards, pre-cut, stacked flat against the far wall. That was Cal, she assumed — Cal Burrell, the carpenter the real estate agent had described as solid, which Maggie had interpreted as efficient and hoped meant quiet. There was a note on the stack in handwriting that was large, unhurried, and entirely practical: *Here at 3. C.*

Two words. She already liked him.

She used the hour to start unloading the car. Clay tools wrapped in old towels, sorted by type so she could find them: the loop tools and ribbon tools together, the ribs separate, the trimming tools in a canvas roll that had been a birthday present from her sister three years ago — the only birthday present from the last five years she'd actually kept. A box of half-glazed seconds she'd been carting since February because throwing them out felt like admitting the last eight months had been purely bad, and she wasn't ready to do that. The seconds were evidence that she had kept making things even when everything else had been a mess; she would throw them out when she'd made better ones. A portable wedging board. Two rolls of plastic sheeting. A bag of sponges. The spare apron, the everyday one. The good apron, which she folded with some care and set on top of the second box.

The box of invoicing binders she'd packed and then spent the entire drive not opening, because she knew perfectly well there was nothing in them she needed but she hadn't been able to leave them sitting on the kerb in front of the old house.

She left those in the boot.

The kiln was better than the photos had promised. She lifted the canvas and ran her hand around the inside of the door seal, checking for cracks, and found none. She checked the elements — intact, no visible degradation, good contact — and the shelves were stacked nearby with a bag of kiln wash that someone had left, generously, when they'd cleared out. The whole thing smelled of past firings, someone else's work, and she stood with her hand on the cold door for a moment longer than necessary. Other people's clay, other people's hours. The kiln remembered all of it and said nothing.

Cal arrived at three on the dot. She heard the ute before she saw it — a sound like a boat engine clearing its throat — and he pulled up at the kerb and took about ninety seconds to decide how close to park before getting out. He was tall, wearing a canvas work jacket gone soft at the elbows, and he had the particular quality of stillness that belongs to people who spend a lot of time measuring.

"Cal Burrell," he said.

"Maggie Quill." She'd been going back and forth on whether to say *hi* or *hello* or *good to meet you* and had ended up saying nothing at all, which came out fine.

He looked at the shelving note still sitting on the floor where she'd left it, then looked at the brackets, then looked at the wall. He took a small level out of his jacket pocket without making a production of it.

"Where d'you want them?" he said.

She'd thought about this. She showed him with her hands — three runs along the east wall, spaced for tall pieces at the bottom and smaller work up top. He nodded, already measuring.

Over the next hour and a half he said, in no particular order: *the third bracket's an inch off, I'll fix it*, *plugs in here, is that right*, *you'll want clearance at the top for airflow*, and *saw something in the grain, she'll hold fine*. Each sentence arrived after the gap it needed and not before. Maggie moved around him, unpacking, and found that his presence was exactly like having a radio on in another room — aware of it without having to think about it, the absence of pressure a form of relief.

The sawdust came down at five past four, when he was cutting the last board at the portable saw he'd set up on the pavement out front. It drifted in through the open door on the sea breeze and settled on the concrete, and she watched it happen and did not particularly mind. When he was done he swept most of it up with a push broom he'd produced from somewhere, and left the rest. That felt right too.

He packed up with the same economy he'd brought to everything else. She offered coffee and he said he was right, thanks. She was about to say she also had tea bags somewhere in the third box, except she couldn't be sure they hadn't gone in the boot, and she stopped herself before she could say any of that.

She said she'd be calling about the shelves in the upstairs flat eventually.

He said *whenever you're ready* and picked up his toolbox and reversed the ute up the street with the ease of someone who had always reversed up streets and saw no reason to think about it.

She stood in the doorway after he'd gone and watched the water. The cloud sat low over the horizon, a particular grey that she didn't have a word for yet — not threatening, not especially beautiful, just present, the colour of the sea doing its own business. One container ship very far out. A couple of gulls doing nothing in particular over the car park. The town making itself small against the coming winter.

She went back inside.

She'd been going through the second box of tools when the cat walked in. She didn't hear him before he appeared, which she suspected was intentional — he was a large, disreputable-looking ginger tom with one ear that stood straight and one that had given up, and he was wearing clay dust in his fur the way certain men wear flour: so thoroughly that it had simply become part of him. He walked in through the open door as though he'd been here before, which, she supposed, he probably had.

He stopped in the middle of the floor and looked at her with the frank assessment of a feline who has arrived to conduct a property inspection and is mildly underwhelmed by the new management.

"Hello," she said.

He went to look at the kiln. He walked around it twice, applied one paw briefly to the door seal in a gesture she felt was scientific, and then sat down with his tail curled around his feet and continued to look at the kiln with an expression of measured satisfaction.

"That's not yours," she told him.

He turned his head and regarded her briefly, then looked back at the kiln.

She went back to unpacking. After a few minutes she was aware that he had moved, and then she found him sitting on her good apron — the thick canvas one she'd had since the pottery class in Fitzroy, seven years ago, when she'd first walked into a studio and felt the clay do what it was supposed to do under her hands and had understood, in a very quiet way, that she had been doing everything else wrong. The apron was folded on the top of the second box. He had arranged himself on it with the complete commitment of an animal who has already decided.

She looked at him. He looked at her.

She could move him. He was a cat; she could simply pick him up and put him outside, or on the floor, or anywhere that was not her good apron. This was the sensible thing. This was what a person did when they'd just arrived in a new town and were already behind on unpacking and did not need complications.

She picked up the next item from the box — a turning tool, she'd been wondering where that was — and set it on the shelf Cal had just hung.

The cat rearranged himself very slightly on the apron, tucked his chin down, and closed his eyes with an air of finality.

She didn't move him.

The day went quiet around them both. Outside the open door the sea was doing what the sea does, which is to say it was going on without her, as it had been doing her whole life, and would keep doing. The bare shelves waited. The kiln was cold and clean and ready. The sawdust had settled.

She had not come here to solve anything. She had come to make things with her hands and stop having to manage the exits. But standing in her empty, salt-smelling shop in the early autumn dark with a cat of uncertain allegiance sleeping on her good apron, Maggie Quill allowed herself something she hadn't felt in a long time.

Not happiness, not yet. Just the possibility of it.

She did not close the door.
