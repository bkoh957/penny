# Chapter One — The Wheelhouse

The car smelled of her whole life, or the parts of it she had decided to keep. Iron oxide and old cardboard, damp canvas, and the fine grey dust that settles into everything a potter owns and never leaves. It had got into the seatbelt webbing and the creases of her knuckles, and Maggie found she did not mind. What she minded was absent — no invoicing folders on the passenger seat, no lanyard in the door pocket, no managed silences waiting to be smoothed.

Brunswick had fallen away hours ago, and Geelong after that, and then the road gave up any pretence of being sensible and tightened against the coast until the Otways came crowding down to the verge. Wet eucalypts, fern gullies dark with rain, the whole green weight of the hills leaning towards the water as if it too were thinking of leaving. The sea flashed between the trunks in hard silver coins. She passed a sign for a lookout and did not stop; she had stopped at the first three and then made herself a rule, because a woman who stops at every lookout is giving herself chances to turn around.

She had chosen this town. Not *a* coastal town — this one. Four years ago she had come down the Great Ocean Road on a long weekend, married and sensible and filing away every inconvenient want the moment she felt it, and she had wandered into a modest pottery trail as a paying visitor and stood too long in one particular doorway. Light off the water had been moving over a shelf of glazed work, the room built for hands rather than for meetings, and she had thought, with an embarrassment that took her breath, *if I had a life, it would look something like this.* Then she had laughed at herself and gone home to Tommy.

She was not laughing now. Every other listing up and down the coast had felt like reinvention performed for an audience. Pelican's Crook had not. It had a real empty studio with a kiln already standing in it, a main street small enough that a person's work might matter, a flat upstairs to live over, and enough distance from Brunswick that Tommy's version of her could not keep strolling in uninvited. The lease had been just possible on the settlement, terrifyingly just. Twenty years she had spent managing other people's exits; this time she was trying, clumsily, to enter something instead — a woman finally driving towards the life she should have noticed twenty years sooner.

Pride caught her under the ribs, warm and unfamiliar, and the moment it did his voice arrived to meet it.

*A shop. With your savings. Christ, Mags, you do know hobbies are meant to cost less than a mortgage.*

She heard it in his exact register, the reasonable one that had always sounded like it was doing her a favour. Her hands tightened on the wheel. She turned the radio up, and a mile later turned it off again, because she was done drowning herself out to make a room quieter for him.

The road bent one final time and gave her the town all at once. The bay had been folded into a crook of the coast, a headland curling round to hold the water still, the houses tucked along a thin green shelf between sea and steep hills as though someone had put them there carefully, trying not to wake the place. Pelican's Crook. It sounded like a bird, an illness, and a petty crime all at once, and this was either a sign or very poor branding, and possibly, in a town this size, the same thing.

---

She came down into the main street at ten past two, and the off-season was the first local fact she learned. The tourist brightness had been packed away out of the wind. A bakery awning was tied back and dark behind the glass; a couple of shops wore short-hours signs written by hand; a dog walker crossed against no traffic at all. The street ran parallel to the bay, so the sea kept appearing between buildings in flat grey-green panels, the hill rising green and close behind. Two pubs, a post office, a hardware shop with a wall of poly pipe out the front, gulls standing about the car park with the settled boredom of ratepayers.

She drove the length of it once before parking, and knew why. She was new enough to be unsure which turn was hers, proud enough not to perform a three-point turn in front of an entire town, and some HR-trained corner of her brain offered the phrase *stakeholder reconnaissance* and she banned it before it could reach her mouth. Nobody waved; nobody knew her. One man glanced at the loaded hatchback and looked away with the neutrality of a place deciding to wait and see.

Then The Wheelhouse arrived on her left, and everything in her went quiet. It was modest, which she was grateful for — a working room, not a postcard. Salt had hazed the big front window to the softness of sea glass, and the frontage still carried the bones of its bait-and-tackle days in the ghost of old lettering and a hook rail nobody had pulled down. It looked small enough to be ridiculous and solid enough to be real, and she recognised the doorway before she had consciously found it, because it was *the* doorway. This exact one. She could have leased somewhere cheaper, busier, safer; none of those had lodged under her ribs. This one had, and had gone on calling until she ran clean out of excuses not to answer.

---

The front door stuck, the way a door does when a building has been holding its breath. She put her shoulder to it and it gave, and the shut-up room came out to meet her — cold concrete, old timber, salt, and underneath it the mineral memory of clay.

She stood just inside and did not work. She let herself stand on her own floor, where nobody had asked her all day to be reasonable, and the relief was so large it frightened her. Her own door. Her own future mess, unmade, waiting.

The kiln stood at the back, bigger than she had let herself hope; the wheel sat under its canvas cover like a sleeping animal. And on the estate agent's key ring — she felt it before she understood it — one of the split rings was empty. Bright and shiny at the coil where a key had slid off, but empty. The front key had turned; the rear door, when she tried it, swallowed the wrong keys and gave nothing, and its little brass face wore a fresh graphite smear and a bright scrape, the kind a key leaves when it has been used lately and often. She stood with the ring in her palm and made herself notice, rather than catastrophise, that somebody had been through this door more recently than an empty studio had any business being.

Not ominous, she said in plain words — a previous tenant who never handed one back, a caretaker with a spare, a problem the shape of a Tuesday errand. Though the ginger cat sitting beside that same rear door, as though he used it more than she ever would, was going to have to explain himself eventually.

*You always did like the idea of things more than the work, Mags.*

That went straight for the fear she had driven three hours to outrun: that this was a performance, a woman playing potter, a set dressed to look like a vocation. That was the trick of him — he only ever said the thing she already feared, so agreeing felt like honesty. She did not answer it; answering only kept him in the room. Instead she propped the door with a brick, opened the two windows that would open, and let the sea move the shut-up air. She checked the kiln properly, because she was not a helpless newcomer and it steadied her to prove it — elements sound and unbroken, the door seal true when she pressed along it, a film of kiln wash left by whoever had fired here last. Then she found a pencil and wrote *rear key — ask agent / Cal?* on the back of a docket, so the problem would wait somewhere other than her chest.

She began to unpack, and caught herself unpacking wrong. The box she opened first was the seconds — the half-glazed compromises, the safe pieces that apologised for themselves before anyone could. Her good work stayed wrapped at the bottom, because some careful clerk in her head had decided the town had not yet earned it. Then she heard what she was doing. She was treating her own studio like a probation period, auditioning for a job she had already given herself.

She stopped, unwrapped one good thing, and set it on the sill in the salt light. A small bowl, and not pretty in the way a shop window wants pretty. Better than that. The wall came up thin and lifted at the rim where her fingers had asked it to, and the salt-grey glaze she had chased for two years had run and pooled dark in the well and broken pale over the throwing rings, so the whole inside held the record of a hand deciding. Iron had bloomed through at the foot in three small rust freckles, uninvited and perfect. It carried its weight low, where a bowl should. This was the work — not the seconds, this — and she did not put it away again, and that was the whole of her defiance for the afternoon, and it felt enormous. The HR binders stayed in the boot, because throwing away twenty years of competence is hard; she did not bring them inside.

---

At three exactly a ute came down the street and settled at the kerb with an engine that turned over like a boat clearing its throat, and Maggie discovered she had built a small foolish structure of feeling out of a two-word note. *Here at 3. C.* — left on the stack of pre-cut shelving delivered ahead of her. On the strength of two words and their efficient full stop she had decided a number of things about the man attached to them, knew it was ridiculous, and had gone on building it anyway.

He was tall, good-looking in the unbothered way of a man who has never once thought about it — weathered, a canvas work jacket gone soft at the elbows, a small level riding in the breast pocket. He had the stillness of people who measure things for a living.

"Cal Burrell," he said.

"Maggie Quill."

She had prepared three versions of hello and reached for the one that sounded like two agents exchanging evidence at a border checkpoint.

He looked past her into the room — at the wheel, the kiln, the one good bowl on the sill and the open box of seconds beneath it — and she watched him file her. Another sea-change. Another shopfront with a lifestyle behind it and city money underneath.

"Town's had a few of these," he said, not unkindly. "People turn up with the signage sorted before they've met the wind."

It touched a nerve so exactly she nearly gasped, because that was the fear — that her brave true choice would *look* like branding. She bristled, and could not stop that either.

Then the other thing began, the way it always began — a fine sharpening at the edges of things, the light gone a half-shade too clear, a thin taste of metal at the back of her tongue and a small pressure behind her right eye like a thumb pressed to clay. Not a full migraine. A warning flare, at newness and a room being rearranged by a man who had walked in without asking it to.

And it gave her too much. The jacket worn at the elbows because he used his elbows. The boots — good ones, resoled rather than replaced, the stitching of the repair still darker than the leather. His hands as he set his takeaway cup well back from the bench edge without a thought, a knuckle carrying a shallow fresh cut. The flat palm he laid against the east wall, pressing, reading it for what it would bear before he trusted it with anything of hers. The hand that went out and steadied a warped board any other man would have complained about, as though the complaint and the fix were the same motion and only the fix was worth doing.

She could see use, and wear, and repair, and restraint. She could not see one thing about the inside of him, and the Too-Much would have let her pretend otherwise — it always offered the shortcut, a whole verdict for free. She refused it. He was not an employee, not a candidate, not a grievance, not a husband to be decoded before he could hurt her. She was allowed to notice how a man treated his boots. She was not allowed to claim she knew him. She pressed the eye with two fingers and let the shortcut close.

Which meant that while she was busy nobly not reading him, she fumbled the conversation.

"Three exactly," she said, going for light. "Should I be impressed or worried?"

"Neither." He was looking at the wall. "Clock in the ute works."

"Good. I was hoping for reliable rather than ominous."

He let the corner of his mouth reach his voice a full second before it reached his face. "Ominous costs extra."

He understood her — that she registered a beat too late, once the moment had moved on without her. He had understood she was flirting, and chosen to answer the literal words and keep the rest for himself, keeping the ball in play by pretending there was no ball. When she tried to widen it — coffee, could he point her anywhere that did a decent flat white — he did not shut her down. He sidestepped with something useful about the bakery's hours and went back to the wall.

"Where do you want them?" he asked.

The flirtation had not died. He had set it on a shelf she could not reach yet. She recovered the only way she trusted, which was to become competent, and showed him where.

---

He worked, and she unpacked around him, and neither filled the quiet, and she liked the quiet and resented liking it. His dialogue came in single practical units — *third bracket, clearance, airflow, grain* — and he measured everything twice before trusting it once. Three runs went up along the east wall, spaced wide at the bottom for tall pieces and closer above for small work, exactly as she would have asked had she known how. She arranged the seconds along the new boards and left the good work wrapped, because her hands were still deciding that the town had not earned it. He noticed. Noticing was his trade too.

"Those the ones you're selling?" he said, not looking up from the level.

"Those are the approachable ones."

"Approachable's not the same as good."

It landed too near Tommy, and for one bad second she heard them as the same voice, because both had put a finger straight through the story she was telling herself. Then she heard the difference. Tommy had belittled to make her smaller and himself larger; this man was refusing to flatter her, which was a different and more useful thing, and she was not going to shrink for either of them.

"I'm opening a pottery studio," she said, "not an emotional-growth seminar."

"Shelves are cheaper than those."

"You've priced both?"

"Only shelves." He seated a screw. "Emotional growth tends to go over budget."

She laughed, surprised out of herself, and the room loosened a notch. But he had seen the wrapped box, and she was not going to be caught hiding her own best work on her first afternoon in her own building.

She unwrapped one more thing while his back was turned, and it was not the pretty bowl, and it was not a peace offering, whatever she told herself. A workshop mug — heavy through the base so it would sit down and stay on a surface someone actually used, the wall thickened where a thumb would want to press, the handle thrown generous and finished with a flattened place along the inner curve, glazed a dry stoneware brown that would take a sawdust fingerprint and never show it, the lip left plain and unprecious. She had made it for no one, the way she had made a great many things for no one, waiting for the right hand.

"Here," she said, setting it on the bench near his tools, keeping her voice offhand. "Borrow that one if you're having your coffee while you work. It won't tip if someone bumps the bench."

She could see him decide she was upselling him — the sea-change shopkeeper with a handmade mug and a handmade story attached. He picked it up to be polite.

And then he went still. It had nothing to do with beauty. His thumb had found the flattened place without looking for it; the handle had taken his fingers without a fuss and the weight had settled low into his palm exactly where a hand wants weight, and she watched a man who priced things for a living be handed something he had no price for. He turned it once, looked into it, and at nothing.

"Handle's right," he said.

That was all. He set it down — well back from the edge, the way he had set his own cup — and it stood there being right, and Maggie felt both the flush of it landing and the exposure of having landed it. Her talent was never so intimate as when it actually worked. She had just told a near-stranger something about himself she had no business knowing and no words for anyway.

He looked at her differently after that. Not softened. Recalibrated.

"So," he said, and there was no cruelty in it, only bluntness sharpened by something that might have been respect. "You opening a shop here. Or hiding in one?"

She opened her mouth, found she did not have the answer, and discovered that not having it did not feel, for once, like failing.

---

He finished as the light went. He swept most of the sawdust and left a scatter of it, the way a man does when he wants the floor to remember work happened here. He declined coffee — she was relieved, because she still had no idea where the tea bags had got to, and annoyed, because she had wanted him to want to stay.

"I'll come back for the shelves upstairs," he said from the door. "Whenever you're ready."

There might have been a softness under it. She had just lectured herself about reading softness into practical men, so she let it be exactly what it said, and watched the ute reverse up the empty street with the unhurried competence of everything he did.

Then the town settled towards evening around her open door. The cloud had come down low over the water and the bay had gone the grey-green of a mussel shell; the gulls lifted off the car park; the bakery stayed dark; the hill dropped its long shadow, and the salt wind came in through the propped door and moved the newspaper on the floor. For the first time all day the place felt located — not a listing, not a memory, but a room in a particular town on a particular grey autumn evening, with her in it.

The cat walked in as though he owned the freehold. A large disreputable ginger tom, thoroughly dusted in clay he had not earned, one ear standing to attention and the other long since surrendered to experience. He crossed to the kiln, walked around it twice, applied one considering paw to the door seal, and sat with his tail curled about him in the measured satisfaction of an assessor who has found the premises barely adequate but will not, on this occasion, be writing it up.

"That's the heater," Maggie told him. "You'll want to declare an interest."

He gave her the flat yellow stare of a difficult stakeholder, and she caught the word in her own mouth and put it down. Not a stakeholder — she was done with stakeholders. Landlord, perhaps. Then he crossed to where the good apron sat folded on the second box — thick canvas gone soft at the ties, the apron from the Fitzroy class seven years ago, the afternoon she had understood in her wrists that clay was the true work and she had been doing everything else wrong. He climbed onto it, turned round twice, tucked his chin, and closed his eyes with an air of finality. She could move him, protect the one holy object she owned, be sensible — which she had been for twenty years, and which had got her a clean divorce and a company car she'd had to give back. She let him stay.

She had not solved a single thing. Tommy's voice was not gone, only quiet, and quiet was not the same as gone. Cal had not flattered her and had left a question hanging she still could not answer. And there was a key missing from the ring — the rear door, the studio she couldn't yet reach — waiting to be tomorrow's first small problem, the shiny empty split ring and the fresh scrape she would have to explain to somebody in the morning. But the room was real. The kiln was cold and hers to fill. The cat was disgraceful and asleep on the one thing she loved. The sea was outside, going about its enormous grey business in the dark. She was here.

And when she went to close up she found the evening had come all the way in, and rather than shut it out she left the front door standing open a while longer — on the dark street, on the salt, on the town that had not yet decided about her — because for the first time in longer than she could stand to count, she was not managing an exit. She was leaving something open, and seeing what walked in.
