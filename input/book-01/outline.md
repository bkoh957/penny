## Solution: the-central-mystery
- culprit: Mary Kearney (Cal's honorary aunt / mother's old friend / workshop-adjacent caretaker; daughter of the dying man)
- victim: Dr Neil Hartigan, the town GP
- central deception / motive: Mary has believed for a decade that her father was murdered; a recent discovery finally named Neil as the hand behind his death. She kills him believing she is avenging a crime — but Neil eased her father's passing at the old man's own repeated, desperate plea. She is right that his death was no accident, and catastrophically wrong about what it meant. Neil's compassion is the thing that kills him; his honesty in letting the truth surface is the trigger.
- suspects: Mary Kearney, Saffron, Beryl Foss, Faye, Vanessa Calloway, Cobber (witness)
- key locations: Neil's cottage kitchen (the erasure scene), The Wheelhouse, the community hall, the beach at dawn, the old cemetery

## Threads
- A-murder — a beloved GP is killed for a mercy mistaken as murder; the truth surfaces through Maggie's gift and detonates the chosen family she is falling for
- B-romance — a slow-burn between Maggie and Cal, carpenter, complicated when the killer is revealed to be his honorary aunt / mother's old friend; wounded but bonded by the end
- C-internal — a woman who stopped seeing her own life honestly learns to see again; recovering from a stale marriage, reclaiming her art, finding a place
- pottery-business — Maggie moves from hiding behind seconds and underpricing her work, through the surf-club commission and repaired-glaze breakthrough, to claiming The Wheelhouse as a viable studio, shop and teaching space.
- gift — the Too-Much moves from hyper-detailed, unranked observation / cognitive overload, through dangerous overconfidence and social-validation misuse, to disciplined observation that can guide but not replace proof; the origin mystery is teased through Artie and Elspeth Vale but not resolved.
- victim-reveal — Neil is mourned as a saint, exposed as a sinner when his old mercy surfaces, then understood as a difficult, secretive person: capable of compassion, capable of harm, and human enough to have underestimated what truth would do to the people left behind.
- comedy — the op-shop lookalikes' contradictory testimony, Faye and Iris serving the same disputed jam, Glaze the studio cat, and Maggie's mortifying faux-pas blurts keep the tone warm throughout
- series-seeds — Artie Selwood hints he has seen Maggie's kind of sight before; Vanessa Calloway's buried bench skeleton is raised but left unresolved; Mara's headland/developer conflict remains community texture unless promoted in a later book; Saffron's humiliation opens a possible future friendship and the Elspeth Vale hook.

## Chapter Engine Used Throughout

### Sleuth Method Contract

Maggie is a reluctant, meticulous witness, not a human lie detector or Holmesian profiler. Her Too-Much is visual, tactile and structural: objects returned to the wrong place, rooms reset according to someone else's habits, routines interrupted, tool marks, wear, weight, balance, cracks, joins, weak points, repairs, overworking and strain. Her core weakness is not seeing too little; it is deciding too soon what a real anomaly means.

Pottery is the bridge between the gift, the business, Tommy's ghost and the mystery — but never magical evidence. When Maggie cannot make sense of an observation, she returns to the bench. In sketching, pinching, throwing, cutting apart, repairing or remaking clay, she discovers the shape of the story she is currently telling herself. That story may be partly right, unfair, incomplete, or entirely wrong. **A crack tells the story of what happened to the clay, not the secret moral truth of the person holding it.**

Use Maggie's right-piece talent sparingly as the warm, artistic form of her seeing. A right-piece moment is not ceramic therapy and not soul-reading: Maggie notices hands, use, hesitation, weight, balance, pressure, glaze, rim, foot, wall and fit, then offers or withholds an object. The recipient's reaction should be practical and restrained — use, refusal, silence, a tiny recalibration — never a speech about being understood. Recommended Book 1 motif beats: Cal's workshop mug/bench dish in ch 1; Neil's severe/plain useful piece before death in ch 3; Mary's dark inversion through exact handling of a lidded vessel/cake plate/tea towel in ch 5; one repair/trust beat through Faye or Beryl after Maggie damages trust; and the surf-club commission as the community-scale right piece in chs 28–29. Do not give every character a bespoke ceramic revelation.

After Neil's cottage, Pruitt gives her a practical notebook discipline with three columns: **What I saw**, **What it might mean**, and **What else could explain it**. Maggie privately adds a fourth, optional studio question: **What shape does it make?** The fourth column is not proof. It helps her see when she is imposing a story onto incomplete information. Early in Act II she still lets the middle column feel like fact. After the Beryl bakery failure, she begins forcing herself to test alternate explanations. By Act III, her mature sleuth marker is: **I do not know what this means yet. I only know it does not fit.**

Use the three-part investigation engine throughout:

- **Maggie:** notices an anomaly and may translate it into a provisional clay form — what does not fit?
- **Cal:** becomes Maggie's informal practical co-sleuth — tests local, practical and physical plausibility without replacing Pruitt or becoming an official investigator — is this actually unusual here?
- **Pruitt:** verifies evidence, timelines and proof — can this survive testing?

Do not make one bespoke suspect portrait per chapter, and do not turn pottery into a clue machine. Use a sparse, evolving private body of studio work at hinge points only: the ugly vase, a quiet divided Faye/Iris form if needed, the distorted room-vessel, cracked/repaired commission bowls, a light rope/coil experiment if it earns its place, a contained Mary-adjacent lidded vessel kept subtle, and the final surf-club bowls. Pottery is a question, never a verdict.

Keep only one major public investigative failure: the Beryl bakery incident. Other false leads should be quieter, more evidence-based, and varied in what they protect: business failure, medical privacy, family dignity, local-normal routines, old obligations, or simple refusal to be town property. Do not make every secret an identical category of concealment, and do not treat privacy as guilt.

Every chapter carries a complete mini-story:

1. **Start / Desire** — What Maggie wants in this scene or chapter.
2. **Pressure / Obstacle** — The person, event, social cost, clue, or practical problem that blocks her.
3. **Turn / Change** — What is materially different by the end.
4. **Texture / Pleasure Layer** — Humour, coastal life, pottery, food, local rituals, animal business, or relational warmth.
5. **Hook** — The unresolved question or complication that earns the next chapter.

### Story Tracks

- **M — Mystery:** evidence, suspects, motive, victim revelation, fair-play logic.
- **P — Personal:** Maggie's migraines, guilt, old HR failure, confidence, and learning to separate observation from certainty.
- **R — Romance / Community:** Cal, Mary, Faye, Saffron, chosen-family loyalty, belonging, and town politics.
- **B — Business:** The Wheelhouse, the surf-club commission, professional confidence, pricing, sales, and Maggie's artistic voice.

A chapter should move at least two tracks. Most move three. The quieter chapters are intentionally allowed to move **R + B + P** while the murder thread rests, so the book feels like a lived-in coastal mystery rather than a procession of interviews.

Use small practical mysteries sparingly to create cozy movement and make Maggie likeable through usefulness rather than interrogation. They should not all feed the murder. Recommended micro-arcs for Book 1:

- **Missing Wheelhouse key (chs 1–2):** an arrival logistics problem solved through object/lock observation and local knowledge; quick payoff, possible tiny old-tenant seed only.
- **Jam-feud mini-mystery (chs 5–12):** what really happened to the shared/same-jam origin and Faye's mother's preserve entry, why the town remembers the cheap version, and why the truth is emotionally incomplete; private payoff, no forced reconciliation.
- **Kiln/commission repair problem (chs 13–17, payoff 28–29):** business/art pressure independent of the murder; failed firing or flawed bowls become the repair-glaze breakthrough.

Do not add more than this unless a later book needs it. The rule is: a small problem should reveal character, deepen a relationship, enrich Pelican's Crook, or leave one useful detail — not turn every inconvenience into a murder clue.

# Act I — Arrival, Connection, Murder (Chapters 1–7)

## Chapter 01 — The Wheelhouse

### Overall Summary
Maggie arrives in Pelican's Crook with the giddy, frightening certainty that this sea-change is the first right thing she has done in years, and makes The Wheelhouse physically real: a lease, a kiln, shelves, stock, and one missing practical key that turns arrival into a small solvable debacle. The front door opens, but the key to the rear studio/kiln-room access or upstairs utility cupboard is missing from the estate agent's ring. The chapter establishes her lifelong art talent, her almost sensual way of reading clay/use/fit, Tommy's off-page ghost voice, the first harmless use of observation, and the first charged friction with Cal. Cal arrives to fit shelves and initially reads Maggie through the town's existing Saffron-shaped scepticism about polished sea-change reinventions. After the key/shelf awkwardness, Maggie gives him a modest workshop mug or bench piece that fits him with unnerving practical rightness. The moment gives the reader a reason to like Maggie: she is not only wounded or defensive; she is talented, generous, funny, and brave enough to offer the right piece without making a speech about it. It ends with the town not yet welcoming her, but with Glaze, the studio, and the sea giving her a first fragile claim on the place.

### Scene 1 — The Drive: A Woman Choosing Herself

**Location:** Hatchback on the Great Ocean Road, nearing Pelican's Crook; late April, early autumn afternoon.

**Purpose:** Open with movement, landscape and motive. Let Maggie think before the town/murder machinery begins.

**Beat flow:**

1. Start in the car, not at the shop window. The hatchback smells of iron oxide, old cardboard, damp canvas and clay dust. Everything she has chosen to keep from the old life is physically around her; everything she has refused to keep is absent.
2. Establish the drive as long enough for self-mythologising and doubt. Brunswick has fallen away; Geelong has fallen away; the road has narrowed into coast, cliff, wet eucalypts, gulls, signs for lookouts she does not stop at because if she stops too often she might turn the car around.
3. The coast should give pleasure. Maggie notices the sea flashing between trees, the Otways crowding the road, fern gullies dark with recent rain, the absurd courage of building a road on the edge of all that water. She is frightened, but also awake in a way she has not been for years.
4. Establish why she chose **Pelican's Crook specifically**, not just "a coastal town." Four years earlier, she came through Pelican's Crook on a long weekend down the Great Ocean Road and wandered into a small pottery trail as a paying visitor, not for work. She stood too long in the doorway of this exact studio, held by the light on glaze and the feel of a room built for hands rather than meetings. She did not act then because she was still married, still sensible, still filing inconvenient desires away. After the divorce, she searched practical listings across the coast, but Pelican's Crook was the only place that did not feel like reinvention-as-performance. It had a real empty studio, a kiln already in place, a main street small enough that work might matter, and enough distance from Brunswick that Tommy's version of her life could not keep walking in uninvited.
5. Make the choice economically and emotionally concrete: The Wheelhouse lease was risky but just possible with the divorce settlement/savings; the flat upstairs meant she could live over the shop; the town's off-season quiet meant she could start before anyone expected polish; the long-weekend pottery-trail memory gave her one private proof that she had once wanted this before the marriage ended, so it was not simply a post-divorce panic purchase.
6. She tries on versions of the decision:
   - sea-change, if said by a real estate agent;
   - breakdown, if said by Tommy;
   - artist's retreat, if said by someone with linen trousers and generational wealth;
   - in Maggie's own words, perhaps: a woman finally driving towards the life she should have noticed sooner.
7. Fold in why she is there: HR taught her to manage everyone else's exits; the marriage taught her to shrink; pottery was the thing she had loved before she knew what a career was supposed to look like. She has always had the art-talent version of the Too-Much: the right piece for the right person, the cup someone keeps touching before they can admit they want it, the bowl whose weight suits one pair of hands and not another. The Wheelhouse is not a whim. Pelican's Crook is not random. This is the first decision in years that was not designed to look sensible to someone else.
8. First Tommy ghost. Trigger: she lets herself feel proud of choosing the town and signing the lease.
   - Treatment: one italic burst, no attribution tag, clearly remembered voice.
   - Function: sting, not melodrama. Something like: *"A shop. With your savings. Christ, Mags, you do know hobbies are meant to cost less than a mortgage."*
   - Maggie's response should show the new self: she hears it, flinches, keeps driving. Maybe she turns the radio up, then turns it off because she refuses to drown herself out.
9. Give the reader the first glimpse of Pelican's Crook as arrival, not just map: the bay appears in a crook of coast; a headland curls around the water; the town sits on the thin shelf between sea and steep green hills as if it has been tucked there by someone trying not to wake it.
10. Maggie clocks the triple strangeness of the name lightly if natural — Pelican's Crook sounds like a bird, an illness, and a crime. She decides this is either a sign or very bad branding. This gives voice/personality without over-explaining.

**Emotional turn:** By the end of the drive, Maggie is still afraid, but the landscape has made the choice feel real and possible. She is not only fleeing Tommy/HR; she is arriving at herself.

**Texture to include:** wet eucalypt, salt through the vents, road signs, empty lookouts, autumn light, sea flashing between trees, boot rattle from tools, clay dust on her sleeve, hands tight then loosening on the wheel.

### Scene 2 — First Look at Town: The Map of a New Life

**Location:** Pelican's Crook main street and foreshore glimpses.

**Purpose:** Establish where we are. Make the town vivid before it becomes a suspect pool.

**Beat flow:**

1. Maggie arrives at ten past two. Off-season is visible: tourist brightness packed away, closed/short-hour signs, a bakery awning tied back, dog walkers, post office, hardware shop, two pubs, op-shop/general-store possibility, gulls in the car park.
2. The main street runs parallel to the bay, with the sea visible between buildings and the hill rising behind. The headland/lighthouse can be glimpsed or implied without overloading Book 1's mystery.
3. She drives the street once before parking because she is trying to look like someone who knows where she is going. This is comic and revealing: she is new enough to miss the turn, proud enough not to do a three-point turn in front of the whole town, and HR-trained enough to call this "stakeholder reconnaissance" in her head before banning herself from the phrase.
4. Establish outsider status: no one waves because no one knows her yet; one person looks at the hatchback and looks away; she is relieved and disappointed by this in equal measure.
5. The Wheelhouse appears as the emotional target: salt-hazed glass, weathered frontage, former bait-and-tackle bones, a main-street shopfront with living space at the back/upstairs, and the faint trace of the previous owner/tenant whose story no one volunteers yet. It should feel modest, not twee. A working room, not a postcard.
6. Let the four-years-ago pottery-trail memory answer the "why here" again, now tied to the physical building. Maggie remembers standing in this doorway as a private visitor on a long-weekend pottery trail, watching water-light move over glaze, and thinking with embarrassing clarity: *if I had a life, it would look something like this*. She had laughed at herself then and gone home. Now she has stopped laughing.
7. The choice should feel half practical, half irrational in the right way: she could have leased somewhere cheaper, busier, safer, closer to Melbourne, but none of those places had lodged under her ribs. Pelican's Crook did. The town is not yet home; it is the one place that kept calling after she had run out of excuses not to answer.

**Emotional turn:** The town is not yet home, but it is specific. Maggie can imagine being seen here, which is both terrifying and exactly the point.

### Scene 3 — Unlocking The Wheelhouse: Relief and the Ghost

**Location:** Inside The Wheelhouse.

**Purpose:** Let the studio landing breathe. Make the room a refuge and a challenge.

**Beat flow:**

1. Maggie unlocks the front door. This should be a body-level moment: key in lock, door sticking slightly, salt/cold/clay/old timber smell, a room that has been shut up waiting.
2. She stands inside and does not immediately work. She lets herself feel the relief of a space where no one has asked her to be reasonable. Her own door. Her own floor. Her own future mess.
3. The relief catches on a practical snag: the estate agent's key ring has an empty split ring, and the rear studio/kiln-room access, upstairs utility cupboard, fuse box or delivery door will not open. The front door works; something essential does not.
4. Maggie initially assumes administrative error, then notices harmless object facts: the empty ring is shiny from recent use, the stubborn lock has a fresh scrape or graphite mark, Glaze sits too confidently by the rear door, and someone has used this access more recently than the listing implied.
5. Keep the key problem small and non-ominous. It gives Maggie a concrete external want — make the whole studio usable — and lets her solve by observation before any murder plot begins. At most, it leaves a tiny old-Wheelhouse object or previous-tenant hint.
6. Second Tommy ghost. Trigger: the relief is too big; confidence threshold.
   - Possible burst: *"You always did like the idea of things more than the work, Mags."*
   - It should sting because it hits her fear: that this is performance, not vocation.
7. Maggie counters it through action rather than affirmation. She opens windows/doors where she can, checks what she can reach, touches the wheel cover, lays out tools, and writes down the missing-key problem instead of spiralling. She does not answer Tommy in exposition; she makes the room usable.
8. Establish the kiln as better than expected where accessible and The Wheelhouse as a working studio that still has local logistics attached. She checks elements, door seal, shelves, kiln wash or notes what she cannot yet check until the missing key is found. Let competence show. She is not a helpless newcomer; she knows the craft.
9. Establish the safe-stock problem without making Maggie seem timid by nature. She has brought good work and knows it is good; the defensive habit is about public value, not artistic doubt. She starts unpacking half-glazed seconds and safer pieces first because the town has not earned the vulnerable work yet, then catches herself treating her own studio like a probation period.
10. Put at least one genuinely strong piece on the page before Cal arrives: a mug, small bowl, tool dish or lidded vessel whose description is vivid, sensual and specific rather than generic cozy prettiness. Use Maggie's hyper-focused maker eye — weight under the heel of the hand, thumb-place, thrown wall, glaze pooled thin at the rim, iron bloom, salt-grey run, the clay's memory of pressure — to show how she sees.
11. The old HR binders stay in the boot. Important symbolic beat: she brought them because throwing away competence is hard, but she does not bring them inside. The new life is not pure yet, but the boundary begins.
12. Late that night, if there is room, Maggie begins one tiny private clay study without naming it: sturdy, slightly overbuilt, with a repaired-looking join. It is not a "Cal portrait"; it is the first sign that unresolved observations become form in her hands.

**Emotional turn:** The Wheelhouse gives real relief, but the ghost tests it. Maggie's answer is not triumphant; it is practical. She keeps unpacking.

### Scene 4 — Cal Arrives: Too-Much as Temptation, Comedy, and Misread

**Location:** The Wheelhouse, front door/east wall.

**Purpose:** Establish the romance/community thread with humour, friction, Cal's initial Saffron-shaped misread of Maggie, and the first restrained flirtation with the gift.

**Beat flow:**

1. Cal arrives at exactly 3pm in the ute. Preserve existing sensory detail: engine like a boat clearing its throat; tall; good-looking in a practical Australian bloke way rather than polished-romance-cover way; canvas work jacket gone soft at elbows; small level in pocket; stillness of a person who measures things for a living.
2. Maggie has built up a private fantasy from the note `Here at 3. C.` and his efficient silence. She has decided, on very little evidence, that she likes him. This should be funny because she knows it is ridiculous and still does it.
3. Introduction stays spare:
   - "Cal Burrell."
   - "Maggie Quill."
   - Optional internal: she had prepared three versions of hello and somehow chosen the one that sounded like exchanging evidence at a border checkpoint.
4. Cal's first private read of Maggie should be wrong but fair: another sea-change business, maybe another Saffron with nicer clay — signage, lifestyle story, city money, town as backdrop. Do not make him sneer or say this bluntly. Let it surface in a dry practical line: "Town's had a few reinventions," or "People come here with signage before they've met the wind." Maggie hears the comparison and bristles because it touches a real fear: that her brave choice looks like branding.
5. The Too-Much onset begins lightly. Not a full migraine; more like a warning flicker / sharpening / metal taste / sound edges. It is triggered by stress, newness, attraction, or the way Cal enters a room without asking it to rearrange itself around him.
6. What she perceives should be too much but not plot-solving or romantic mind-reading:
   - jacket worn at elbows, good boots repaired rather than replaced;
   - hands nicked, clean nails, one shallow cut from recent work;
   - the way he checks load-bearing points before trusting the wall;
   - his hand automatically steadying a warped shelf rather than complaining about it;
   - his preference for useful repair over replacement;
   - he looks once at her strongest wrapped piece and once at the seconds, and understands more than she wants. She can see use, repair and restraint; she cannot know his private life or interest.
7. Maggie actively resists turning the observations into a verdict. This is crucial. She tells herself Cal is not an employee, not a candidate, not a grievance file, not a husband to be decoded before he can hurt her. She can notice use, wear and habit; she does not get to claim she knows him.
8. Humour: because she is busy nobly not reading him, and also busy being annoyed at the Saffron-shaped misread, she misses or bungles the actual conversation.
9. She tries light flirting, awkward but warm. Cal gets it. The joke is not that he is oblivious; the joke is that he chooses to play ignorant because he is amused, careful, and not going to make the first meeting too easy for either of them. Options:
   - She compliments the punctuality: "Three exactly. Should I be impressed or worried?"
   - Cal answers with deliberate literalness: "Neither. Clock in the ute works."
   - She tries again: "Good. I was hoping for reliable rather than ominous."
   - He looks at the wall, lets half a smile reach his voice before his mouth, and says, "Ominous costs extra."
   This gives him dry humour, warmth and control without making him chatty. Maggie should realise he understood the flirtation only after the moment has already slipped past her.
10. He remains welcoming but hard to get. When she tries to extend into social warmth — coffee, local recommendations, maybe a joking "is this where you tell me every shelf in town has a secret?" — Cal does not shut her down. He sidesteps with good-natured practicality, keeping the ball in play while pretending there is no ball: useful answer, faint smile, back to the wall.
11. Cal asks where she wants the shelves. Maggie realises the flirtation has not died; it has been neatly set on a shelf she cannot reach yet. She recovers by becoming competent.

**Emotional turn:** Maggie is attracted/interested, embarrassed, and slightly annoyed. Cal is not a fantasy generated by a two-word note; he is a good-looking, good-natured local with boundaries, humour, enough confidence to let her flirt without immediately rewarding it, and enough town-scepticism to misread her before he knows better.

### Scene 5 — Shelves, Work, and the First Right Piece

**Location:** The Wheelhouse, east wall; portable saw on pavement.

**Purpose:** Preserve the original establishment: shelves, defensive stock, Cal's blunt challenge, business/personal tracks — then give the reader a reason to like Maggie by showing her gift at its warmest: matching the right piece to the right person without making a speech.

**Beat flow:**

1. Cal works. Maggie unpacks around him. Keep his dialogue sparse and practical: third bracket, clearance, airflow, grain, plugs. He measures twice. She likes the quiet and resents liking it.
2. Maggie arranges seconds and safe pieces where the new shelves will carry them. She hides or leaves wrapped the stronger pieces — the ones with a real point of view, less saleable in her anxious mind.
3. Cal notices. He does not make a grand speech. He asks one blunt question or makes one practical observation:
   - "Those the ones you're selling?"
   - Maggie: "Those are the approachable ones."
   - Cal: "Approachable's not the same as good."
4. This lands too close to Tommy but from a different direction. Tommy belittled; Cal refuses to flatter. Maggie initially hears them as the same because both puncture the story she is telling herself.
5. Tommy ghost can flicker here if not overused: one short line that punishes taking up space, e.g. *"Best not lead with the strange ones. People like to know what they're buying."* Use only if it does not crowd the earlier bursts; two Tommy bursts may be enough for ch1.
6. Maggie pushes back with humour because defensiveness alone makes her smaller:
   - "I'm opening a pottery studio, not an emotional-growth seminar."
   - Cal: "Shelves are cheaper than those."
   - Maggie: "You've priced both?"
   - Cal: "Only shelves. Emotional growth tends to go over budget."
7. He still refuses to flatter, but the refusal should feel useful rather than hostile. This establishes the core dynamic: he sees craft and evasion; she sees bluntness, good humour, and the danger of being seen.
8. If the missing-key problem has made her look flustered or over-managed, let this scene rebalance her. Maggie is not a hapless newcomer; she knows objects, use and hands. She notices how Cal sets his takeaway cup away from the bench edge, the size of his fingers through a handle, the way he tests weight before trusting anything, the dust that would show on glossy glaze, and the kind of object that would survive a workshop without asking to be admired.
9. She chooses one better piece to unwrap — ideally a workshop mug with a heavier base and a handle right for his hand, or a small bench dish for screws/washers. It should not be obviously romantic or symbolically labelled. It is a practical offering after an awkward day: "Borrow that one if you're having coffee while you work," or "That one won't tip if someone bumps the bench."
10. Cal thinks she is upselling him or performing the sea-change shopkeeper role, then picks it up and goes still. The rightness is physical, not sentimental: weight low in the palm, thumb finding the flattened place, handle taking his fingers without fuss, glaze that will not mind sawdust fingerprints, lip plain enough not to be precious. He is stunned not by beauty but by fit.
11. Keep the reaction restrained. No soul-language. No "How did you know me?" Cal turns it once, sets it down away from the edge, and says something minimal: "Handle's right," or "That'll work." Maggie should feel both pleased and exposed; her talent is intimate when it lands.
12. The moment should soften but not solve the clash. Cal has to recalibrate: Maggie may be new, but she is not selling a lifestyle brochure. She makes useful, exact things. Maggie has to recalibrate too: being seen by a practical person can feel better than being flattered.
13. He asks the hook question in some form: whether she is opening a shop or hiding in one. Keep it blunt but not cruel. Coming after the mug/bench-piece moment, it should feel like respect sharp enough to sting, not dismissal.

**Emotional turn:** The shelves are not just furniture; they force the question of whether The Wheelhouse is a refuge from judgement or a place where she will risk being known. The right-piece moment gives Maggie agency and charm after the key debacle: she is someone who can make a stranger feel accurately held by an object, then blush because that is more revealing than she meant to be.

### Scene 6 — Departure, Town Quiet, and Glaze Chooses Her

**Location:** The Wheelhouse doorway/interior; early autumn dark.

**Purpose:** Return to warmth after the Cal friction; end with personality and belonging.

**Beat flow:**

1. Cal finishes, sweeps most of the sawdust, leaves enough to prove work happened. He declines coffee. Maggie is relieved because she still does not know where the tea bags are and annoyed because she wanted him to want coffee.
2. He offers to return for upstairs shelves "whenever you're ready." This is practical but can carry a faint softness. Maggie should not over-read it; she has just lectured herself about that.
3. He leaves. She watches the ute reverse up the street. The town settles towards evening: low cloud, sea grey-green, gulls over car park, bakery dark, hill shadow, salt wind through the open door. The chapter should now feel located.
4. Glaze walks in. Keep the original comic inspection but heighten Maggie's personality. He is a ginger tom with one ear surrendered to experience, wearing clay dust like a credential. He inspects the kiln as if conducting compliance work.
5. Maggie speaks to him as if he is a difficult stakeholder, then catches herself and revises: no, not stakeholder; landlord, perhaps. Or quality assurance.
6. Glaze claims the good apron. This connects to Maggie's pottery origin (Fitzroy class, seven years ago, knowing clay was the true work). It also gives her a small choice: move the cat / protect the good apron / be sensible, or let one ridiculous local creature choose her.
7. She lets him stay. This is a low-stakes but emotionally meaningful acceptance of mess, attachment and local complication.
8. Final emotional beat: She has not solved her life; she has opened a door. Tommy's voice is not gone, Cal did not flatter her, the town has not welcomed her yet, and the shelves hold too many seconds. But the room is real. The kiln is real. The cat is real. The sea is outside. She is here.
9. End close to original but with stronger agency: not happiness yet; possibility, yes — and maybe the first tiny act of defiance is that she leaves the door open.

### Chapter Structure Summary

- **Start / Desire:** Maggie wants to arrive in Pelican's Crook as the new version of herself: an artist with a studio, not a divorced HR director performing competence.
- **Pressure / Obstacle:** The landscape invites her forward, but Tommy's ghost voice and her own defensive habits keep reframing the choice as foolish, temporary or performative. Cal's bluntness exposes this defence.
- **Turn / Change:** The Wheelhouse becomes physically real: shelves fitted, kiln checked, one stronger piece offered to the right person, and Glaze installed. Maggie has not solved every defensive habit, but she has shown the reader and Cal what her talent can do.
- **Texture / Pleasure Layer:** Great Ocean Road drive, wet eucalypt, salt through car vents, off-season main street, headland/bay, salt-hazed glass, key ring with an empty split ring, kiln smell, clay tools, sawdust, dry flirtation, a workshop mug or bench dish fitting Cal's hand, Glaze's inspection.
- **Humour Layer:** Maggie's internal labels for the decision; her failed/over-managed greeting; Too-Much restraint making her miss normal social cues; Cal knowingly playing literal/ignorant when Maggie flirts; cat-as-property-inspector.
- **Hook / Closing Question:** Is Maggie opening a shop, or hiding in one? The question remains unresolved but active, while the missing key gives tomorrow a practical first problem to solve.
- **Tommy burst:** Two early stings: one on the drive/lease decision, one inside The Wheelhouse when relief makes her vulnerable. Optional third only if needed at the defensive-stock beat.

## Chapter 03 — The Doctor Who Listens Too Late

### Overall Summary
Maggie's first serious migraine in Pelican's Crook strips away her carefully staged competence and forces her to be seen as vulnerable in the studio. The attack is triggered not by melodramatic sensory volume but by cognitive overload: she has been unconsciously using the Too-Much to perfect every shelf, tag, pot and surface until her mind cannot stop registering detail. Dr Neil Hartigan arrives as a difficult kind of help: brusque, clinically serious, genuinely caring, and the first person to understand that Maggie's problem may be hyper-observance rather than ordinary sensitivity. The chapter plants Neil's changed reputation, gives Maggie specific medical advice that explains the gift to the reader, and creates a charged first sale when Neil buys the ugly failed vase that later becomes a key physical object.

### Scene 1 — The Wheelhouse Becomes Too Exact

**Location:** The Wheelhouse, late morning/early afternoon; workbench, shelves, kiln area.

**Purpose:** Let the migraine begin inside Maggie's professional space. Show the Too-Much as uncontrolled hyper-observance and cognitive saturation, not heightened sensory pain or magic.

**Beat flow:**

1. Open with Maggie trying to make The Wheelhouse look ready enough to deserve local faith: wiping shelves, re-spacing mugs by height, re-writing price tags, aligning wrapped work, checking the kiln area, clearing the cake crumbs and tin from the counter.
2. She does not realise she is using the Too-Much as a perfection tool. Her mind keeps catching ordinary discrepancies: a shelf bracket not quite level, a glaze drip on one mug, dust in a corner, a price tag angled differently from the others, the ugly heavy vase in the wrong place.
3. The first migraine signal is cognitive overload: not light becoming magically brighter or sound becoming painfully louder, but every ordinary detail arriving with equal priority and refusing to drop away.
4. Glaze is present as comic comfort and practical obstruction. He moves one object, sits on one cloth, nudges one tag, and Maggie's mind instantly tries to re-map the whole room around the disruption.
5. Maggie tries to correct one more thing, then one more thing after that. The compulsion is recognisable: if the room is perfect, perhaps she will be safe to be judged.
6. The overload tips. She reaches for, moves, or tries to save a pot and accidentally breaks it. Make this visible and dramatic enough that the crisis cannot stay private: clay shattering, glaze chips, one piece she cared about now irreparable.
7. She tries to manage the breakage as if it were a shopkeeping problem — sweep, hide, minimise — but dizziness/nausea/pressure and the flood of competing details make sequence impossible.

**Emotional turn:** Maggie begins by trying to prove competence through control; by the end, that control has broken a pot and made privacy impossible.

**Texture to include:** price tags, shelf dust, glaze drips, clay shards, broom/dustpan, cake tin moved out of cat range, Glaze disrupting the display, kiln notes, the ugly vase's exact placement, Maggie's hands shaking as she tries to sweep.

### Scene 2 — Faye Sees the Break

**Location:** The Wheelhouse doorway/front room, then threshold between shop and street.

**Purpose:** Bring community help into Maggie's private crisis. Let Faye's care deepen while making Maggie's fear of being seen socially costly.

**Beat flow:**

1. Faye arrives or checks in and sees the broken pot before Maggie can turn it into a tidy anecdote. Keep Faye practical: she lowers her voice, moves Glaze, gets water, assesses the shards, and asks short useful questions.
2. Maggie insists she is fine because a migraine sounds acceptable and manageable; the broken pot says otherwise. She tries to frame it as clumsiness rather than collapse.
3. Faye notices the mismatch: Maggie is speaking politely, but she is pale, dizzy, tracking too many small things, and not processing questions in order.
4. Faye decides to call Dr Neil Hartigan despite Maggie's embarrassment. This should establish Faye as warm but not easily managed: she will be kind, and she will still do the sensible thing.
5. Neighbouring town attention begins in small, plausible ways: a passerby slows, Dot and Glad may be visible outside arguing over whether broken pottery counts as an emergency while denying they look alike, but keep it contained. Maggie does not experience this as loudness; she experiences it as too many simultaneous facts.
6. Maggie catches fragments of local reaction to Neil before he arrives: a hesitation, a carefully neutral phrase, someone saying he is thorough, someone else going quiet. Do not explain yet.
7. The waiting stretches. The Wheelhouse, which was supposed to prove she could manage her life, becomes a room where other people are deciding what she needs.

**Emotional turn:** Maggie has to accept care before she feels she has earned it, which makes the town's kindness both comforting and exposing.

**Texture to include:** Faye's bakery smell in the studio, broken glaze chips, lowered blinds, water glass, cool cloth, passersby reflected in salt-hazed glass, Glaze relocated against his will, Faye's shoes among clay shards.

### Scene 3 — Neil Hartigan Names the Pattern

**Location:** The Wheelhouse front room / makeshift exam space near counter or workbench.

**Purpose:** Introduce Neil as medically serious rather than soothing: useful, abrasive, genuinely caring, and more complex than either saint or villain. Use him to clarify how the Too-Much works.

**Beat flow:**

1. Neil arrives with the authority of someone used to being obeyed and too tired to soften it. He is not cruel, but he is brusque; not charming, but not dismissive.
2. His first actions are competent: checks speech, pupils/light tolerance if medically needed, medication history, onset pattern, neurological red flags, hydration, whether the migraine has changed character. He asks the questions no one has bothered to ask before.
3. Maggie, still hyper-observant, sees something that does not match the town's guarded reaction: Neil cares. His manner is poor, but his attention is exact, patient-safety focused, and unwilling to wave her away.
4. He makes her describe the experience honestly. Guide the drafter away from sensory cliché: Maggie is not saying light becomes magically brighter; she is saying every visible/remembered/heard detail arrives at once and her mind cannot rank it.
5. Neil recognises the pattern enough to give it shape. He may call it hypervigilance, migraine aura, pattern-saturation, overload, or the brain refusing to filter. He should not treat it as supernatural, but he should understand that she is observing too much, not merely being dramatic.
6. Maggie reads him in fragments but cannot assemble him: tired cuffs, medical bag worn at corners, a pause when she describes seeing too much rather than merely pain, the quick practical care of someone who has seen people dismissed before.
7. He gives a practical path: rest, notes, follow-up, possibly tests/referral/medication review. He is impatient with her habit of apologising for taking up medical space.
8. Faye or another local reacts to Neil's tone. The room's temperature changes subtly when he speaks too sharply, showing his town reputation without stating it.

**Emotional turn:** Maggie moves from humiliation at needing a doctor to the uncomfortable recognition that this difficult man may be the first doctor to take her experience seriously and accurately.

**Texture to include:** leather/medical-bag smell, Neil's precise hands, prescription pad/notes app, broken pot swept imperfectly aside, Faye very still in the background, Glaze objecting from exile, Maggie trying to choose which detail to answer first.

### Scene 4 — The Town Flinches Around Him

**Location:** The Wheelhouse doorway and main street immediately after the exam.

**Purpose:** Plant Neil's changed reputation and the town's uneasy relationship with him while keeping Maggie's interpretation incomplete.

**Beat flow:**

## Chapter 06 — The Cost of Being Interesting

7. Maggie starts to form the sentence "someone cleaned up" before she has earned it.

**Emotional turn:** Maggie sees enough to know the room is wrong, but not enough to know what the wrongness means.

**Texture to include:** threshold angle, stair shadow, cup ring, damp cloth smell, biscuit tin label/colour, dust where tidiness breaks, wiped glaze, heavy clay, migraine pressure.

### Scene 4 — The Vase Makes Her Suspect-Shaped

**Location:** Neil's cottage interior / threshold / garden boundary.

**Purpose:** Let the obvious weapon status create social and procedural pressure around Maggie while keeping Pruitt fair.

**Beat flow:**

1. The vase's condition bothers Maggie: wiped carefully dry, set slightly off its mark, clean in a room whose cleanliness already feels staged.
2. Make the object physical: weight implied by its proportions, glaze flaw, uneven rim, the part Maggie knows is technically bad, the part Neil apparently chose to keep visible.
3. Pruitt asks practical questions: when did Neil buy it, did anyone else know, did she touch it after the sale, can she identify distinguishing marks, did she have any reason to visit Neil's cottage.
4. Maggie feels the town's gaze through the walls. The vase being hers does not make her guilty, but it gives people a story-shaped handle: the new pottery woman, her object, the dead doctor.
5. Her hurt contaminates her interpretation. Because she feels judged by the object, every detail becomes sharper and less trustworthy.
6. Pruitt keeps his tone careful. He is neither accusing her nor indulging her; he is collecting facts.
7. The boundary shifts: Maggie is no longer only a grieving patient/friend. She is a witness with a possible evidence link.

**Emotional turn:** The scene stops being only Neil's death and becomes tangled with Maggie's work, pride, public standing, and possible suspicion.

**Texture to include:** failed glaze, heavy clay, evidence handling, hall table/floor, clean/dry surface, slight off-mark placement, Maggie's remembered embarrassment selling it, Pruitt's controlled questions.

### Scene 5 — Blurting Before Proof

**Location:** Cottage exterior / boundary line, immediately after the interior impressions.

**Purpose:** Let Maggie be right and wrong at the same time. She overwhelms Pruitt with accurate observations before she can organise them into evidence.

**Beat flow:**

1. Once Pruitt escorts her back out, the wrongness accumulates until Maggie blurts that someone cleaned up or that the fall is too tidy.
2. The line lands badly because she is outside the process again, visibly unwell, and emotionally compromised by the vase being hers.
3. Locals react: some lean in, some recoil, some begin rewriting the story around her words. The town is hungry for narrative and suspicious of the newcomer providing it.
4. Pruitt shuts down the public performance without humiliating her. He asks for specifics or tells her observation is not proof.
5. Maggie tries to list details, but because she is flustered and the Too-Much is flooding, the list overwhelms him and the crowd: chair, cup, towel, biscuit tin, wiped vase, dust break, object placement, too much order, wrong kind of clean.
6. Tommy burst hits as doubt cuts through the certainty.
   - Treatment: one sharp remembered line.
   - Function: he tells her she has always imagined more than is there.
7. Maggie feels both defiant and ashamed. She knows she saw something, but she cannot defend it cleanly.
8. Mary is distressed; Faye may be worried; Cal, if present, sees the town begin to look at Maggie differently.

**Emotional turn:** Maggie's accurate perception becomes socially damaging because she turns it into public certainty too soon.

**Texture to include:** crowd silence/rustle, wet shoes shifting, Pruitt's calm voice tightening, Maggie's overheated face, Tommy's italic sting, thermos steam, neighbour whispers, the cottage door behind them.

### Scene 6 — Observation Is Not Proof, But It Is Not Nothing

**Location:** Cottage path / edge of police scene as crowd breaks up.

**Purpose:** End with Pruitt's warning, but on a more positive partnership note: he does not accept Maggie's conclusions, yet he respects the precision of what she noticed.

**Beat flow:**

1. Pruitt speaks to Maggie more quietly after the public moment. He asks her to write down exactly what she noticed without embellishment and to separate what she saw from what she thinks it means.
2. He tells Maggie observation is not the same as proof. Keep it firm but not cruel; he is setting a boundary she needs.
3. Maggie bristles because he is right and because being right does not make her feel less dismissed.
4. Then Pruitt adds the important positive turn: he is not saying she saw nothing. He is saying useful observation has to survive being written down, checked, timed, and tested.
5. He gives her the notebook discipline that becomes her method: three columns — **What I saw**, **What it might mean**, and **What else could explain it**. The instruction is practical, not inspirational.
6. Let one or two of Maggie's early observations be modestly verified here or shortly after: the second cup ring is recent, the vase was wiped after being moved, or the room was reset after Neil's death because dust/object alignment does not match Neil's lived-in habits. These are evidence-based wins, not a solved culprit.
7. This gives Maggie a sliver of respect without giving her authority. Pruitt has not made her a partner, but he has not thrown her away as a crank either.
8. The business thread lands through the vase: her worst piece is now evidence, maybe the weapon, and certainly part of the public story before she is ready.
7. Close on Pruitt/vase/threshold rather than a solved clue. The reader should feel the room is staged, the vase matters, and Maggie's observations may matter if disciplined.

**Emotional turn:** Maggie leaves with the first real mystery evidence in her head, the first official warning against turning perception into performance, and the first hint that Pruitt may become frictional ally rather than simple obstacle.

### Track Movement

# Act IIa — False Leads, Costs, and Attachment (Chapters 8–17)

## Chapter 08 — The Studio Becomes an Incident Room

### Overall Summary
After Neil's death, The Wheelhouse becomes a comic, chaotic unofficial incident room because everyone now knows Maggie's pottery vase may have been the murder weapon. Locals arrive with sympathy purchases, casseroles, thermoses, rumours, and transparent excuses to stand near the woman whose work is evidence. Play the chapter funnier than the surrounding murder material: Glaze in the cash drawer, customers buying mugs they do not need, Beryl trying to impose order, Faye translating subtext with pastries, and Mary gently making the studio the room everyone gathers in. On first read, Mary's herding looks like practical community care; later, it should reread as someone quietly listening for suspicion and keeping an eye on what people think. Cal should be natural support to Maggie — practical, warm, and quietly attractive — not coldly argumentative.

### Scene 1 — Sympathy Purchases and Evidence Tourism

**Location:** The Wheelhouse front room, morning after the cottage scene.

**Purpose:** Turn the studio into a community pressure point and comic set-piece. People come because Maggie's vase is connected to Neil's death, and they pretend they are there for pottery.

**Beat flow:**

1. Open with Maggie trying to restore The Wheelhouse to ordinary function: unboxing market stock, checking the surf-club commission notes, wiping clay dust, deciding whether the shop should even open.
2. The first visitor comes in under a practical pretext — buying a mug, checking on her, asking whether she is all right — but really wants to stand in the shop of the woman whose vase may have killed Neil.
3. The purchase is unnecessary and awkwardly kind. Maggie can feel the money, the condolence, the curiosity and the avoidance attached to it. The business win is already contaminated and faintly absurd.
4. More people follow. They buy small, safe pieces or pick them up and put them down while saying almost nothing direct about Neil. The Too-Much notices object-and-routine facts instead of diagnosing grief: who avoids the empty shelf where Neil's vase stood, who asks whether a vessel can be repaired, who repeats a cottage detail not yet public, who accepts the cup Mary assigns without noticing the room has been reorganised around them.
5. Play the comedy: people inspecting mugs as if mugs might explain homicide, someone asking if a vase can be ordered "lighter," someone pretending not to look at the empty shelf where Neil's vase used to be.
6. Glaze occupies the cash drawer, wrapping paper, or a display shelf, creating small comic obstruction that also keeps the scene domestic and cozy.
7. By the end of the scene, the studio has stopped being only a studio. It is becoming the town's unofficial incident room, with a card reader.

**Emotional turn:** Maggie starts the day wanting to keep the business alive and ends realising the business may survive by becoming attached to Neil's death.

**Texture to include:** mugs wrapped in paper, card reader, clay dust, market crates, surf-club sketches half-covered, doorbell, Glaze in the cash drawer, condolences delivered with purchases, sea light on shelves.

### Scene 2 — Mary Makes Gathering Look Like Care

**Location:** The Wheelhouse front room/back sink/packing table as visitor traffic builds.

**Purpose:** Put Mary at the centre of the comic flow without villain signalling. She gently herds people into the studio, apparently to support Maggie and organise grief; later this can reread as listening and surveillance.

**Beat flow:**

1. Mary arrives with food, spare cups, or some practical excuse that makes her presence feel like rescue rather than intrusion.
2. She notices the room is becoming too much and immediately gives people small harmless tasks: put that plate there, take tea to Faye, don't block the doorway, let Maggie sit down, use the blue mug not that one. Her care works through objects and space.
3. She gently redirects stragglers into The Wheelhouse rather than letting rumour scatter along the street. On first read this looks like community management; under the later reveal it becomes a way to hear what people think and whether anyone suspects her.
4. Keep the behaviour plausible and kind. Mary is not lurking in corners; she is making tea, assigning cups, moving plates, returning things to places, lowering voices, and making herself useful.
5. The comedy comes from how easily people accept The Wheelhouse as headquarters once Mary gives the room a rhythm. Someone asks where to put biscuits as if Maggie has opened a branch office of grief.
6. Maggie feels grateful. After the cottage humiliation and the vase suspicion, being practically protected feels good.
7. The Too-Much notes Mary's orderly competence, but does not interpret it as suspicious. It is filed as care.

**Emotional turn:** Maggie's trust in Mary deepens: help feels like rescue, and she does not yet see the cost of letting someone else define the room around her.

**Texture to include:** tea tray, cups, folded tea towels, paper bags, Mary tying string, kettle steam, low-voiced corrections, Glaze accepting Mary as staff, Maggie's relief at not having to manage every person.

### Scene 3 — The Ones Not Quite Sad Enough

**Location:** The Wheelhouse front room/counter amid the comic traffic.

**Purpose:** Broaden the suspect field through Maggie's Too-Much observation rather than public gossip. Keep the funny incident-room surface while letting grief, relief and performance become readable.

**Beat flow:**

1. A sequence of townspeople passes through, but they do not hand Maggie a list of Neil stories. They offer condolences, make practical offers, buy pottery, ask after Faye or Mary, and avoid the obvious question.
2. The Too-Much accumulates object-and-routine clues under the words: who reaches for Neil's vase shelf and stops; who asks whether the vase can be repaired before asking about Neil; who knows a cottage detail not yet public; who mentions Beryl's committee roster too casually; who watches Mary reorganise cups rather than the police car.
3. Keep each observation physical and specific, not magical or grief-diagnostic: hands on objects, timing, repeated phrases, what information is known too early, which cup or shelf draws attention, which silence protects a fact rather than revealing an emotion.
4. Maggie begins mapping anomalies: which objects, routes, details and timings do not fit what people claim to know.
5. Her HR past returns as muscle memory: room dynamics, social performance, managed risk, people who make a silence do the work a sentence would have done.
6. The Too-Much should be quieter here than in migraine chapters: observational accumulation, not supernatural certainty. It gives clues about mismatched objects, timing and knowledge, not proof of motive or grief.
7. The danger is pleasure. Maggie feels competent. Reading the room gives her the role she lost in HR: the person who can notice what matters before anyone admits it.
8. Do not settle on a suspect yet. Let the field broaden and become messier through reactions, not accusations.

**Emotional turn:** Maggie shifts from wanting a clean explanation to enjoying the competence of collecting messy physical/social data.

**Texture to include:** overlapping condolences, half-drunk tea, hands on pottery, wrapped mugs, raincoats by the door, Glaze making customers step around him, eyes drawn to the empty vase shelf, Maggie mentally sorting objects/timings into invisible columns she should not trust yet.

### Scene 4 — Faye's Pastries and the Ugliest Vase

**Location:** The Wheelhouse counter/back table, late morning.

**Purpose:** Plant Faye's offhand remark about Neil's contempt for the vase while establishing the second new information channel: insider conversation from a friend who knows the town's subtext but still refuses cheap gossip.

**Beat flow:**

1. Faye arrives with pastries as comfort, friendship repair, and insider translation. She is not there to collect gossip; she is there because Maggie is now close enough to be told when she is reading the room wrongly.
2. Their friendship remains newly alert after the open-studio near-mistake. Faye is warm but watchful; Maggie wants to prove she can be trusted and keeps being interrupted by visitors whose objects, timings and details do not line up with their words.
3. Maggie admits, awkwardly, that some details do not line up: people know too much, avoid the wrong objects, or repeat polished condolences around practical inconsistencies. Faye does not reward overreach, but she understands the observation. She translates carefully: in a town this small, odd behaviour can mean a dozen things besides murder.
4. Faye shares or corrects one insider detail in passing rather than airing grievances. Without emphasis, she mentions Neil had described his pottery purchase to someone as the ugliest thing he had ever paid money for.
5. The line should hurt Maggie but not fully land because three other things are happening: someone is paying, someone knows a detail too early, Glaze is in the way, another person is waiting.
6. Maggie registers the vase insult as one more sting in a crowded room and does not come back to it. It now matters because the vase is evidence, but she is too busy being useful to process it.
7. Faye may not realise the remark's weight, or may assume Maggie already knows Neil had a dry cruelty about the purchase.
8. Maggie chooses the role of listener/observer over the vulnerability of saying that hurt me. The friendship is still cautious after the open-studio near-miss, but Faye has become an insider source because she is a friend, not because she is a gossip.

**Emotional turn:** A personal sting is planted inside public noise; Maggie misses its mystery significance because she is busy being useful to everyone else.

**Texture to include:** pastry box, sugar on paper, Faye's bakery smell, queue at the counter, wrapped purchases, pottery glaze under Maggie's thumb, the phrase "ugliest thing" landing then being buried.

### Scene 5 — Cal Becomes a Natural Support

**Location:** The Wheelhouse doorway/front room, late afternoon.

**Purpose:** Bring Cal in as practical support and warm boundary-setter. Further the neutral simmering attraction instead of making the interaction cold or combative.

**Beat flow:**

1. Cal arrives to repair a loose studio door, bracket, lock, or other genuine maintenance problem. He expects a job and finds the studio functioning as an unofficial incident room with pastries, mugs, and witnesses.
2. He clocks what the room is doing to Maggie before he comments on it: the strain, the way people are using her, the business traffic attached to Neil's death, the vase suspicion hanging around her work.
3. He helps practically first — fixes the door, carries something, opens a stuck window, changes the room's airflow, ushers a lingering visitor out by giving them a harmless task elsewhere.
4. Maggie notices the support physically: his steadiness, his quiet competence with tools, the way he does not make her explain being overwhelmed. Let attraction simmer through relief and shared competence, not banter alone.
5. He offers food, a chair, or five minutes of quiet in a way that echoes but softens his earlier "eat something" care. This time she receives more of it.
6. He gently names the risk: The Wheelhouse is gaining traffic, but the traffic is attached to Neil's death and to Maggie's role as listener. He is concerned for her and the business, not scolding.
7. Maggie pushes back because someone has to listen, especially if people were hurt by Neil and if Pruitt is moving carefully. Keep the disagreement low-voiced and intimate rather than cold.
8. Cal replies that noticing is not the same as knowing, but he frames it as protection: if she is going to notice, she needs not to let the town eat her alive while she does it.

**Emotional turn:** Cal becomes a steadier kind of support: he sees the cost before Maggie admits it and helps without trying to own the room.

**Texture to include:** loose door/lock, tool belt, sawdust or screw tray, visitors lingering, Cal's quiet physical efficiency, cups everywhere, Glaze inspecting tools, Maggie's sales notebook beside death notes, a brief charged silence over a practical kindness.

### Scene 6 — Saffron Refuses the Simple Question

**Location:** The Wheelhouse after most visitors leave / doorway or counter.

**Purpose:** End by positioning Saffron as the first plausible false lead while keeping Maggie's commitment to investigate emotionally motivated and methodologically flawed.

**Beat flow:**

1. After Cal's warning, Maggie tries to prove to herself she is not collecting people; she is following facts. This makes her more vulnerable to a clean suspect-shaped interaction.
2. Saffron appears, is mentioned, or returns to retrieve/order something. She is composed, careful, and visibly managing how she is perceived after Neil's death.
3. Maggie asks a simple question about where Saffron was on the night Neil died or whether Neil's retreat-related accusations had escalated.
4. Saffron refuses to answer fully. The refusal can be polite, cold, or framed as dignity: she will not perform innocence for a room that has already decided effort is guilt.
5. Maggie reads the performance as concealment. The reader should see that it could be guilt, humiliation, self-protection, or simple fury at being turned into a story.
6. Cal's warning hangs over the moment; Maggie chooses the pattern anyway because the pattern feels useful.
7. End with Saffron's non-answer as the hook: she was not at home / will not say where she was / refuses to make her private movements town property.

**Emotional turn:** Maggie commits herself to investigating, but the first suspect-shaped answer she gets is already contaminated by her hunger for pattern.

## Chapter 09 — Saffron's Convenient Guilt

### Overall Summary
Maggie wants the Saffron theory to work because it offers shape: Neil publicly undermined Saffron's wellness retreat, threatened her credibility with investors, and Saffron's movements/booking records leave a partial alibi gap she refuses to explain. Maggie follows facts quietly, and the reader sees both why Saffron is plausible and why Maggie cannot yet prove anything.

**Beat flow:**

**Purpose:** Show the town's appetite for an outsider culprit and keep Faye's strained friendship active while Maggie gathers Saffron context through an insider who is trying to protect both truth and privacy.

**Beat flow:**

Conversation about Saffron emerges through bakery traffic and Faye's careful context rather than open gossip: someone wonders whether Neil was right to question the retreat; someone mentions supplier invoices, investor brochures or retreat cancellations; someone wants Saffron to be guilty because she is polished, outside-ish, a corporate escapee who got here first, and easy to dislike.

### Scene 3 — Dot and Glad Remember a Car, Sort Of

**Location:** Dot and Glad's op-shop/front room/verandah/garden path.

**Purpose:** Plant the blue-green car clue while making eyewitness testimony comic, contradictory and unreliable in interpretation.
