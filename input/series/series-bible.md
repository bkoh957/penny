# THE PELICAN'S CROOK MYSTERIES
Story Bible & Series Guide
*Book* *One  ·*  *A Cosy Crime Series*
*A seaside town on the Great Ocean Road, where everyone has a secret*

# 1.  The Series at a Glance

**Logline.**  A divorced former HR director trades a career spent keeping other people’s secrets for a quiet pottery studio on the Great Ocean Road — only to discover that the blinding migraines she has suffered her whole life flood her with hyper-detailed perception, turning her into the most reluctant, and most accidental, detective on the coast.

**Genre.**  Cosy mystery (amateur sleuth). Murder offstage, violence implied not shown, puzzle over peril.

**Tone.**  Cosy and gentle with light laughs. Warm, witty, community-driven. The comfort of the town is the point; the murder is the engine, not the mood.

**Format.**  First in an ongoing series. Each book is a self-contained mystery; the town, the regulars, and the slow-burn romance carry across volumes.

**What makes it different.**  The heroine is not a genius. Her gift records everything and explains nothing. She perceives in overwhelming detail but cannot interpret until a later, ordinary moment makes a detail click into place — and along the way she blurts out half-formed observations that land her in comical, sometimes mortifying, trouble.

## The Series Promise

Readers return to a cosy series for the people and the place far more than the puzzle. Each book should deliver:

1. A return to Pelican's Crook and its beloved, bickering regulars.
1. A fresh, fair-play mystery the reader could theoretically solve.
1. A forward step in Maggie's slow-burn romance and her growing mastery of her gift.
1. At least one warm community set-piece — a market, a festival, a CWA fundraiser, a wake.
1. Genuine laughs, usually born from Maggie's gift misfiring at the worst moment.

# 2.  The Heroine — Margaret “Maggie” Quill

**Age.**  43.

**Background.**  An artist at heart who set her craft aside long ago for a sensible life — twenty years as a corporate HR director and a marriage that went stale. The marriage was to Tommy Quill, a real-estate broker who was jealous of her and belittled her for years; the staleness was her shrinking to fit him until she stopped seeing her own life. He had an affair; when she finally saw it she offered to forgive, and he divorced her in anger. After her divorce she fled the city, bought a small place in town, and opened a pottery studio, The Wheelhouse, on the main street: a return to the passion she shelved, and a flight from a life spent managing people and absorbing their problems into the solitude of making.

**The old career — and why it matters.**  Twenty years as an HR director made her a professional keeper of other people’s secrets: the quiet firings, the complaints, the crises behind the leave requests. That rhymes directly with Dr Neil Hartigan, the town’s medical keeper of secrets — she instinctively understands a man whose job was knowing what everyone conceals, which gives her private insight the police lack. It also rationalises her perceptiveness without inflating it: she is a trained reader of people and rooms (the tell, the account that doesn’t match the body language), which complements the Too-Much rather than duplicating it — the gift floods her with physical detail; her career taught her the human undercurrent. And it gives her real internal friction: a woman who spent a career keeping confidences must now expose people, against every instinct she has.

**Why she stays nosy.**  She is new enough to be an outsider who notices what locals have stopped seeing, but she has sunk her savings and her hopes into this town — so she is invested, not passing through. The classic insider-outsider.

## Character Notes

Maggie is warm, observant, a little raw from the divorce, and quietly funny — the kind of dry that surprises people. She is competent with her hands and uncertain about her new life. She did not come to Pelican's Crook to solve murders; she came to be left alone with her clay, which is exactly why the town keeps pulling her in.

### Who she is underneath — the emotional core

At heart, Maggie is an artist. The pottery is not a quirky occupation; it is a passion she set aside long ago — for a marriage, a career, a sensible life — and is only now returning to. The HR years were the detour; the clay is the homecoming. The Wheelhouse is where she is rebuilding herself, not merely where she sells mugs.

The divorce — the end of a stale marriage — is painful, but it is also the thing that frees her. The marriage went stale because she had stopped looking honestly at her own life; she had settled, and settling meant no longer seeing clearly. Now she is a woman trying to recover, to find herself again, and to find a place where she belongs.

And here is the quiet rhyme that makes the gift hers and no one else’s: the Too-Much is the literal version of her emotional journey. She is learning to see clearly — to perceive what is truly there rather than what she would prefer, or what she had settled for. Her recovery and her gift are the same journey in two costumes: a woman who stopped seeing her own life honestly, learning to see again. Coming to terms with the gift and coming to terms with herself are one act.

### The arc across the series

Four threads, but really one: recovering from the stale marriage, finding herself, coming to terms with her abilities, and finding a place. She moves from a woman hiding in a coastal town and apologising for taking up space — to someone who has reclaimed her art, found a community and a love, made a home in Pelican’s Crook, and learned to wield her strange gift instead of fearing it. The murders are the crucible that forces the seeing.

### The gift as a vice — bingeing and the price

A crucial flaw, and a more human one than ‘involuntary power’: Maggie can misuse the gift. Lonely and desperate to belong, she sometimes leans into the perception — even courts it — because being the woman who notices things, who remembers the detail, who says the uncannily perceptive thing, is social currency. It makes her interesting; it gets her seen and wanted in a town that hasn’t accepted her yet. At a community gathering she overindulges, performing her sharpness for approval. The high is real in the moment. The price comes due the next day: the migraine flattens her, and she lies in a darkened room with the pleasure curdled into shame and pain. A binge, and the hangover.

The analogy earns its place because it rhymes with who she is. Using the gift to be liked is the same move as staying in a stale marriage to feel safe — reaching for external validation instead of sitting with herself — the same external validation Tommy trained her to crave by withholding it. So the binge is a character flaw in action, not just a power beat: part of her arc is learning that the gift, like the marriage, cannot be the thing that makes her feel worthy. There is restraint to learn.

It pays off in plot, too. If she has been performing her perceptiveness for approval, the town already knows her as ‘uncanny Maggie who notices everything’ — which makes her later, real insights both more credible to some and easier for others to wave off as her party trick. And it sets up a painful irony: the one time it truly matters, at Neil’s death, the gift comes unbidden, and she would give anything not to have seen.

More than a job; it is the charm engine. A shopfront means townsfolk wander in to browse, commission, complain, and gossip — funnelling the whole community past Maggie's bench. The wheel, the kiln, the glazes give you tactile, sensory writing and a rich vein of metaphor (firing, cracking, glaze that only reveals its true colour after the heat). The kiln also makes a handy, homely place for clues, conversations, and the occasional discovery.

# 3.  The Gift — The Too-Much

This is the heart of the series and its most original element. Treat its rules as load-bearing: keep them firm and consistent, and the gift feels fresh and fair. Let them go soft, and it reads like the heroine simply knows whatever the plot needs.

## How It Works

**The trigger.**  It comes with her migraines — the same painful headaches she has had all her life. She does not choose it; the pain brings it.

**The effect.**  During an episode, the world becomes overwhelmingly detailed. She perceives everything — textures, asymmetries, a wet hem, a wrong shadow, a smell out of place — in punishing clarity. It is a flood, not a focus.

**The cost.**  Cognitive overload. The same flood that records the clue also incapacitates her — nausea, pain, sensitivity to light and sound. The gift and the suffering are the same event. She cannot use it freely without paying for it.

**The crucial limit.**  She is not Sherlock Holmes. She has no encyclopaedic knowledge, draws no instant deductions. She records the detail but cannot interpret it. Meaning arrives only later, often when she is pain-free and doing something mundane, when a second, ordinary thing connects to the stored detail and it clicks.

**The comedy.**  Because she perceives without understanding, she blurts. She voices a raw observation — “your sleeves are soaked”, “that is not your handwriting” — with no idea what it means or who it implicates, causing faux pas, false accusations, and mortification. Her gift embarrasses her as often as it helps her.

## The Camera Metaphor (for your reference)

Think of Maggie as a camera that captures everything but cannot develop the film in the moment. The migraine takes the photograph — every detail, perfectly recorded. The clue is captured early. But the picture only develops later, when an unrelated moment supplies the missing chemical and the image resolves into meaning. This is why the gift never feels like cheating: the reader sees the same detail Maggie does, planted in plain sight, and gets the same delayed click.

## Rules to Keep Consistent

1. She cannot summon it at will. It rides in on the migraines, uninvited.
1. It records; it does not explain. No instant deductions, ever.
1. Interpretation needs a second trigger — a later, ordinary detail that connects.
1. It always costs her. Every use carries pain and overload.
1. She is only beginning to understand what it is. Mastery is a series-long arc, not a book-one switch.

## Who Knows

At the start, no one — not even Maggie, fully. She thinks she is just observant, or unlucky with her health. Part of book one is her dawning realisation of what these episodes actually are. A natural series thread: deciding whom to trust with the secret, and discovering that one old local may have seen this gift before.

## Rendering the Gift on the Page

The gift only sings if it is dramatised, not narrated. A set of techniques for showing it, so it reads as cinematic, involuntary, and costly rather than as convenient.

### The onset — the migraine as weather

Don’t open with ‘she got a headache.’ Show the aura: edges shimmer or fracture, sound sharpens to something painful, a smell turns unbearable, light grows teeth. The reader learns to dread — and lean into — the onset, because they know detail is coming. A recurring physical tell (a taste of metal, a ringing, one watering eye) lets you signal an episode in three words later in the series.

### The flood — overload, not a clue list

When it hits, resist ‘she noticed X, Y and Z.’ Drown her instead: everything screams for attention at the same volume — a wrong-buttoned cuff, dust on one shoe, a bead of wax, a second dent in a cushion — none of it ranked, none of it chosen. The gift is a curse of too much; meaning must be excavated later. Write her fighting to stay upright in a tide of specificity.

### The delayed click — staged in a mundane moment

The payoff lands hardest when she is pain-free and doing something ordinary: wedging clay, watching Glaze knock a cup off a shelf, drying a mug. The stored detail collides with the present one and develops like a photograph. Write the click as physical — a jolt, a dropped tool, the room tilting — so the reader feels the understanding arrive. The gap between seeing and knowing is the suspense engine.

### The faux-pas blurt — comedy and danger from one mechanism

Because she perceives without understanding, she says the quiet part aloud at the worst moment: ‘your sleeves are soaked,’ ‘that’s not the shirt you had on yesterday.’ She has no idea why she said it or what it means, and watches the listener’s face change. Played for comedy, it’s mortifying; played for tension, she has just told a killer she sees too much. Same tool, two temperatures.

### The cost — made visible

Let the gift wreck her so the reader respects it. She loses the next day to a darkened room; she can’t drive home; she misses a date with Cal because an episode flattens her. Crucially, it sometimes strikes at the worst possible time — mid-confrontation, mid-escape — so the very thing that lets her see also blinds and incapacitates her. The gift as liability, not just asset.

### Clay as the externalised gift

Her craft can be the vehicle. Her hands rebuild a detail before her conscious mind catches up — the angle of a chair, the position of a cup — and she looks down to find she has thrown the answer on the wheel. Or she glazes obsessively and only later sees she has reproduced a colour she saw at the scene. The artist’s hands know before she does. Unique to her being a potter — worth mining hard.

### The unreliable flood — a mis-filed detail

For a strong red-herring beat: she is certain she saw something, swears by it, builds a theory — and she’s wrong, because she recorded it accurately but read the context falsely. It humbles the gift, keeps it from being a cheat code, and makes the true click later more earned.

### Sensory cross-wiring at the peak

At maximum overload, let perception bleed: she ‘sees’ the cold of a room, ‘tastes’ the silence, a sound has a texture. Slightly synaesthetic, slightly frightening — it makes the episodes feel genuinely altered rather than merely ‘very observant.’ Use sparingly, at the highest-stakes moments.

### The binge — using it to belong (see Character Notes)

The maladaptive use: lonely and wanting acceptance, she performs her perceptiveness as a party trick for social approval, overindulges at a gathering, and pays with a brutal migraine the next day. A binge and its hangover — the gift as a vice, mirroring her larger arc of learning self-worth. Detailed in the Character Notes; placed at the Act I community set-piece.

# 4.  The Town — Pelican’s Crook

A small fictional town on the Great Ocean Road, tucked between Lorne and Apollo Bay. Fictional by design, so geography bends to the story. The name carries a quiet triple meaning: a “crook” is a bend in the coastline; in Australian slang “crook” means unwell (echoing Maggie’s migraines); and a crook is a criminal. Three meanings, one cosy-mystery town.

## Geography

The town sits in its namesake ‘crook’ — a sheltered bend of coast between Lorne and Apollo Bay, where a headland curls around to make a bay calm enough for a jetty. Crook Creek runs down from the hills behind town and meets the sea at the bay; that freshwater landing is the reason a settlement took hold here at all. The town is hemmed in: ocean and cliffs on one side, steep bush-clad hills (the foot of the Otway ranges) rising on the other, so it can only spread along a thin coastal shelf — one main street running parallel to the water, with a few short streets climbing the slope behind it.

This geography is a plotting asset. It is a place you cannot easily slip into or out of unseen; the bush behind town swallows things and people; and the single road in is cut by floods and landslips each winter, which can strand the whole town — and any killer in it — exactly when the story needs isolation. The headland holds the lighthouse; the old cemetery sits up the hill; the beach is wide, dramatic, and often empty off-season.

## History

The shipwreck past is the bedrock, and it is morally grey on purpose. This stretch of coast was lethal to sailing ships, and Pelican’s Crook grew as a salvage-and-rescue community — part lifesavers, part wreckers. The ambiguity is deliberate fuel: did the founding families heroically rescue survivors, or quietly profit from what washed ashore? A respectable old-money family whose Victorian fortune may have come from plundered cargo is a renewable well of series secrets.

After the wrecking era came a quieter century: timber felled in the Otways and floated out through the bay, a fishing fleet, then a brief genteel guesthouse age when sea-bathing became fashionable and city families summered here. Decline followed as the timber and the fish thinned. The modern chapter is the Great Ocean Road itself — built by returned soldiers after the First World War — which turned the coast into a tourist route and gave the town its present boom-and-bust seasonal economy. Layers of history (wreckers, timber, fishing, tourism) mean layers of old grievance, ownership disputes, and faded fortunes to mine.

## Demographics

A few hundred permanent residents — the off-season town — swelling to several thousand each summer with tourists and holiday-house owners. That swing builds the central social tensions in: locals versus blow-ins, old salvage-and-timber families versus moneyed tree-changers, year-round workers versus absentee weekenders who own half the good real estate and use it six weeks a year.

The permanent population skews older (retirees, multi-generation locals) with a thinning middle of working families squeezed by holiday-home prices, and a small, restless cohort of young people who mostly leave for the city. A handful of recent arrivals — Maggie, Saffron, the wellness set — are reinventing themselves here, which the established families regard with suspicion. Faultlines worth keeping live: who is ‘from here’ and who never will be; who owns the waterfront; whose family did what during the wrecking years; and the quiet resentment of a town that depends on tourists it doesn’t much like.

## Rhythm of the Town

In summer the population swells with tourists; in the off-season it shrinks to a few hundred locals who all know one another's business. Maggie arrives in autumn, as the town empties and the real community closes ranks — the perfect moment for an outsider to start learning who everyone really is, and the perfect pressure-cooker for a murder, because the suspect pool is small, local, and tangled.

## Key Locations

1. **The Wheelhouse —** Maggie’s pottery studio and shopfront on the main street. Home base.
1. **The bakery —** warm, floury heart of the town gossip network (see Faye, below).
1. **The op-shop / general store —** run by the sisters; the town’s unofficial surveillance HQ.
1. **The pub —** where the town drinks, argues, and accidentally confesses.
1. **The lighthouse & the wreck-strewn coast —** atmosphere, history, and a bottomless well of old local secrets.
1. **The old cemetery —** graves of unidentified shipwrecked sailors; the town’s long memory made literal.
1. **The wellness retreat —** the too-fancy newcomer business the locals resent (see Saffron, below).

# 5.  The Recurring Cast

These are your series regulars — the people readers come back for. Names are placeholders; change any that don’t sing. Each one carries a built-in friction or secret, because in a cosy every beloved local must also be a plausible suspect.

## Cal — the love interest (non-cop local, slow-burn)

**Role.**  Series romance, kept deliberately separate from the investigation — and, in book one, unknowingly tied to the killer.

A local carpenter — another maker who works with his hands, which quietly rhymes with Maggie’s pottery. He fits out the shelving in The Wheelhouse in chapter one, their first crossed wires. Because he is not the law, the romance never gets tangled in procedure, which keeps it cosy and lets the relationship breathe. He is steady where Maggie is raw, patient with her strange episodes before he understands them, and quietly the first person to take her seriously since Dr Hartigan. The book-one complication: the killer is his own paternal aunt, which puts the truth Maggie is chasing on a collision course with the family she is beginning to hope she might belong to.

**Hook.**  His aunt is the book-one killer (tragically mistaken). The romance survives but is wounded — fuel for the slow burn.

## Faye — the jam-feud baker

**Role.**  Best friend & exposition engine. Warm, gossipy, lovable.

Runs the bakery and knows everyone’s business going back three generations. She becomes Maggie’s guide to the town’s hidden map of feuds and history — the natural, lovable way to deliver exposition. Her own running storyline: she has entered the same agricultural-show jam competition for 22 years and has never once won, locked in a slow, simmering feud with the judge. Ferociously competitive about that one thing; soft as warm dough about everything else.

**Hook.**  Her jam feud is a recurring comic B-plot — and a useful red herring (a 22-year grudge is a motive, isn’t it?).

## The Op-Shop Sisters — Dot & Glad

**Role.**  The town’s unofficial surveillance network. Comic duo.

Two elderly sisters who run the op-shop / general store, finish each other’s sentences, and miss nothing that happens on the main street. They are the closest thing the town has to CCTV — invaluable to a sleuth. The catch: they disagree bitterly about one ancient event no one else even remembers, and their accounts of any given afternoon never quite match, so Maggie must referee two unreliable narrators to get one usable fact.

**Hook.**  Great for fair-play clues delivered through comic, contradictory testimony.

## Cobber — the dawn surf-bum

**Role.**  Witness-at-large. Beach philosopher.

A man in his fifties who has been “about to leave town” for thirty years. He is on the beach every dawn, so he sees who comes and goes before the town is awake — making him an accidental witness to things that happen at the edges of the day. Speaks in unhurried surf-philosophy; knows more than he realises he knows.

**Hook.**  Perfect source of a clue Maggie can only use once a later detail explains it — mirrors her own gift.

## Saffron — the wellness-retreat blow-in

**Role.**  Resented newcomer. Suspect-shaped, secretly fragile.

Glamorous tree-changer running a too-fancy wellness and yoga retreat the locals resent — all green juice, crystals, and unsolicited energy-readings. The town’s favourite person to blame. Beneath the serenity she is quietly broke and rather desperate, which gives her motive, vulnerability, and — once you scratch the surface — unexpected sympathy. A useful mirror for Maggie: another woman who came here to reinvent herself.

**Hook.**  Ideal recurring red herring; also a possible reluctant ally once the gloss cracks.

## Beryl — the CWA matriarch

**Role.**  Social gatekeeper. Comic antagonist (affectionate).

Runs the town’s social calendar with an iron fist and a clipboard, and Did Not Approve of the newcomer. Every fundraiser, market, and funeral runs through her. She is the institutional power of the town — to be accepted in Pelican’s Crook is to be accepted by Beryl. Across the series she thaws from obstacle to fierce, prickly ally.

**Hook.**  Her events give you the warm community set-pieces every book needs — and a stage for Maggie’s gift to misfire publicly.

## Sergeant Rick Pruitt — the local cop

**Role.**  Friction & collaboration. The law at street level.

Competent but stretched thin, the only real police presence for miles. Sceptical of Maggie’s “hunches” — understandably, since she can never explain how she knows things (“I just… noticed” is not evidence). Their clash-and-collaborate dynamic is a core engine: he needs what she sees, she needs his authority to act on it, and neither fully trusts the other yet. Not the romance — that’s deliberate, so the friction stays clean.

**Hook.**  His scepticism keeps the gift honest: Maggie can’t just announce the solution, she has to make it provable.

## Vincent Calloway — the retired magistrate

**Role.**  The law at the level of the bench. Keeper of old judgements.

A retired magistrate who still carries the authority of decades on the bench. Where the Sergeant handles the present, the Magistrate embodies the town’s legal past — old cases, old rulings, old grudges from people once sentenced or once let off. A source of buried history, moral weight, and quiet menace. Knows where a great many bodies are buried, figuratively (and given the cemetery, perhaps not only figuratively).

**Hook.**  A walking archive of motive: anyone the Magistrate ever judged has a reason to remember.

## Artie Selwood — the elder, retired lighthouse keeper

**Role.**  Living memory. The one who recognises the gift.

The retired keeper of the lighthouse, full of embellished shipwreck history he’ll narrate to anyone he can corner — but underneath the tall tales, he genuinely knows where every secret in this town is kept. Crucially, he may be the one person who recognises what Maggie’s episodes really are, because he saw the same thing once before, long ago. A key to the series-long mystery of the gift’s origin.

**Hook.**  Slow-drip his recognition of Maggie’s gift across several books.

## Glaze — the studio cat

**Role.**  Cosy mascot. Emotional anchor.

A scruffy, clay-dusted ginger tom who decided The Wheelhouse is home and Maggie is staff. Obligatory cosy companion, comic relief, and a soft place for Maggie (and the reader) to land after the hard scenes. Occasionally, conveniently, draws attention to the right corner of a room.

**Hook.**  Named for the glaze he’s permanently dusted in; considers the kiln his personal heater.

# 6.  Book One — The Victim

## Dr Neil Hartigan — the town GP

**The name.**  Dr Neil Hartigan — a male GP of long standing. “Hartigan” carries the warm, trustworthy weight of a doctor who delivered half the town and sat with the other half as they died. (Alternates if you want them: Dr Sam or Dr Jim Hartigan.)

**Role in the story.**  A beloved local pillar, introduced warm and alive in the opening chapters so the reader feels the loss — then murdered, detonating the plot of book one.

### Why the GP is the perfect victim

A small-town doctor is the ultimate hub. Everyone is a patient. Over thirty years the GP has been in the room for every birth, death, diagnosis, and shameful late-night visit — and therefore knows who is really the father, who is drinking again, who got which result, who came in with injuries they lied about. Killing the doctor means killing the one person who knew everybody’s hidden life. The suspect pool is, quite literally, the whole town, and the motives write themselves.

It also harmonises with the series’ spine: a story about a woman whose gift arrives through illness, set in a town whose name means unwell. A doctor at the dead centre of book one ties medicine, bodies, and the things we hide about our health into a single thematic knot.

### The hidden crack — the Mercy (locked)

Neil was genuinely good — and carried one secret that compromised him. Years ago, a beloved town elder lay dying in prolonged agony and begged Neil, repeatedly, to help him go. Neil did — quietly, lovingly — and signed the certificate kindly. It was the clearest kind of mercy: he did not decide who should die, he answered a desperate, repeated plea. Most of the family grieved what they understood to be a peaceful natural death. Neil carried it for years, and it became the root of his lifelong gentleness toward suffering.

The investigation reveals his character in three movements:

1. Saint. The town mourns a beloved healer; grief is universal and uncomplicated.
1. Sinner. Maggie uncovers evidence that Neil hastened a death. Sympathy wobbles — even Maggie wonders if he was who she thought.
1. Person. The fuller truth lands: it was a begged-for mercy, the most loving thing anyone did for the dying man. Saint, then sinner, then person — and the town’s grief re-complicates.

### The personal connection to Maggie — the series’ secret engine

The GP took Maggie’s migraines seriously — likely the first person ever to. He may have begun to suspect what her episodes truly were, and what they might mean. Then he died, possibly before finishing what they were going to tell her.

This does enormous work. It gives Maggie a private grief and a personal stake far past nosiness — she is not a busybody, she is a patient who lost her doctor, which the Sergeant cannot easily dismiss. And it threads the victim into the series-long mystery of the gift’s origin: the one professional who might have helped Maggie understand herself is now gone, leaving a thread she keeps pulling on for books to come. The Elder may hold the other end of that thread.

# 6b.  Book One — The Mystery

Victim, killer, and motive are locked, all drawn from inside the town so the murder exposes the web of relationships we’ve built. What remains is the fine detail and the keystone clue. The summary: a beloved GP is killed by a grieving woman who has fatally misread an old act of mercy as murder — and the sleuth uncovering it is falling for the killer’s nephew.

## The Killer & Motive — the Tragic Mistake (locked)

**The killer.**  Cal’s aunt — his father’s sister, and daughter of the dying man Neil helped. She has lived in town her whole life and is avenging her own father. Grieving and sympathetic until the truth comes out.

**The love interest.**  Cal — a local carpenter (fits out Maggie’s studio shelving in chapter one; another maker who works with his hands). The slow-burn romance of the series. His family is the family at the centre of the murder; the dying elder was his grandfather.

### The motive

The aunt has carried a suspicion for years: she always believed her father’s death was no accident — that he was murdered. But for a decade she suspected the wrong person (a family rival, a carer, someone who stood to gain), nursing a cold certainty the town has long dismissed as her harmless fixation — ‘poor thing, she’s always had a bee in her bonnet about her dad.’

Then a discovery reveals the death was hastened after all — vindicating her lifelong suspicion — but the hand was Dr Neil Hartigan’s. The discovery names the method and the culprit, not the mere fact of foul play; she always had the fact, she lacked the name. Her theory is confirmed and inverted in the same instant: the beloved town doctor, the last man she would ever have suspected, did it. In her grief-warped logic she cannot see mercy — only that she was right all along, and the culprit was hiding in plain sight as a saint. Decades of suspicion curdle into a fury that feels, to her, entirely justified: ‘I always knew — and now I know who.’ She confronts Neil and kills him.

She is both right and catastrophically wrong. Right that her father’s death was no accident. Wrong about what it meant — he begged Neil to help him go. She has destroyed the one person who acted purely out of love.

### Why this motive is so strong

1. The killer hides in plain sight: the town knows the aunt as the eccentric with a grudge about an old death, so no one links that harmless fixation to the new murder.
1. She is sympathetic and a little frightening — not a sudden snap, but a decade-old wound finally given a target.
1. It gives Maggie a thread to pull: why would this particular woman care so much about a death from years ago?

### The collision with the romance — the book-one wound

This is the engine that can ache for volumes. Maggie is falling for Cal, and her gift leads her straight to his aunt. To solve Neil’s death — to clear the doctor’s name and prove it was mercy — Maggie must expose the woman as a killer, detonating the family she was beginning to hope she might belong to. The truth and the romance are on a collision course. Maggie isn’t unmasking a monster; she’s destroying a grieving family over a misunderstanding, and only her gift for seeing what is truly there can carry her to a truth no one else wants.

### Why this is the perfect machine for the series

Neil’s compassion is the single unbroken thread: the mercy that gets him killed is the same gentleness that made him take Maggie’s migraines seriously. The murder is intimate, mistaken, and rooted entirely in the town’s buried history. And the series’ thesis is dramatised in the plot itself — the town misjudged, the killer misjudged, and only Maggie, who sees what is actually there rather than what everyone assumes, can find the truth.

## The Keystone Clue — The Erasure (locked)

The detail Maggie’s gift photographs early and only understands late. Its design does double duty: it points to the killer’s intimacy with Neil (the mercy/history thread) and, ultimately, to the aunt herself. The elegance is that the clue is not a thing present but a thing erased — the killer cleaned up to hide that she was ever there, and a normal person sees only a tidy room. Maggie’s hyper-focus sees the seams of the cleaning.

### Stage one — what the migraine records (early, at the scene)

Drawn to Neil’s home soon after the death, Maggie is struck by a migraine and, in the flood, perceives not what is there but what is subtly wrong about a room smoothed over by someone hiding their presence: a chair squared a fraction too neatly while Neil’s own sits askew; a faint ring on the wood where a second cup stood, the cup washed and reshelved — but a fraction out of Neil’s habitual order, which Maggie, a maker of cups, would clock; fresh dishwashing at the wrong hour; a tea towel damp and refolded; the biscuit tin lid not quite seated. None of it means anything to her yet. She records only: the room is wrong, the room has been erased — and the pain swallows her before she can think.

The keystone within the keystone: the washed second cup was dried and returned with an unthinking, proprietary familiarity — the visitor knew this kitchen, knew where things lived, had sat here many times over many years. Not a stranger. Someone intimate with Neil and with the house. That single detail points away from an intruder and toward history and family — the mercy connection and the aunt, in one stroke.

### Stage two — what makes it click (late, pain-free, ordinary)

Much later, in a mundane moment — tidying after a CWA event, or washing up in Maggie’s studio or Cal’s kitchen — Maggie watches the aunt dry a cup and put it away with that same unthinking, proprietary familiarity, the exact gesture of someone who knows where everything belongs. The photograph develops. Maggie understands at last what her migraine recorded at Neil’s: not two cups, but the signature of a person who belonged in that kitchen, erasing herself. The click names the killer and confirms the visit was intimate, not violent — someone Neil welcomed, someone from the family, someone tied to the old death.

### Why it works

1. Fair play: the wrongness of the cleaned room is on the page early, dismissed as atmosphere. The gift smuggles in nothing; it only notices harder.
1. It requires Maggie specifically: a normal observer sees ‘tidy’; the Sergeant sees nothing actionable; only hyper-focus reads the seams of erasure.
1. It dramatises the series thesis: the truth is not in what is there, but in what someone tried to erase — and only Maggie sees it.
1. Comedy hook: Maggie can blurt a half-formed ‘someone washed up after’ at the worst moment and accuse the wrong tidy person (Beryl, who tidies everything) before the real click lands.

# 6c.  Book One — The Suspect Web

The principle for a charm-first cosy: make everyone a suspect. The whole town under gentle suspicion means the whole town gets exposed — which is the reader’s real pleasure. Each suspect carries a motive (why they could have done it), a secret (something they’re hiding that makes them look guilty — usually not the murder), and a demolition (the clean reason they’re cleared). Because Neil knew everyone’s secrets, nearly every secret orbits him, so clearing a suspect still deepens the town.

## The Double-Bluff — the aunt, suspected first and dismissed

The killer is hidden in plain sight by being suspected first — for the wrong reason. Early on, the town and Sergeant Pruitt glance at Cal’s aunt because she is the eccentric who has always gone on about her father’s death, and now there is a fresh body. But they attribute it to her general ‘instability’, not to the real motive — because no one yet knows the discovery exists that names Neil. She is cleared on the wrong grounds (‘poor thing, she’s harmless, she wouldn’t hurt a fly’) and crossed off.

This is the engine of the misdirection: once a suspect is raised and waved away, the reader mentally crosses them off and never looks back. She hides behind her own early exoneration. The clearing is true-but-irrelevant — no one ever tested the real motive — so when the erasure clicks at the climax, the reader realises she was never actually ruled out on the thing that mattered.

**Extra twist of the knife.**  Maggie herself, out of kindness, may help dismiss the aunt early — so at the end she must overturn her own exoneration.

## Saffron — the loud red herring (and the false solution)

**Motive.**  Neil quietly warned patients off her dubious treatments; she blamed him for scaring away clients and resented him openly.

**Secret.**  She’s not just broke — her retreat is unlicensed and she reinvented her past to escape something in the city.

**Demolition.**  Her alibi is humiliating: she was somewhere she didn’t want known (a creditor meeting / secret second job one town over). Once forced to admit it, she’s cleared.

**Note.**  The town’s favourite suspect, and the false solution that collapses at the Act II low point. Her secret turns her from villain into a possible reluctant ally — another woman who came here to reinvent herself, mirroring Maggie.

## Beryl — the ‘too obvious’ suspect

**Motive.**  Years of clashing with Neil over town power; she was overheard arguing with him days before his death.

**Secret.**  The argument was Neil gently refusing to bend something for her — or Beryl hiding a diagnosis Neil gave her that she’s terrified the town will learn. The iron matriarch is mortal.

**Demolition.**  Sympathetic secret, not a murderous one; and she was running an event in front of fifty witnesses. Thaws her toward ally.

**Note.**  Her need for control reads as guilt until the softer truth lands.

## Faye — the suspect Maggie doesn’t want to suspect

**Motive.**  Thin on the surface, but Neil knew something about Faye’s family, or about the long jam-feud judge.

**Secret.**  The 22-year jam feud hides a real old wound — a history between Faye and the judge that Neil happened to know.

**Demolition.**  Cleared warmly and a little comically; her secret is poignant, not criminal. Tests Maggie’s loyalties without breaking the friendship.

**Note.**  Useful for the emotional stakes of suspecting a friend.

## Vincent Calloway — the dark horse

**Motive.**  A retired magistrate and a doctor both keep the town’s secrets; perhaps Neil knew of an old judgement Calloway got wrong, or a favour that crossed a line. His gravitas makes him feel capable of it.

**Secret.**  Something genuinely buried in his years on the bench.

**Demolition.**  His secret is real but unconnected to Neil’s death — a separate skeleton.

**Note.**  SERIES SEED: leave his bench skeleton dangling for a future book. Do not resolve it here.

## Cobber — the accidental-witness wildcard

**Motive.**  Not really a suspect — but he was on the beach at dawn and saw someone near Neil’s.

**Secret.**  His vague, philosophical account points first the wrong way (toward Saffron or a stranger).

**Demolition.**  N/A — he’s a witness, not a suspect.

**Note.**  Much later his account becomes the corroboration that helps Maggie place the aunt. He mirrors Maggie’s gift: he saw the truth without understanding it.

## Series Seeds (use sparingly)

1. Calloway’s buried bench skeleton — unresolved, a thread for a later book.
1. Artie Selwood’s recognition of Maggie’s gift — drip-fed, the origin mystery of the Too-Much.
1. The Saffron friendship — a reinvented woman who may become an ally.

1. At least one comic faux pas where Maggie blurts a recorded detail and accuses the wrong person.
1. Sergeant Pruitt’s scepticism forces Maggie to make her insight provable, not just speak it.
1. A first meaningful step in the slow-burn romance.
1. One warm community set-piece (a Beryl-run event is the obvious candidate).
